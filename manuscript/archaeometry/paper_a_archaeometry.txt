ABSTRACT
A one-dimensional angular enrichment test is applied to the UNESCO World Heritage Cultural and Mixed corpus (𝑁 = 1011) using the 0.1-bēru interval (3°), the smallest integer subdivision of the Babylonian unit (MUL.APIN, c. 1000 BCE). Tier-A+ denotes
sites within ≤ 0.002 bēru (approximately 6.7 km) of a 0.1-bēru harmonic measured from Mount Gerizim (35.269°E). Domed monuments (𝑁 = 90) show significant enrichment at harmonic nodes (Bonferroni 𝑝adj = 0.003, 𝑘 = 3), and A+-bearing harmonics
host 4.19× as many sites as non-bearing harmonics (𝑝adj = 0.01). A phase coincidence limits findings to exploratory status. The
paper provides a transferable, reproducible template for testing angular units.

Introduction

This study applies a one-dimensional angular enrichment test to
the externally curated UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus (𝑁 =
1011), using a reproducible computational pipeline (see Data and
Code Availability, §A.2). The methodological approach employs
a geometric null with no free parameters and is intended as a
reusable methodological framework for spatial pattern-detection
studies in archaeological heritage data.
The corpus (𝑁 = 1011) appears to contain a global 3° periodic structure in its longitude distribution. Every longitude of
the form 𝑥.18°E produces the maximum observed Tier-A+ count
when used as an angular reference (sites within ≲ 6.7 km of
a harmonic node, against a 4% geometric null). The 3° spacing
equals the smallest integer sub-division of the Babylonian beru
unit (0.1 beru = 3° = 3 uš, MUL.APIN, c. 1000 BCE). Because
Gerizim’s longitude falls within the 𝑥.18°E optimal band, the Levantine corridor result is the motivated entry point for the study
rather than independent evidence; the full treatment is in §6.1.
Why this design addresses key methodological criticisms.
Prior metrological investigations were rightly criticized for

investigator-assembled site lists (Williamson & Bellamy 1983,
Freeman 1976, Kendall 1974). Three features distinguish this
study: (1) the UNESCO site list was compiled without reference
to this hypothesis and is fixed; (2) the 0.1-beru interval is documented in cuneiform sources (Hunger & Steele 2019) as the finest
integer subdivision, limiting the parameter space; (3) tier boundaries were fixed on geometric grounds and keywords are simple
morphological matches.
Positioning within quantitative archaeometry. This paper
belongs to the tradition of probability-based spatial analysis in
archaeometry (Magli 2013 2016, Ruggles 1999 2015, Silva 2020).
The fixed external corpus, geometric null, and publicly archived
pipeline respond to the standards articulated in those frameworks
and in the ICOMOS-IAU thematic study (Ruggles & Cotte 2010).
The template is reusable for any candidate angular unit and
externally curated corpus.
Structure. Section 2 gives background on the beru unit; §3 describes data and protocol; §§4–5 present results and robustness
checks; §6 catalogs limitations including the circularity treatment; §7 evaluates alternatives and concludes. The Appendix
contains the keyword audit.

2.1

Background
The Beru Unit

The beru (30° of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN cuneiform compendium (Hunger & Steele 2019, Watson & Horowitz 2011), dated
to the late second or early first millennium BCE. The unit divided
the ecliptic into twelve segments and served for both stellar and
terrestrial angular measurement; the terrestrial use is explicit in
MUL.APIN II ii 43–iii 15. The sub-division into 30 uš (1 uš = 1°)
is well attested (Hunger & Steele 2019, Robson 2008), and the
0.1-beru interval (3°) tested here corresponds to exactly 3 uš.
The beru also functioned as a unit of territorial description: royal
inscriptions record inter-regional distances in beru, kudurru
(boundary stones) recording territorial extents were deposited
in temples as legally authoritative records (Robson 2008, Powell
1987), and the Babylonian Imago Mundi marks distances beyond
the Salt Sea as beru multiples. Systematic meridian transit observation and eclipse timing, both attested in the cuneiform record
(Hunger & Steele 2019, Neugebauer 1975), are sufficient to derive inter-site angular separations at 1° resolution; the gnomon,
attested across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Indian traditions
(Neugebauer 1975, Shamasastry 1915), provides an independent
means of establishing true meridian. This combination of a fractional angular unit, temple-deposited territorial records, and attested observational infrastructure provides the documentary basis for testing whether a 0.1-beru harmonic spacing is detectable
in the longitude distribution of major monuments. This study
tests the statistical pattern only; mechanism is outside scope.

2.2

Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular
measures across large geographic scales has a long and contested
history. Thom’s “megalithic yard” (Thom 1967 1971 1964) stimulated statistical refinement (Kendall 1974) and methodological
critique (Freeman 1976). Powell’s treatment of Mesopotamian
metrology (Powell 1987) remains the standard reference. More recently, Magli (Magli 2013 2016) has applied Monte Carlo methods
to archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have
advanced probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims (Ruggles 1999 2015, Silva 2020). The ICOMOS-IAU
thematic study (Ruggles & Cotte 2010) provides institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage properties. Medieval cartographic traditions also employed directional
reference systems anchored to sacred geography, with Jerusalem
and Mecca serving as orienting meridians in mappamundi and
qibla charts (Harvey 1987), providing historical context for the
Levantine corridor hypothesis.
This study departs from prior work in three respects: the site
list is externally curated (UNESCO World Heritage), eliminating selection bias; the test is one-dimensional (longitude only),
yielding a well-defined geometric null (4%) with no free parameters; and the analysis is fully computational and publicly archived
(Tenenbaum 2026).

2.3

The Anchor: Mount Gerizim

Mount Gerizim (35.269°E) is the primary reference anchor because of its documented tabbur ha-aretz (“navel of the earth”)

role in Samaritan cosmographic tradition (Purvis 1968, MacDonald 1964, Crown 1989), analogous to Babylon’s meridian in
cuneiform astronomical records (Hunger & Steele 2019). As a
prominent mountain (881 m) in the central highlands, it would
have served as a natural survey reference point. No optimization
across alternative Levantine anchors was performed.
Gerizim’s tentative-list coordinate (ref. 5706, DMS E35 16 08) is
externally sourced and does not appear in the UNESCO inscribed
corpus (UNESCO World Heritage Centre, Tentative List ref. 5706,
State of Palestine, submitted 02 April 2012; archived at https://
whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/). Jerusalem (35.2317°E),
approximately 4.1 km east of Gerizim, is an inscribed corpus
member. It is self-excluded when used as anchor and produces
the same A+ count (§5.3). The enrichment belongs to the 4.1 km
corridor, not to the specific choice of anchor within it. Mecca
reaches only 𝑝 ≈ 0.057 (ns); Mt. Meru (Tanzania, 36.750°E)
reaches 𝑝 ≈ 0.305 (ns) and Mt. Kailash, the most cited geographic
candidate for the cosmological axis mundi, reaches 𝑝 ≈ 0.206
(ns). Neither alternative anchor approaches significance.

2.4

Interpretive Framework

This paper is a pattern-detection study, not a historical attribution. Three classes of explanation are evaluated throughout:
(1) Geographic concentration — major civilizations developed
in Eurasian longitude bands that align with 0.1-beru harmonics (the dedicated simulation in §4.1 shows this remains a viable first-level account); (2) Metrological transmission — a
3° angular reference system anchored near the Levant informed
major foundations (no mechanism is documented); (3) Unmodeled statistical artifact — systematic bias in UNESCO curation,
geocoding, or the longitude distribution produces the apparent
periodicity (an artifact account must additionally explain the
dome enrichment and cluster asymmetry). Adjudicating between
explanations 1 and 2 requires independent corpora.

Data and Methods
Corpus Construction

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset contains 1248 inscribed nominations across all categories. Two outcome-blind
filters yield the analysis corpus: (1) Natural-only sites are excluded (−235, retaining Cultural and Mixed); (2) entries lacking
geocoordinates are excluded (−2). The result is 𝑁 = 1011 sites
with valid longitudes.

3.2

Geodetic Framework

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at 35.269°E, from Palestine’s UNESCO tentative list (ref. 5706, 2012; see §2.3). Gerizim
is not on the inscribed World Heritage List and does not appear
in the test-site corpus. Jerusalem is also excluded in both sweeps
(§2.3).
For a site at longitude 𝜆, the beru-distance is 𝐵 = |𝜆 − 35.269|∕30
and the beru-deviation from the nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is 𝛿 =
|𝐵 − round(𝐵, 0.1)|. The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion. Kilometer labels (e.g. ≲ 6.7 km for Tier-A+) are equatorial

approximations computed as 𝛿×30×111 km/°, becoming smaller
at higher latitudes. This makes the angular criterion conservative
at high latitudes. A geodesic sensitivity check confirms that applying a strict physical threshold would increase the A+ count
to 70, strengthening all primary results. No latitude correction is
applied.
The geometric null for Tier-A+ is 2 × 0.002∕0.1 = 4%, assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference,
but the primary tests are simulation-based null models (§5.5)
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian
KDE produces 40.4 ± 6.2 expected A+ (∼4.0%), confirming the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.

3.3

Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing

The harmonic spacing tested is 0.1 beru (3°), the smallest integer sub-division of the beru attested in cuneiform metrology,
equalling exactly 3 uš (Hunger & Steele 2019, Robson 2008, Powell 1987). It was selected because it is the finest metrologically
attested integer sub-division that yields a tractable test (120 harmonics, 4% geometric null). Coarser spacings (0.5 beru, 1 beru)
are uninformative; finer sub-divisions (0.02 beru) do not produce
the signal (Table 7). No spacing was selected post-hoc.
The 0.05-beru spacing (6°) also reaches significance (Table 7).
This is structurally expected because every 0.10-beru harmonic is
also a 0.05-beru harmonic, making the 0.05-beru result a coarser
sub-sampling of the same periodicity, not a separate signal.

3.4

Tier Classification

Sites are classified by 𝛿, the angular deviation from the nearest
0.1-beru harmonic. All tier boundaries were fixed on metrological
grounds, not tuned to the data. Tier A++: 𝛿 ≤ 0.0002 beru (≲
0.67 km), 𝑃0 = 0.4%, descriptive only. Tier A+: 𝛿 ≤ 0.002 beru
(≲ 6.7 km), 𝑃0 = 4% (i.e. 2×0.002∕0.1), primary threshold. Tier A:
𝛿 ≤ 0.010 beru (≲ 33 km), 𝑃0 = 20%. Tier B: 𝛿 ≤ 0.050 beru.

6.1

Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment

How the circularity arose. This study began with the hypothesis that the Babylonian beru unit might be detectable as a
longitude-spacing signal in the UNESCO corpus, using Mount
Gerizim (35.269°E) as the reference anchor. Running the global
anchor sweep revealed that Gerizim’s longitude falls within the
𝑥.18°E phase band. Had the sweep revealed a different optimal phase, this Gerizim-anchored framing would not have been
adopted.

7.1

Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias

Geographic and cultural selection bias is the most important
alternative explanation. The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor (Europe:
∼44% of Cultural/Mixed sites; Arab States: ∼7%; Asia-Pacific:
∼26%; Americas: ∼13%; Africa: ∼9%). Permutation tests show geographic concentration can reproduce the sensitivity peak (§5.2).
The dome enrichment surviving the restricted geographic draw
(Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080, **) remains the primary evidence against a
purely geographic account.

Alternative Explanations

(1) Independent convergence. Hemispherical structures are
a recurring architectural solution, and convergent astronomical
orientations across unconnected cultures have been documented
(Aveni 2001 2003). However, independent convergence predicts
no consistent longitude specificity.
(2) Geographic concentration. This is the strongest alternative
and cannot be excluded on this corpus alone. Permutation tests
show geographic concentration can reproduce the sensitivitycollapse pattern (§5.2). However, the restricted geographic draw
(Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080, **) shows that other monuments from
the same longitude footprint are significantly less enriched than
dome sites.
(3) Random coincidence. Three simulation approaches fail
to reproduce the dome enrichment (permutation 𝑝 = 0.004;
bootstrap 𝑝 = 0.006).

Discussion

Conclusion

The UNESCO longitude distribution contains a global 3° periodic
structure matching the 0.1-beru unit. The dome sub-population
(Test 2, Bonferroni 𝑝adj = 0.003, 𝑘 = 3) survives all three simulation nulls, and A+-bearing harmonics host 4.19× more sites than
non-A+ harmonics (Test 3, 𝑝 = 0.0033).
The two findings most resistant to a purely geographic account
are morphological specificity (dome enrichment exceeding the
restricted geographic draw, Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080) and cluster
asymmetry (𝑝 = 0.0033). This paper documents the quantitative
regularity; its cause is an open question.

A.1
Dome/Spherical Monument Filter (Test 2 and
Test 2x)
7 morphological keywords from four roots (stupa/stupas, tholos, dome/domed/domes, spherical) are matched as whole words
against each site’s name, XML description, and extended OUV
text.
Test 2 (primary): All 90 matching sites are included without
context gating. This raw positive sweep uses a population fully determined by the keyword list, with no post-hoc filtering decisions.
The sweep is deliberately over-inclusive: it accepts sites where the
matched keyword refers to geological domes, vernacular housing,
or natural spherical features. The trade-off favors simplicity and
reproducibility at the cost of known false negatives in specificity.
Test 2x (context-validated robustness check): Unambiguous
keywords are accepted without validation. Ambiguous keywords
(dome, domed, domes, spherical) are subject to a two-gate context check: Gate 1 (28 negative patterns) rejects non-architectural
uses; Gate 2 (86 positive patterns) requires architectural confirmation. Full gate lists: lib/dome_filter.py. The gates reduce
false positives but cannot eliminate interpretive ambiguity at the
boundary between architectural and non-architectural uses.
Of the 90 raw-sweep sites, 7 are rejected by context validation
in Test 2x (Hiroshima Peace Memorial, Richtersveld, Gyeongju,
Viñales Valley, Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, Uluru-Kata Tjut.a,
and Diquís Stone Spheres). In every case, the exclusion is driven
by the semantic domain of the keyword match, not by any
property of the site’s longitude. Test 2x yields 𝑁 = 83, 𝑝 =
0.0005, 3.31× enrichment, confirming that the 7 rejected sites are
longitude-diluting false positives.

A.2

Founding-Origin Classifier (Test E, Post-Hoc)

The founding-origin classifier (lib/founding_filter.py) assigns sites to five categories: Founding Capital (F), Sacred Origin
(S), Founding Monument (M), Founding Axis (X), and Ancient
Continuous Landscape (L), using unambiguous and contextgated keywords. Full keyword lists and gate-logic patterns are in
keywords.json, the single source of truth for all keyword content in this project (see also lib/beru.py:load_keywords()).
This classifier was iteratively tuned after data inspection. The objective was to reduce false positives while accepting that keywordbased classification cannot fully eliminate interpretive ambiguity
at the boundary between founding-related and general heritage
descriptions. It carries no weight in the primary evidential chain
but addresses a substantive question: what kinds of sites, beyond
domed and stupa monuments, tend to fall on 𝑥.18°E nodes? The
classifier finds enrichment not only in morphologically domed
