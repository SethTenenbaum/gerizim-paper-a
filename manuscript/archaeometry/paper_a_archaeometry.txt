ABSTRACT
A one-dimensional angular enrichment test is applied to the UNESCO World Heritage Cultural and Mixed corpus (𝑁 = 1011)
using the 0.1-bēru interval (3°), the smallest integer subdivision of the Babylonian unit (MUL.APIN, c. 1000 BCE). Mount Gerizim
(35.269°E) serves as anchor on historical grounds (§6.1). Sites within ≤ 0.002 bēru (∼6.7 km) of a harmonic node are designated
Tier-A+. Domed monuments (𝑁 = 90) show significant A+ enrichment (Bonferroni 𝑝adj = 0.003, 𝑘 = 3), and cluster asymmetry
independently reaches significance (𝑝adj = 0.01). The anchor is historically motivated; the pipeline is fully reproducible.

1

Introduction

This study applies a one-dimensional angular enrichment test
to the externally curated UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus
(𝑁 = 1011), using a reproducible computational pipeline (see
Data and Code Availability, §A.2). The approach employs a geometric null with no free parameters and is intended as a reusable
framework for spatial pattern-detection studies in archaeological
heritage data.
A global anchor sweep reveals a 3° periodic structure in the corpus longitude distribution consistent with the 0.1-beru unit; the
full treatment and its methodological implications are in §5.3 and
§6.1.
Why this design addresses key methodological criticisms.
Prior metrological investigations were rightly criticized for
investigator-assembled site lists (Williamson & Bellamy 1983,
Freeman 1976, Kendall 1974). Three features distinguish this
study: (1) the UNESCO site list was compiled without reference
to this hypothesis and is fixed; (2) the 0.1-beru interval is documented in cuneiform sources (Hunger & Steele 2019) as the finest

integer subdivision, limiting the parameter space; (3) all subpopulations and results are fully computationally reproducible
from public source data (Tenenbaum 2026).
Positioning within quantitative archaeometry. This paper
belongs to the tradition of probability-based spatial analysis in
archaeometry (Magli 2013 2016, Ruggles 1999 2015, Silva 2020).

2.1

Background
The Beru Unit

The beru (30° of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN cuneiform compendium (Hunger & Steele 2019, Watson & Horowitz 2011), dated
to the late second or early first millennium BCE. The unit divided
the ecliptic into twelve segments and served for both stellar and
terrestrial angular measurement; the terrestrial use is explicit in
MUL.APIN II ii 43–iii 15. The sub-division into 30 uš (1 uš = 1°)
is well attested (Hunger & Steele 2019, Robson 2008), and the
0.1-beru interval (3°) tested here corresponds to exactly 3 uš.
The beru also functioned as a unit of territorial description: royal
inscriptions record inter-regional distances in beru, kudurru
1 of 12

(boundary stones) recording territorial extents were deposited
in temples as legally authoritative records (Robson 2008, Powell
1987), and the Babylonian Imago Mundi marks distances beyond
the Salt Sea as beru multiples. Systematic meridian transit observation and eclipse timing, both attested in the cuneiform record
(Hunger & Steele 2019, Neugebauer 1975), are sufficient to derive inter-site angular separations at 1° resolution; the gnomon,
attested across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Indian traditions
(Neugebauer 1975, Shamasastry 1915), provides an independent
means of establishing true meridian. This combination provides
the documentary basis for the hypothesis; the causal question of
precisely how or why this unit is applied is outside scope.

2.2

Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular
measures across large geographic scales has a long and contested
history. Thom’s “megalithic yard” (Thom 1967 1971 1964) stimulated statistical refinement (Kendall 1974) and methodological
critique (Freeman 1976). Powell’s treatment of Mesopotamian
metrology (Powell 1987) remains the standard reference. More recently, Magli (Magli 2013 2016) has applied Monte Carlo methods
to archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have
advanced probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims (Ruggles 1999 2015, Silva 2020). The ICOMOS-IAU
thematic study (Ruggles & Cotte 2010) provides institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage properties. Medieval cartographic traditions also employed directional
reference systems anchored to sacred geography, with Jerusalem
and Mecca serving as orienting meridians in mappamundi and
qibla charts (Harvey 1987), providing historical context for the
Levantine corridor hypothesis.
This study departs from prior work in three respects: the site
list is externally curated (UNESCO World Heritage), eliminating selection bias; the test is one-dimensional (longitude only),
yielding a well-defined geometric null (4%) with no free parameters; and the analysis is fully computational and publicly archived
(Tenenbaum 2026).

2.3

The Anchor: Mount Gerizim

Mount Gerizim (35.269°E) is the primary reference anchor because of its documented tabbur ha-aretz (“navel of the earth”)
role in Samaritan cosmographic tradition (Purvis 1968, MacDonald 1964, Crown 1989), analogous to Babylon’s meridian in
cuneiform astronomical records (Hunger & Steele 2019). As a
prominent mountain (881 m) in the central highlands, it would
have served as a natural survey reference point. No optimization
across alternative Levantine anchors was performed.
Gerizim’s tentative-list coordinate (ref. 5706, DMS E35 16 08) is
externally sourced and does not appear in the UNESCO inscribed
corpus (UNESCO World Heritage Centre, Tentative List ref. 5706,
State of Palestine, submitted 02 April 2012; archived at https://
whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/). Jerusalem (35.2317°E),
approximately 4.1 km east of Gerizim, is an inscribed corpus
member. It is self-excluded when used as anchor and produces
the same A+ count (§5.3). Both lie within the Levantine corridor
(∼35°E, Jordan Valley to Levant), and the enrichment belongs to
this corridor, not to the specific anchor within it. Mecca reaches

3.1

Data and Methods
Corpus Construction

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset contains 1248 inscribed nominations across all categories. Two outcome-blind
filters yield the analysis corpus: (1) Natural-only sites are excluded (−235, retaining Cultural and Mixed); (2) entries lacking
geocoordinates are excluded (−2). The result is 𝑁 = 1011 sites
with valid longitudes.

3.2

Geodetic Framework

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at 35.269°E, from Palestine’s UNESCO tentative list (ref. 5706, 2012; see §2.3). Gerizim is
not on the inscribed World Heritage List and therefore does not
appear in the standard corpus. Jerusalem is an inscribed corpus
member, scored as a regular site in Tests 1–4 (A+ at 4.1 km under
Gerizim). For the anchor-comparison audit and both sensitivity
sweeps (§5.3), Gerizim is added as a synthetic corpus entry so that
each anchor can self-exclude symmetrically: Gerizim removes itself and tests against the 𝑁 = 1011 inscribed sites (including
Jerusalem); Jerusalem removes itself and tests against the same
𝑁 (now including Gerizim).
For a site at longitude 𝜆, the beru-distance is 𝐵 = |𝜆 − 35.269|∕30
and the beru-deviation from the nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is 𝛿 =
|𝐵 − round(𝐵, 0.1)|. The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion. Kilometer labels (e.g. ≲ 6.7 km for Tier-A+) are equatorial
approximations computed as 𝛿×30×111 km/°, becoming smaller
at higher latitudes. This makes the angular criterion conservative
at high latitudes. A geodesic sensitivity check confirms that applying a strict physical threshold would increase the A+ count
to 70, strengthening all primary results. No latitude correction is
applied.
The geometric null for Tier-A+ is 2 × 0.002∕0.1 = 4%, assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference,
but the primary tests are simulation-based null models (§5.5)
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian
KDE produces 40.4 ± 6.2 expected A+ (∼4.0%), confirming the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.

3.3

Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing

The harmonic spacing tested is 0.1 beru (3°), the smallest integer sub-division of the beru attested in cuneiform metrology,
equalling exactly 3 uš (Hunger & Steele 2019, Robson 2008, Powell 1987). It was selected because it is the finest metrologically
attested integer sub-division that yields a tractable test (120 harmonics, 4% geometric null). Coarser spacings (0.5 beru, 1 beru)
are uninformative; finer sub-divisions (0.02 beru) do not produce
the signal (Table 6). No spacing was selected post-hoc.
The 0.05-beru spacing (6°) also reaches significance (Table 6).
This is structurally expected because every 0.10-beru harmonic is

4.4

Cluster Asymmetry (Test 3, Primary)

Full corpus (𝑁 = 1011), no keyword filter (see §3.5). For each site,
count other UNESCO sites within ±0.3° longitude; compare cluster sizes for A+ vs. non-A+ sites (Mann-Whitney 𝑈, permutation
test). At the harmonic level, compare per-harmonic site counts
for A+-bearing vs. non-A+ harmonics.
A+-bearing harmonics host 4.06× more sites on average than
non-A+ harmonics (𝑝 < 0.0001; Table 4). Sites at harmonic
nodes cluster more densely than sites at harmonic midpoints
(9.85 vs. 5.83 neighbors; 1.69×, 𝑝 = 0.0238).
Subtest: does clustering produce the unit-sensitivity peak?
A joint permutation test (50000 iterations, two independent shuffles per trial) asks whether extreme clustering mechanically produces the canonical-unit peak. Of 182 clustering-matched permutations (𝑝cluster = 0.0036), the conditional fraction producing the
peak is 0.1209, nearly identical to the unconditional rate of 0.1681.
The unit-sensitivity peak and the clustering signal are statistically
independent; the primary evidence remains the clustering (Test 3,
𝑝 = 0.0033) and dome enrichment (Test 2, 𝑝 = 0.004).

5.3

Global Anchor Sweep

A global sweep (0–360°E, 0.01° resolution, 36,000 trial anchors)

to Jerusalem, and 38 are shared (supplementary anchor comparison audit). No other historical meridian reaches significance

Limitations

6.1

Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment

The public-data constraint. Both the UNESCO coordinate file
and the candidate anchor (Mount Gerizim) are fully public. Any
investigator could run this pipeline at any time, before or after seeing these results. Pre-registration cannot verify temporal priority
for statistical analyses of public databases: the data are permanently accessible, so any registration claim is unverifiable. The
relevant question is whether the reported pattern exists in the
data and whether the anchor choice was justified independently
of the result. Both are addressed directly.
What the anchor sweep establishes. The global anchor sweep
(§5.3) tests all 120 possible 𝑥.18°E anchors against the same corpus. Gerizim and Jerusalem both place the ∼35°E corridor in the
global top 2.8% of all trial anchors. The enrichment is a property
of the corridor, not of the specific anchor chosen within it. The
choice of Gerizim was made on documented historical grounds
(§2.3); that its longitude falls within the optimal phase band is an
empirical observation, not a post-hoc rationalization.
What remains genuinely contaminated. The narrative framing of Gerizim as a historically motivated anchor is post-hoc
in the sense that reporting it was conditioned on observing the
phase alignment. The Rayleigh Test B is tautological by construction (§4.4). These are noted for completeness.
What is not contaminated. The 3° periodic structure in the UNESCO longitude file is recoverable by any unanchored 120-phase
scan without reference to Gerizim. The dome enrichment (Test 2)
is identical across all 120 𝑥.18°E anchors. The cluster asymmetry
(Test 3) requires no keyword filter and no anchor.
Accordingly, the Levantine corridor statistics are reported as the
motivated entry point rather than independent confirmation, and
the Bonferroni correction is applied as a descriptive bound on
effect magnitude.

10 of 12

6.2

Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias

Geographic and cultural selection bias is the most important
alternative explanation. The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor (Europe &
North America: 50% of Cultural/Mixed sites; Arab States: 9%;
Asia-Pacific: 23%; Americas: 11%; Africa: 7%). Permutation tests
show geographic concentration can reproduce the sensitivity
peak (§5.2). The dome enrichment surviving the restricted geographic draw (Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080, **) remains the primary
evidence against a purely geographic account.

6.3

Americas Negative Control

The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus (𝑁 = 145) shows directional
depletion (A+ = 2.1%, 𝑝 = 0.164, ns) relative to the 4% null,
consistent with a Eurasian-anchored grid predicting reduced harmonic occupancy in the western hemisphere.

6.4

Other Limitations

Spatial autocorrelation. DEFF correction: 𝑁ef f = 772 (DEFF
= 1.310, corrected 𝑝 = 0.020). Block bootstrap CI [4.04%, 6.87%]
excludes the 4% null.
Geocoding precision. UNESCO XML coordinates are administrative centroids, introducing unsystematic noise but not directional bias. Dome enrichment is robust across raw and contextvalidated populations and all three simulation nulls.
Metrological evidence. No physical surveying instrument calibrated in beru subdivisions has been recovered; documentary
sources attest the beru system (Hunger & Steele 2019, Robson
2008, Powell 1987). This study therefore treats the coordinate
alignment of Mount Gerizim as a novel empirical observation.

7.1

Discussion
Alternative Explanations

(1) Independent convergence. Hemispherical structures are
a recurring architectural solution, and convergent astronomical
orientations across unconnected cultures have been documented
(Aveni 2001 2003). However, independent convergence predicts
no consistent longitude specificity.
(2) Geographic concentration. This is the strongest alternative
and cannot be excluded on this corpus alone. Permutation tests
show geographic concentration can reproduce the sensitivitycollapse pattern (§5.2). However, the restricted geographic draw
(Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080, **) shows that other monuments from
the same longitude footprint are significantly less enriched than
dome sites.
(3) Random coincidence. Three simulation approaches fail
to reproduce the dome enrichment (permutation 𝑝 = 0.004;
bootstrap 𝑝 = 0.006).

7.2

Conclusion

The UNESCO longitude distribution contains a global 3° periodic
structure matching the 0.1-beru unit. The dome sub-population

Archaeometry, 2026

(Test 2, Bonferroni 𝑝adj = 0.003, 𝑘 = 3) survives all three simulation nulls, and A+-bearing harmonics host 4.06× more sites than
non-A+ harmonics (Test 3, 𝑝 = 0.0033).
The two findings most resistant to a purely geographic account
are morphological specificity (dome enrichment exceeding the
restricted geographic draw, Null C, 𝑝 = 0.0080) and cluster
asymmetry (𝑝 = 0.0033).

Replication and methodological transferability

This paper is a terminal analysis of the only available globally
comprehensive, institutionally curated monument corpus. The
pipeline is transferable to any corpus assembled prior to and
without reference to this hypothesis, provided it is sufficiently
selective. Named heritage registers, protected site inventories,
and operationally defined named lists all qualify. The Wikidata
Q180987 stupa result (§5.6) illustrates one such application; further applications to independent corpora will either accumulate
evidence or not.
The pipeline offers a general framework for testing other candidate angular units against any externally curated monument corpus; the null model and tier boundaries are fixed independently
of the data.

full gate lists are in lib/dome_filter.py. The seven rejected
sites are: Hiroshima Peace Memorial, Richtersveld, Gyeongju,
Viñales Valley, Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, Uluru-Kata Tjut.a,
and Diquís Stone Spheres. In every case the exclusion is driven
by the semantic domain of the keyword match, not by the site’s
longitude.
Test 2b adds earthen mound-stage keywords (tumulus, tumuli,
barrow, barrows, kofun unambiguous; mound context-validated)
to trace the hemispherical tradition from burial mound through
stupa to architectural dome. Test 2bx applies the same architectural context gates to the extended population. Statistics for all
four variants are reported in §4.2 and §4.3.

A.2

Founding-Origin Classifier (Test 5, Post-Hoc)

The founding-origin classifier (lib/founding_filter.py) assigns sites to five categories: Founding Capital (F), Sacred Origin (S), Founding Monument (M), Founding Axis (X), and
Ancient Continuous Landscape (L). Each category has its
own unambiguous keyword set plus context-gated ambiguous terms; positive and negative gate patterns are defined
per category in keywords.json. The classifier was iteratively
tuned after data inspection to reduce false positives; residual interpretive ambiguity at the boundary between foundingrelated and general heritage descriptions is accepted. Statistics are reported in §4.6; the full site-by-site listing is in
supplementary/audit/founding_keyword_audit.txt.
