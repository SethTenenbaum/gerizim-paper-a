\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

This study applies a one-dimensional angular enrichment test to the
externally curated UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$),
using a reproducible computational pipeline (see Data and Code Availability,
\S\ref{sec:data_code}). The approach employs a geometric null with no free
parameters and is intended as a reusable framework for spatial pattern‑detection
studies in archaeological heritage data.

A global anchor sweep reveals a 3\textdegree{} periodic structure in the
corpus longitude distribution consistent with the 0.1-beru unit; the full
treatment and its methodological implications are in
\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global} and \S\ref{sec:limitations_circularity}.

\textbf{Why this design addresses key methodological criticisms.}
\label{sec:circularity}
Prior metrological investigations were rightly criticized for investigator-assembled
site lists \citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}.
Three features distinguish this study: (1)~the UNESCO site list was compiled
without reference to this hypothesis and is fixed; (2)~the 0.1-beru interval
is documented in cuneiform sources \citep{hunger2019mul} as the finest
integer subdivision, limiting the parameter space; (3)~all sub-populations 
and results are fully computationally reproducible from public source 
data \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.

\textbf{Positioning within quantitative archaeometry.}
This paper belongs to the tradition of probability-based spatial analysis in
archaeometry \citep{magli2013,magli2016,ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.

\section{Background}

\subsection{The Beru Unit}
\label{sec:beru}

The \textit{beru} (30\textdegree{} of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN
cuneiform compendium \citep{hunger2019mul,watson2011writing}, dated to the late
second or early first millennium~BCE. The unit divided the ecliptic into twelve
segments and served for both stellar and terrestrial angular measurement;
the terrestrial use is explicit in MUL.APIN~II~ii~43--iii~15. The sub-division
into 30~\textit{u\v{s}} (1~u\v{s}~$= 1$\textdegree{}) is well attested
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics}, and the 0.1-beru interval
(3\textdegree{}) tested here corresponds to exactly 3~\textit{u\v{s}}.

The beru also functioned as a unit of territorial description: royal
inscriptions record inter-regional distances in beru, \textit{kudurru}
(boundary stones) recording territorial extents were deposited in temples
as legally authoritative records \citep{robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological},
and the Babylonian \textit{Imago Mundi} marks distances beyond the Salt Sea
as beru multiples.
Systematic meridian transit observation and eclipse timing, both attested
in the cuneiform record \citep{hunger2019mul,neugebauer1975history}, are
sufficient to derive inter-site angular separations at 1\textdegree{} resolution;
the gnomon, attested across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Indian traditions
\citep{neugebauer1975history,shamasastry1915arthashastra}, provides an independent
means of establishing true meridian.
This combination provides the documentary basis for the hypothesis;
the causal question of precisely how or why this unit is applied is outside scope.

\subsection{Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice}

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular measures
across large geographic scales has a long and contested history.
Thom's ``megalithic yard'' \citep{thom1967,thom1971,thom1964}
stimulated statistical refinement \citep{kendall1974} and methodological
critique \citep{freeman1976}. Powell's treatment of Mesopotamian metrology
\citep{powell1987metrological} remains the standard reference. More recently,
Magli \citep{magli2013,magli2016} has applied Monte Carlo methods to
archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have advanced
probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims
\citep{ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.
The ICOMOS-IAU thematic study \citep{ruggles2010icomos} provides
institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage
properties. Medieval cartographic traditions also employed directional
reference systems anchored to sacred geography, with Jerusalem and Mecca
serving as orienting meridians in mappamundi and qibla charts
\citep{harvey1987mappamundi}, providing historical context for the
Levantine corridor hypothesis.

This study departs from prior work in three respects: the site list
is externally curated (UNESCO World Heritage), eliminating selection
bias; the test is one-dimensional (longitude only), yielding a
well-defined geometric null (4\%) with no free parameters; and
the analysis is fully computational and publicly archived \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.



\subsection{The Anchor: Mount Gerizim}
\label{sec:anchor}

Mount Gerizim (\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E) is the primary reference
anchor because of its documented \textit{tabbur ha-aretz} (``navel of the
earth'') role in Samaritan cosmographic tradition
\citep{purvis1968,macdonald1964,crown1989}, analogous to Babylon's meridian
in cuneiform astronomical records \citep{hunger2019mul}. As a prominent
mountain (881~m) in the central highlands, it would have served as a
natural survey reference point. No optimization
across alternative Levantine anchors was performed.

Gerizim's tentative-list coordinate (ref.~5706, DMS \GerizimLonDMS{}) is
externally sourced and does not appear in the UNESCO inscribed corpus
(UNESCO World Heritage Centre, Tentative List ref.~5706, State of Palestine,
submitted 02~April~2012; archived at
\url{https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/}).
Jerusalem (35.2317\textdegree{}E), approximately \GerizimJerusalemKm~km east
of Gerizim, is an inscribed corpus member. It is self-excluded when used as
anchor and produces the same A+ count (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}).
Both lie within the \textit{Levantine corridor} ($\sim$35\textdegree{}E,
Jordan Valley to Levant), and the enrichment belongs to this corridor,
not to the specific anchor within it.
Mecca reaches only $p \approx \pMeccaAnchor$ (ns);
Mt.\ Meru (Tanzania, 36.750\textdegree{}E) reaches $p \approx \pMeruAnchor$ (ns)
and Mt.\ Kailash, the most cited geographic candidate for the cosmological
axis mundi, reaches $p \approx \pKailashAnchor$ (ns).
Neither alternative anchor approaches significance.

\section{Data and Methods}

\subsection{Corpus Construction}
\label{sec:corpus}

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset contains $\NwhcTotal$ inscribed
nominations across all categories. Two outcome-blind filters yield the
analysis corpus: (1)~Natural-only sites are excluded ($-\NexcNatural$, retaining
Cultural and Mixed); (2)~entries lacking geocoordinates are excluded ($-\NexcNoCoord$).
The result is $N = \NclusterTotal$ sites with valid longitudes.

\subsection{Geodetic Framework}
\label{sec:geodetic}

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at \GerizimLon\textdegree{}E,
from Palestine's UNESCO tentative list (ref.~5706, 2012; see \S\ref{sec:anchor}).
Gerizim is not on the inscribed World Heritage List and therefore does not
appear in the standard corpus.
Jerusalem is an inscribed corpus member, scored as a regular site in
Tests~1--4 (A+ at 4.1~km under Gerizim).
For the anchor-comparison audit and both sensitivity sweeps
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}), Gerizim is added as a synthetic corpus
entry so that each anchor can self-exclude symmetrically: Gerizim removes
itself and tests against the $N = \NclusterTotal$ inscribed sites (including
Jerusalem); Jerusalem removes itself and tests against the same $N$ (now
including Gerizim).

For a site at longitude $\lambda$, the beru-distance is
$B = |\lambda - \GerizimLon| / 30$ and the beru-deviation from the
nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is
$\delta = |B - \operatorname{round}(B, 0.1)|$.
The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion.
Kilometer labels (e.g.\ $\lesssim 6.7$~km for Tier-A+) are equatorial
approximations computed as $\delta \times 30 \times 111$~km/\textdegree{},
becoming smaller at higher latitudes. This makes the angular criterion
\emph{conservative} at high latitudes. A geodesic sensitivity check confirms
that applying a strict physical threshold would increase the A+ count to
\GeodesicApPhysical{}, strengthening all primary results. No latitude
correction is applied.

The geometric null for Tier-A+ is $2 \times 0.002 / 0.1 = 4\%$,
assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference, but
the primary tests are simulation-based null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null})
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian KDE produces
$\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$ expected A+ ($\sim$4.0\%), confirming the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.

\subsection{Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing}
\label{sec:metrology_basis}

The harmonic spacing tested is 0.1~beru (3\textdegree{}), the smallest integer
sub-division of the beru attested in cuneiform metrology, equalling exactly
3~\textit{u\v{s}} \citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
It was selected because it is the finest metrologically attested integer
sub-division that yields a tractable test (\NharmonicsCoarse{} harmonics, \NullRateAp{} geometric null).
Coarser spacings (0.5~beru, 1~beru) are uninformative; finer sub-divisions
(0.02~beru) do not produce the signal (Table~\ref{tab:unitsweep}).
No spacing was selected post-hoc.

The 0.05-beru spacing (6\textdegree{}) also reaches significance
(Table~\ref{tab:unitsweep}). This is structurally expected because every
0.10-beru harmonic is also a 0.05-beru harmonic, making the 0.05-beru
result a coarser sub-sampling of the same periodicity, not a separate signal.

\subsection{Tier Classification}

Sites are classified by $\delta$, the angular deviation from the
nearest 0.1-beru harmonic. All tier boundaries were fixed on metrological
grounds, not tuned to the data.
\textbf{Tier~A++}: $\delta \leq 0.0002$~beru ($\lesssim 0.67$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$, descriptive only.
\textbf{Tier~A+}: $\delta \leq 0.002$~beru ($\lesssim 6.7$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$ (i.e.\ $2 \times 0.002/0.1$), primary threshold.
\textbf{Tier~A}: $\delta \leq 0.010$~beru ($\lesssim 33$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
\textbf{Tier~B}: middle zone — neither near a harmonic nor near a midpoint.
The C-band is the symmetric mirror of the A-band, measured as
$\delta_\mathrm{mid} = 0.050 - \delta$ (distance from the nearest
inter-harmonic midpoint rather than from the nearest harmonic):
\textbf{Tier~C}: $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq 0.010$~beru ($\lesssim 33$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
\textbf{Tier~C$-$}: $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq 0.002$~beru ($\lesssim 6.7$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$.
\textbf{Tier~C$--$}: $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq 0.0002$~beru ($\lesssim 0.67$~km), $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$, descriptive only.
C-band tiers are defined here; a full inter-harmonic site listing is in
\texttt{supplementary/audit/interharmonic\_audit.txt}.

\subsection{Test Populations}
\label{sec:test_populations}

Keyword populations are approximate; the raw sweep tolerates known
false positives while the context-validated variant (Test~2x) applies
sentence-level gating for greater precision.
Both populations are reported.

\textbf{Test~1 (full corpus, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; binomial test of
whether the global A+ rate exceeds the 4\% geometric null.

\textbf{Test~2 (dome/spherical, primary).}
\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text ($N = \NcircTotal$).
This raw sweep is over-inclusive: it admits sites where the keyword
refers to geological or vernacular features rather than monumental
architecture. The context-validated population ($N = \NcircValidated$)
is reported alongside all primary results; it yields stronger
significance, consistent with the rejected sites being
non-architectural false positives.

\textbf{Test~2b (mound evolution, sensitivity variant).}
Extends Test~2 by adding earthen mound-stage keywords
(\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun}).
Not counted separately in the Bonferroni family.

\textbf{Test~3 (cluster asymmetry, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; tests whether
A+ harmonics host more total sites than non-A+ harmonics, independent
of any morphological keyword.

\textbf{Test~4 (temporal gradient, descriptive).}
Cochran-Armitage trend test on the full corpus based on year of
inscription into the UNESCO corpus, no keyword filter.

\textbf{Test~5 (founding classifier, post-hoc).}
A context-aware keyword classifier
(\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py}) refined after initial data inspection
to reduce false positives in population construction, with residual
interpretive ambiguity and some false positives accepted
(Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
It addresses what kinds of sites beyond domed
monuments tend to fall on harmonic nodes, but carries no weight
in the primary evidential chain.

\subsection{Multiple-Comparisons Protocol}
\label{sec:bonferroni}

\textbf{Primary test family ($k = \BonfK$).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Test 1} (full corpus, $N = \NclusterTotal$):
    global enrichment above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 2} (domed/spherical monuments, $N = \NcircTotal$):
    morphological sub-population enriched above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 3} (cluster asymmetry, full corpus, no keyword filter):
    A+-bearing harmonics host more total sites.
\end{itemize}
Tests~2 and~3 address structurally different questions on different
populations and carry the primary evidential weight; Test~1 provides the
corpus-level backdrop within the same family.
All three pass the Bonferroni bound at $k = \BonfK$
(full corpus $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$~*,
dome $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$~***,
cluster $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestThree$~**).

\textbf{Other tests.}
Test~4 (temporal gradient, Cochran-Armitage) is descriptive context;
the trend is substantially explained by regional composition
(\S\ref{sec:resultstemporal}) and is not part of the primary family.
Test~2b is a sensitivity variant of Test~2.
Test~5 (founding classifier) is post-hoc (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
Test~B (Rayleigh, \S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) is a structural
description of the A+ phase-lock that is tautological by construction
and is not included in either table.

Results of all primary and secondary tests are consolidated
in Table~\ref{tab:bonferroni_both}.

A+ rate is the null, the stratified signal does not reach significance.

\textbf{Geographic-concentration null (dedicated simulation).}
\label{sec:geo_conc_null}
Domed monuments are concentrated in the Eurasian corridor:
\domeEurasianFraction\% fall in 20\textdegree--120\textdegree{}E
versus \fullCorpusEurasianFraction\% of the full corpus.
Two targeted null models test whether this explains the dome enrichment
(code: \texttt{dome\_geographic\_concentration\_test.py}, \simNperms{} trials).

\textbf{Null~A (within-dome bootstrap):} Draw $N = \NcircValidated$ longitudes
from the dome sites' own list. Mean $= \geoNullDomeBootMean$ A+,
$p = \geoNullDomeBootP$ (ns). The dome sites' positions are concentrated
near harmonic longitudes.

\textbf{Null~C (restricted geographic draw):} Pool all full-corpus sites
within $\pm W$\textdegree{} of any dome site and bootstrap the expected A+
count. The primary window ($\pm 5$\textdegree{}, $N = \geoNullDomeRestrictedN$)
was chosen as a practical compromise: $\pm 2$\textdegree{} yields a smaller
but still adequate pool ($N = \geoNullDomeRestrictedNtwo$), while
$\pm 10$\textdegree{} expands the pool to near-corpus size
($N = \geoNullDomeRestrictedNten$). Dome sites (\NcircTierAp~A+, \NcircApRate\%) significantly
exceed the broader footprint at all three windows:
$\pm 2$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPtwo$, *);
$\pm 5$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, **);
$\pm 10$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPten$, **).
The result is window-independent.

\textbf{Reconciliation.}
Null~A confirms that the dome sites' longitude distribution reproduces their
enrichment (geographic concentration is necessary). Null~C establishes that
other monuments at the same longitudes are significantly less enriched
(geographic concentration is not sufficient). The residual question is whether
the sub-longitude precision of domed monument placement reflects metrological
intent or an unmodeled aspect of these civilizations.

In the global anchor sweep (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}),
Gerizim scores \GerizimSweepAp{} A+ (\anchorSweepPctile{}\textsuperscript{th}
percentile); Jerusalem scores \JerusalemSweepAp{}
(\JerusalemSweepPctile{}\textsuperscript{th}).
The meta-keyword test confirms that only \metaBeatingChiPct\% of
\NmetaKeywords{} candidate words achieve comparable enrichment.

\paragraph*{Methodological note: Rayleigh phase-lock (Test~B).}
\label{sec:results_rayleigh}
The near-perfect Rayleigh $R$ of the A+ sub-sample is a mathematical
consequence of the A+ classification criterion (\S\ref{sec:circularity})
and is reported as a structural description, not independent evidence.
Three formal permutation tests (\NpermRayleigh{} shuffles each, seed~\simSeed{};
\texttt{x18\_periodicity\_formal\_test.py}) address distinct aspects of the
periodicity:

\textbf{Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh, negative control).}
The full-corpus Rayleigh $R = \fullRayleighR$
($Z = \fullRayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$, ns)
confirms no spurious global 3\textdegree{} periodicity is present in the
raw longitude file. The signal is concentrated in A+ sites, not a
global artifact.

\textbf{Test~B (A+-only Rayleigh, structural).}
Among the \Nap{} Tier-A+ sites, $R = \rayleighR$
(\rayleighRPctPhrasing{} A+ sites share the same phase modulo
3\textdegree{}; $Z = \rayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} < \rayleighPermP$).
This confirms self-consistency of the A+ classification but is not
independent evidence, as A+ membership is defined by proximity to a
harmonic (\S\ref{sec:circularity}).

\textbf{Test~C (circular-shift anchor sensitivity).}
Displacing the anchor by a uniform random offset in $[0\textdegree,
360\textdegree)$ and recounting A+ sites yields a null mean of
\anchorShiftNullMu{} vs.\ the observed \anchorShiftApCount{}
($Z = \anchorShiftZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, *).
This is the sole positive test that the enrichment depends on the
specific Gerizim anchor rather than geographic concentration alone.

\subsubsection*{The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic structure}
\label{sec:x18artifact}

The most striking result of the global sweep is that every longitude of
the form $x.18$\textdegree{}E (all \NharmonicsCoarse{} such anchors) produces identically
the maximum A+ count. The spacing between consecutive $x.18$\textdegree{}E
anchors is exactly 3\textdegree{} = 0.1~beru.

The label ``$x.18$\textdegree{}E'' is a rounded shorthand for the phase
$\approx 2.18$\textdegree{} mod 3.0\textdegree{}.
Gerizim's phase ($\GerizimLon \bmod 3.0 = \GerizimPhase$\textdegree{},
gap $\phaseGap$\textdegree{} $\approx \phaseGapKm$~km) is within
coordinate uncertainty.

Any site near the $x.18$\textdegree{} phase can serve as a high-scoring
anchor. The choice of Gerizim was made on historical grounds
(\S\ref{sec:anchor}), and the corridor result is anchor-invariant
within the band.

\textbf{Non-independence note.} \emph{The 3\textdegree{} periodicity and the unit
sensitivity sweep (\S\ref{sec:metrology}) are two views of a single
pattern and do not constitute independent evidence. Neither provides
evidential value beyond the dome enrichment (Test~2) and cluster asymmetry (Test~3).}

Two interpretations apply: (1)~a global grid with 3\textdegree{} spacing
anchored to the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor would produce exactly this
pattern; (2)~Eurasian heritage sites cluster in longitude bands that happen
to align with the $x.18$\textdegree{} phase — this cannot be refuted on the
UNESCO corpus alone.

Code: \texttt{peak\_geography\_audit.py}, \texttt{verify\_x18\_periodicity.py}.

\subsubsection*{Formal statistical tests for the $x.18$\textdegree{} periodicity}
\label{sec:x18_formal}

Four permutation tests (\NpermRayleigh{} shuffles, seed~\simSeed{};
code: \texttt{x18\_periodicity\_formal\_test.py}):
the full-corpus Rayleigh $R = \fullRayleighR$ ($p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$, ns)
confirms no spurious global periodicity in the raw longitude file.
The A+-only Rayleigh ($R = \rayleighR$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \rayleighPermP$)
is structurally expected by the A+ classification criterion and is not
independent evidence (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}).
A circular-shift anchor null recovers only $\sim\!\anchorShiftNullMu$ A+ on average
versus \anchorShiftApCount{} at Gerizim ($Z = \anchorShiftZ$,
$p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, *), providing the only positive evidence that 
the enrichment depends on the Gerizim anchor rather than geographic concentration; 
the discovery-corrected max‑permutation (Test~D) places the sweep 
optimum within the expected range after accounting for 
the \anchorSweepNanchors{}‑anchor search ($p = \anchorMaxPermP$, ns).

The dome-periodicity audit (\texttt{dome\_periodicity\_audit.py}) is consistent
with beru-alignment but equally consistent with geographic concentration:
Gerizim scores at the \domeGerizimPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile
among dome sites, dome site phases are approximately uniform
($\chi^2 = \domePhaseChiSq$, df\,=\,9, $p = \domePhaseChiP$, trend-level),
and all \NcircTierAp{} dome A+ sites have phases within
$\pm 0.15$\textdegree{} of the $x.18$\textdegree{} optimal phase.
The dome enrichment surviving all three simulation nulls remains the primary
evidence against a purely geographic account.

\textbf{Corridor and landmark ranking.}
Gerizim (\GerizimSweepAp{} A+, $p = \clusterApBinom$) and Jerusalem
(\JerusalemSweepAp{} A+, $p = \pJerusalemAp$) both place the
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor in the global top \anchorSweepTopPct\%.
The A+ counts differ by one (Jerusalem finds Gerizim as an extra hit at 4.1~km),
but the compositions diverge: 18 A+ sites are unique to Gerizim,
19 are unique to Jerusalem, and 38 are shared.
The enrichment belongs to the corridor, not to either anchor individually.
Using each UNESCO site as its own anchor, Gerizim ranks
\#\GerizimTempleRank{} of 1012 (top 2.8\%). The 35\textdegree{}E corridor
leads globally; no other corridor shares a cross-cultural tradition.

\subsection{Spatial Autocorrelation}
\label{sec:autocorrelation}

A+ harmonics host $\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ as many total UNESCO
sites as non-A+ harmonics ($p\ \clusterHarmonicMWpStr$).
DEFF correction via ICC ($\pm 0.5$\textdegree{}, $\rho = \ICChalf$):
DEFF~$= \DEFFhalf$, $N_\mathrm{eff} = \NeffHalf$,
corrected $p = \pNeffHalf$.
Block bootstrap (3\textdegree{} blocks, \simNperms{} resamples):
95\% CI $[\blockBootCIlo\%, \blockBootCIhi\%]$ excludes the \NullRateAp{} null.
Within-group permutation using UNESCO-region $\times$ longitude bins
reproduced the observed A+ count exactly in every trial, confirming this
test cannot discriminate alignment from geographic concentration;
the Null~A and Null~C simulations (\S\ref{sec:geo_conc_null})
address that question directly.

\subsection{Null Model Validation}
\label{sec:simulation_null}

Three simulation nulls (\simNperms{} trials each, seed~$= \simSeed{}$,
\citealt{good2005permutation}):
(1)~Longitude permutation: dome ($N = \NcircValidated$)
$p_\mathrm{perm} = \simDomePermP$~(**);
canon ($N = \NcanonSites$) $p = \simCanonPermP$~(ns);
pre-2000 ($N = \NpreTwoK$) $p = \simPreTwoKPermP$~($^\dagger$).
(2)~Bootstrap from full corpus: dome $p_\mathrm{boot} = \simDomeBootP$~(*).
(3)~KDE-smoothed null: expected $\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$, observed \Nap{}, $Z = \simKDEZ$,
$p = \simKDEP$~(*).
Dome enrichment survives all three models.
The geographic-concentration nulls (Null~A, Null~C;
\S\ref{sec:geo_conc_null}) show that proximity to harmonic longitudes
is necessary but not sufficient to explain the dome enrichment.

\subsection{Pipeline Portability: Wikidata Q180987 Stupa Corpus}
\label{sec:stupas_q180987}

The pipeline was applied to $N = \wikiStupaTotal{}$ Wikidata-attested stupas
(class Q180987) retrieved independently of the UNESCO corpus, as a portability
demonstration \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.
The Kedu Plain (Java) hosts the densest cluster at the 2.5-beru harmonic,
anchored by \textbf{Borobudur} (c.\ 800~CE, \wikiStupaBorobudurDevKm~km from
the harmonic, Tier~A) \citep{miksic1990borobudur}, with the site closest to the
harmonic line reaching Tier~A++ (\wikiStupaNgawenDevM~m).
The World Peace Pagoda at Lumbini achieves A++ precision
($\sim$\wikiStupaLumbiniDevM~m) uniquely from Gerizim.

Using the identical anchor and null, the corpus yields
\wikiStupaATierCount/\wikiStupaTotal{} Tier-A sites
(\wikiStupaATierRate\%, $\wikiStupaATierEnrich\times$ null;
binomial $p\ \wikiStupaATierBinomP$, \wikiStupaATierBinomStar;
permutation $Z = \wikiStupaPermZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \wikiStupaPermP$,
\wikiStupaPermStar).
No Tier-A+ enrichment is found ($p = \wikiStupaApBinomP$,
\wikiStupaApBinomStar), consistent with cluster-level rather than
individual-site precision.
Harmonic nodes with at least one Tier-A site carry $\wikiStupaClusterRatio\times$
more stupa sites on average than empty nodes, mirroring the cluster-asymmetry
result of Test~3.

\textbf{Geographic tier breakdown and bimodal structure.}
A sub-regional breakdown reveals a bimodal pattern consistent with the
Buddhist A/C enrichment in the UNESCO religion audit (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}).
The Java/Sumatra node (105\textdegree--115\textdegree{}E, near the 2.5-beru harmonic at 110.3\textdegree{}E)
concentrates \wikiJavaNodeATierN{} of \wikiJavaNodeN{} sites at Tier-A (\wikiJavaNodeATierRate\%, OR $= \wikiJavaNodeATierOR\times$, $p < 0.001$),
rising to \wikiJavaTightATierN/\wikiJavaTightN{} (\wikiJavaTightATierRate\%, OR $= \wikiJavaTightATierOR\times$, $p < 0.001$) in the 107\textdegree--112\textdegree{}E window,
where A+ is also elevated at \wikiJavaTightApRate\% (OR $= \wikiJavaTightApOR\times$, $p = \wikiJavaTightApP$).
The physical heartland, India/Nepal (70\textdegree--90\textdegree{}E) and Myanmar/Cambodia
(90\textdegree--105\textdegree{}E), is A-tier depleted (OR $= 0.15$--$0.68\times$, ns)
but strongly enriched in the inter-harmonic C-band:
Myanmar/Cambodia reaches \wikiMyanmarCbandRate\% (OR $= \wikiMyanmarCbandOR\times$, $p < 0.001$) and the
combined heartland \wikiHeartlandCbandRate\% (OR $= \wikiHeartlandCbandOR\times$, $p < 0.001$).
The two zones are geographically anti-correlated in the same way as the
Buddhist bimodal, supporting a grid-level rather than site-selection account.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment}
\label{sec:limitations_circularity}

\textbf{The public-data constraint.}
Both the UNESCO coordinate file and the candidate anchor (Mount Gerizim)
are fully public. Any investigator could run this pipeline at any time,
before or after seeing these results. Pre-registration cannot verify temporal
priority for statistical analyses of public databases: the data are
permanently accessible, so any registration claim is unverifiable.
The relevant question is whether the reported pattern exists in the data
and whether the anchor choice was justified independently of the result.
Both are addressed directly.

\textbf{What the anchor sweep establishes.}
The global anchor sweep (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}) tests all
\NharmonicsCoarse{} possible $x.18$\textdegree{}E anchors against the same
corpus. Gerizim and Jerusalem both place the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor
in the global top \anchorSweepTopPct\% of all trial anchors.
The enrichment is a property of the corridor, not of the specific anchor
chosen within it. The choice of Gerizim was made on documented historical
grounds (\S\ref{sec:anchor}); that its longitude falls within the optimal
phase band is an empirical observation, not a post-hoc rationalization.

\textbf{What remains genuinely contaminated.}
The narrative framing of Gerizim as a historically motivated anchor
is post-hoc in the sense that reporting it was conditioned on observing
the phase alignment. The Rayleigh Test~B is tautological by construction
(\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}). These are noted for completeness.

\textbf{What is not contaminated.}
The 3\textdegree{} periodic structure in the UNESCO longitude file is
recoverable by any unanchored \NharmonicsCoarse-phase scan without
reference to Gerizim. The dome enrichment (Test~2) is identical across
all \NharmonicsCoarse{} $x.18$\textdegree{}E anchors. The cluster
asymmetry (Test~3) requires no keyword filter and no anchor.

Accordingly, the Levantine corridor statistics are reported as the motivated
entry point rather than independent confirmation, and the Bonferroni
correction is applied as a descriptive bound on effect magnitude.

\subsection{Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias}

Geographic and cultural selection bias is the most important alternative
explanation. The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the
Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor
(Europe \& North America: \CorpusPctEurope\% of Cultural/Mixed sites;
Arab States: \CorpusPctArabStates\%;
Asia-Pacific: \CorpusPctAsiaPac\%;
Americas: \CorpusPctLatAm\%;
Africa: \CorpusPctAfrica\%).
Permutation tests show geographic concentration can reproduce the
sensitivity peak (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_slope_perm}). The dome
enrichment surviving the restricted geographic draw (Null~C,
$p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, **) remains the primary evidence against
a purely geographic account.


\subsection{Americas Negative Control}
\label{sec:americas_negative_control}
The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus ($N = 145$) shows directional 
depletion (A+ = 2.1\%, $p = 0.164$, ns) relative to the 
\NullRateAp{} null, consistent with a Eurasian-anchored grid 
predicting reduced harmonic occupancy in the western hemisphere.

\subsection{Other Limitations}

\textbf{Spatial autocorrelation.}
DEFF correction: $N_\mathrm{eff} = \NeffHalf$ (DEFF $= \DEFFhalf$,
corrected $p = \pNeffHalf$). Block bootstrap CI
$[\blockBootCIlo\%, \blockBootCIhi\%]$ excludes the \NullRateAp{} null.

\textbf{Geocoding precision.}
UNESCO XML coordinates are administrative centroids, introducing
unsystematic noise but not directional bias. Dome enrichment is robust
across raw and context-validated populations and all three simulation nulls.

\textbf{Metrological evidence.}
No physical surveying instrument calibrated in beru subdivisions has been 
recovered; documentary sources attest the beru 
system \citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
This study therefore treats the coordinate alignment of Mount Gerizim as
a novel empirical observation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:alt_explanations}

\textbf{(1) Independent convergence.}
Hemispherical structures are a recurring architectural solution and convergent
orientations across cultures are documented \citep{aveni2001skywatchers,aveni2003archaeoastronomy},
but independent convergence predicts no consistent longitude specificity.

\textbf{(2) Geographic concentration.}
This is the strongest alternative and cannot be excluded on this corpus alone.
The restricted geographic draw (Null~C, $p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, **)
shows that other monuments from the same longitude footprint are significantly
less enriched than dome sites.

\textbf{(3) Random coincidence.}
Three simulation approaches fail to reproduce the dome enrichment
(permutation $p = \simDomePermP$; bootstrap $p = \simDomeBootP$).

\subsection{Conclusion}

The UNESCO longitude distribution contains a global 3\textdegree{} periodic
structure matching the 0.1-beru unit. The dome sub-population (Test~2,
Bonferroni $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$, $k = \BonfK$) survives all three
simulation nulls, and A+-bearing harmonics host
$\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ more sites than non-A+ harmonics (Test~3,
$p = \clusterPermP$).

The two findings most resistant to a purely geographic account are
morphological specificity (dome enrichment exceeding the restricted
geographic draw, Null~C, $p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$) and cluster
asymmetry ($p = \clusterPermP$).

\subsubsection*{Replication and methodological transferability}

The pipeline is transferable to any corpus assembled without reference to
this hypothesis. Named heritage registers, protected site inventories, and
operationally defined lists all qualify; the Wikidata Q180987 result
(\S\ref{sec:stupas_q180987}) illustrates one such application.
The null model and tier boundaries are fixed independently of the data,
so further applications to independent corpora will either accumulate
evidence or not.

\section{Keyword Audit Summary}
\label{sec:kwdaudit}

Full site-by-site audit files are in the archived code repository
(\citealt{tenenbaum2026audit}).

\subsection{Dome/Spherical Monument Filter (Tests~2, 2b, 2x, 2bx)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_dome}

\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text.  All \NcircTotal{} matching
sites are included in Test~2 without context gating; the population is
fully determined by the keyword list with no post-hoc filtering.

For Test~2x, ambiguous keywords (\textit{dome, domed, domes, spherical})
are subject to a two-gate context check: Gate~1 (\NdomeGateNeg{} negative
patterns) rejects non-architectural uses; Gate~2 (\NdomeGatePos{} positive
patterns) requires architectural confirmation.  Unambiguous keywords
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}) are accepted without validation.
The full gate lists are in \texttt{lib/dome\_filter.py}.  The seven
rejected sites are: Hiroshima Peace Memorial, Richtersveld, Gyeongju,
Vi\~{n}ales Valley, Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, Uluru-Kata Tju\d{t}a,
and Diqu\'{i}s Stone Spheres.  In every case the exclusion is driven by
the semantic domain of the keyword match, not by the site's longitude.

\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{Test~2b} adds earthen mound-stage
keywords (\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun} unambiguous;
\textit{mound} context-validated) to trace the hemispherical tradition
from burial mound through stupa to architectural dome.  Test~2bx applies
the same architectural context gates to the extended population.
Statistics for all four variants are reported in
\hyperref[sec:resultscircular]{§\ref*{sec:resultscircular}} and
\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{§\ref*{sec:resultsevolution_inline}}.

\subsection{Founding-Origin Classifier (Test~5, Post-Hoc)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_founding}

The founding-origin classifier (\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py})
assigns sites to five categories: Founding Capital (F), Sacred Origin
(S), Founding Monument (M), Founding Axis (X), and Ancient Continuous
Landscape (L).  Each category has its own unambiguous keyword set plus
context-gated ambiguous terms; positive and negative gate patterns are
defined per category in \texttt{keywords.json}.  The classifier was
iteratively tuned after data inspection to reduce false positives;
residual interpretive ambiguity at the boundary between founding-related
and general heritage descriptions is accepted.  Statistics are reported
in \hyperref[sec:results_founding]{§\ref*{sec:results_founding}}; the
full site-by-site listing is in
\texttt{supplementary/audit/founding\_keyword\_audit.txt}.
