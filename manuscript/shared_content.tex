\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

This study follows a two-stage observational workflow. In the
exploratory stage, a 3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E
longitude frame was noted as a candidate organizing structure in the
UNESCO Cultural and Mixed World Heritage corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$):
domed and stupa-related monument sites showed elevated Tier-A+
node-neighborhood clustering under that frame. In the validation
stage, the same frame was applied without modification to the
externally curated Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus, assembled
independently of the UNESCO analysis, to ask whether the candidate
period recovers statistically significant phase concentration in a
corpus whose membership was not under researcher control.

It does. The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$) shows
independently significant Rayleigh phase concentration at
$T=3^\circ$ ($R = \circConcStupaR{}$, perm-$p = \circConcStupaP{}$).
The full UNESCO corpus does not reach significance globally
($R = \circConcUnescoR{}$, perm-$p = \circConcUnescoP{}$, ns), in
line with the full-corpus Rayleigh null. Watson's two-sample and
Kuiper tests confirm that the two corpora have detectably different
phase distributions at $T=3^\circ$ ($p=\circWatsonPUnescoStupa{}$ and
$p=\circKuiperPUnescoStupa{}$ respectively). This is the expected
result for a monument-type-specific signal: the concentration belongs
to dome and stupa architecture, not to the UNESCO heritage corpus as a
whole. The evidentiary claim rests on that finding --- that the signal
is type-specific and the externally curated stupa corpus independently
recovers the same 3\textdegree{} structure --- rather than on
whole-corpus concordance.

The candidate frame is not presented as pre-registered or
parameter-free. The UNESCO keyword filter corpus was assembled with
knowledge of the data, creating researcher degrees of freedom that
the Wikidata external corpus is specifically designed to address.
Conditional p-values for the UNESCO corpus should be read as
exploratory; the Wikidata stupa concentration result carries the
primary external-validation weight.

The conditional audit yields a limited but statistically nontrivial
pattern. Within the candidate frame, \NclusterAp{} of
\NclusterTotal{} UNESCO sites fall in Tier-A+
(\clusterApRate\% versus the \NullRateAp{} geometric null). This is a
sparse-node enrichment result: most sites do not align, and the full
corpus is not globally periodic. The full-corpus Rayleigh periodogram
on raw longitudes is null ($p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$), so
the paper does not claim smooth sinusoidal periodicity, whole-corpus
alignment, a universal grid, historical intentionality, or a unique
Gerizim origin.

The subset and cross-corpus results ask whether the sparse
overrepresentation has structure within Eurasian monument geography.
Domed/stupa-related monuments show stronger node-neighborhood
behavior. Null~A ($p = \geoNullDomeBootP$) locates this enrichment in
the dome/stupa sites' own longitude distribution; that is, the signal
is carried by the monument subset's geography rather than imposed
uniformly across the UNESCO corpus. Null~C asks whether the broader
UNESCO background occupying the same longitude footprint produces
comparable A+ counts; across the tested windows it expects about
\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeantwo{}--\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeanten{} A+
hits rather than the observed \NcircTierAp{}, with marginal
same-footprint evidence for residual subset structure.

The Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) test provides an
anchor-concordance view at the candidate 3\textdegree{} period.
Monument corpus pairs recover similar anchors ($K \geq \npcDomeStupaK{}$
in all monument configurations, consensus grid lines within
\npcMonGridDeltaE\textdegree{}--\npcTwoGridDeltaE\textdegree{} of
35\textdegree{}E; Table~\ref{tab:npc_summary}), but the non-nested
two-corpus NPC tests do not reach $p<0.05$ individually. This is
consistent with the monument-type-specific framing: NPC measures
cross-corpus anchor concordance, and the Wikidata stupa
concentration result is the stronger individual statement. OWTRAD
Silk Road route nodes provide a negative control at the same period
and do not share the monument NPC anchor.

\subsection{Evidentiary Hierarchy}
\label{sec:evidentiary_hierarchy}

The analyses are organized around five inferential roles so that
primary claims, boundary conditions, and exploratory material are not
assigned the same weight.

\textbf{A. Primary external validation.}
The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$) is an
independently curated external database whose membership was not
under researcher control. It shows statistically significant Rayleigh
phase concentration at $T=3^\circ$ ($R = \circConcStupaR{}$,
perm-$p = \circConcStupaP{}$, \circConcStupaPSig{}). This is the
primary external-validation result. Watson's two-sample and Kuiper
tests confirm that the Wikidata stupa and UNESCO full-corpus phase
distributions are detectably different ($p=\circWatsonPUnescoStupa{}$
and $p=\circKuiperPUnescoStupa{}$ respectively), consistent with a
monument-type-specific signal: the concentration belongs to dome and
stupa architecture, not to the UNESCO heritage list as a whole.
Table~\ref{tab:npc_summary} additionally reports five NPC
configurations at $T=3^\circ$; all monument corpus pairs recover high
$K \geq \npcDomeStupaK{}$ with consensus grid lines within
\npcDomeStupaGridDeltaE\textdegree{}--\npcTwoGridDeltaE\textdegree{}
of 35\textdegree{}E. The non-nested two-corpus tests (Rows~1--2) do
not reach $p<0.05$ individually; the three-corpus test including the
nested dome subset does (Row~3). Replacing the dome subset with the
Americas geographic control (Row~4) or OWTRAD trade nodes (Row~5)
collapses concordance. NPC is reported as supplementary evidence; the
Wikidata concentration result carries the primary evidentiary weight.

\textbf{B. Exploratory discovery corpus.}
The UNESCO Cultural/Mixed corpus and its nested keyword-filtered
dome/stupa subset are the observational origin of the candidate
frame. Within the specified 3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E
harmonic-deviation representation, the UNESCO corpus shows modest
full-corpus Tier-A+ enrichment, the dome/stupa subset shows stronger
A++ and A+ behavior, and harmonic-band / cluster-asymmetry tests
characterize the distribution of sites around A+ harmonics.
Because the keyword filter was constructed with knowledge of the data,
these are conditional exploratory results, not the primary
external-validation evidence. They motivated the Wikidata test.

\textbf{C. Geographic conditioning.}
Null~A locates the dome/stupa signal in the subset's own longitude
geography. Null~C asks whether the broader UNESCO background occupying
the same longitude footprint produces comparable A+ counts. Together
they treat geography as part of the object being studied, while still
testing whether same-footprint background expectations are sufficient.

\textbf{D. Boundary conditions.}
The Rayleigh periodogram tests for global sinusoidal periodicity in
raw longitudes and is null for the full UNESCO corpus; the global anchor-discovery correction is
marginal; and OWTRAD does not share the monument NPC anchor. Watson's
$U^2$ and Kuiper tests confirm that the UNESCO full and Wikidata stupa
phase distributions are detectably different at $T=3^\circ$, as
expected for a type-specific signal. These results prevent an
invariant-period, whole-corpus, or generic Eurasian-spatial-dataset
interpretation.

\textbf{E. Exploratory and descriptive material.}
Religion subgroup analyses, the temporal gradient, mound-evolution
extensions, the founding classifier, and other sensitivity results
are reported to characterize the pattern and generate hypotheses.
They are not load-bearing evidence for the primary conditional claim.

Prior longitude-alignment studies were criticized for
investigator-assembled site lists
\citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}. This study
uses externally curated corpora and fully deposited code
\citep{tenenbaum2026audit}. Its evidentiary standard is transparent
conditional testing under a clearly specified candidate frame, not a
claim that the pattern was discovered in a parameter-free or
geography-independent way.

\subsection{Prior Work on Spatial Periodicity and Sacred Landscapes}

Statistical evaluation of spatial periodicity in monument distributions has a
long and contested history.
Thom's ``megalithic yard'' \citep{thom1967,thom1971,thom1964}
stimulated statistical refinement \citep{kendall1974} and methodological
critique \citep{freeman1976}.
Magli \citep{magli2013,magli2016} has applied Monte Carlo methods to
archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have advanced
probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims
\citep{ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.
The ICOMOS-IAU thematic study \citep{ruggles2010icomos} provides
institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage
properties.

\subsection{Reference Phase and Candidate Anchors}
\label{sec:anchor}

The harmonic deviations in this study are computed relative to a
3\textdegree{} phase in the Levantine $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor.
Mount Gerizim (\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E) is used as the named reference
coordinate for that phase because of its independently documented
\textit{tabbur ha-aretz} (``navel of the earth'') role in Samaritan
tradition \citep{purvis1968,macdonald1964,crown1989}. That background
motivates a candidate point within the corridor; it is not treated as
statistical evidence that Gerizim is the unique origin of a longitude
frame.

\textbf{Caveat on anchor identification.}
The statistical analysis cannot uniquely identify which physical
location, if any, historically defined a phase origin. Any longitude in
the \xbandLabel\textdegree{}E band produces the same selected-frame
enrichment, and any UNESCO site within the A+ tier
($\lesssim$\ApThreshKm{}~km) of the Gerizim-labeled co-grid could be used
to reconstruct an essentially identical representation. The primary
object is therefore the 3\textdegree{} phase and $\sim$35\textdegree{}E
corridor, with Gerizim functioning as one historically motivated label
for that phase.

A site-as-anchor ranking audit (Supplementary Audit S6) is consistent
with this interpretation: the top-ranked competing anchors, including
the Silk Roads: Zarafshan-Karakum Corridor (62.21\textdegree{}E,
deviation 0.061\textdegree{} from Gerizim harmonic $n=9$), the
Birthplace of Jesus (35.21\textdegree{}E), and Old City of Sana'a
(44.21\textdegree{}E), all lie within the A+ tier of the Gerizim-labeled co-grid
and are therefore co-grid points rather than independent alternatives.

A global anchor sweep (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}) likewise shows
that the result is not sensitive to the exact coordinate: every
longitude in the \xbandLabel\textdegree{}E band produces the same
enrichment, and both Gerizim and Jerusalem
(\JerusalemLon\textdegree{}E, $\approx$\GerizimJerusalemKm~km south)
place the corridor in the global top \anchorSweepTopPct\% of
\anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors. Mecca ($p \approx \pMeccaAnchor$,
\pMeccaAnchorSig{}), Mt.\ Meru ($p \approx \pMeruAnchor$,
\pMeruAnchorSig{}), and Mt.\ Kailash ($p \approx \pKailashAnchor$,
\pKailashAnchorSig{}) yield no significant enrichment under the same
procedure.

Gerizim's coordinate is from the UNESCO tentative list (ref.~5706, DMS
\GerizimLonDMS{}, State of Palestine, 2012;
\url{https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/}) and is not
inscribed. Jerusalem is an inscribed corpus member, self-excluded when
used as anchor.

\subsection{Corpus Construction}
\label{sec:corpus}

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset \citep{unescowhc2024xml} contains
$\NwhcTotal$ inscribed nominations. UNESCO designates each site
\textit{Cultural} (criteria i--vi), \textit{Natural} (criteria vii--x),
or \textit{Mixed}; sites with cultural heritage alongside natural values
receive Mixed and are included here.
Retaining Cultural and Mixed ($-\NexcNatural$ Natural-only) and
excluding $\NexcNoCoord$ entries lacking geocoordinates (both linear
route networks) yields $N = \NclusterTotal$ sites.

\subsection{Geodetic Framework}
\label{sec:geodetic}

The selected representation uses a 3\textdegree{} phase in the
Levantine $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor, operationalized by the Mount
Gerizim coordinate at \GerizimLon\textdegree{}E (see
\S\ref{sec:anchor}). Gerizim is not inscribed and does not appear in
the standard corpus. Jerusalem is inscribed and scored as a regular
site in Tests~1--4 (A+ at 4.1~km under the Gerizim-labeled phase).
For sensitivity sweeps (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}), Gerizim is
added as a synthetic entry so each anchor self-excludes symmetrically.

For a site at longitude $\lambda$, the deviation from the nearest 3\textdegree{} harmonic is
$\delta = |\lambda - \GerizimLon| \bmod 3\textdegree{}$ (folded to $[0, 1.5\textdegree{}]$).
The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion.
Kilometer labels (e.g.\ $\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km for Tier-A+) are equatorial
approximations computed as $\delta \times 111$~km/\textdegree{},
becoming smaller at higher latitudes; the angular criterion is consequently
conservative at high latitudes. No latitude correction is applied.

The geometric null for Tier-A+ is $2 \times \ApThreshDeg{}\textdegree{} / 1.5\textdegree{} = \NullRateApPct{}\%$,
assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference, but
the primary tests are simulation-based null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null})
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian KDE produces
$\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$ expected A+ (${\sim}\NullRateApPct{}\%$), indicating the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.


\subsection{Tier Classification}
\label{sec:tier_classification}

Tier boundaries are derived from the cross-corpus von~Mises dispersion scale
$\hat{\sigma} = \vmSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (stupa corpus, $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaStupa{}$;
see ``Von Mises cross-corpus characterisation'' below).
The three A-tiers correspond to $0.25\hat{\sigma}$, $0.50\hat{\sigma}$, and
$1.00\hat{\sigma}$ respectively.

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A++}] $0.25\hat{\sigma}$;\quad
    $\delta \leq \AppThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\AppThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A+}] $0.50\hat{\sigma}$ (primary threshold);\quad
    $\delta \leq \ApThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A}] $1.00\hat{\sigma}$;\quad
    $\delta \leq \ATierThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\ATierThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~B}] Middle zone, not near any harmonic or inter-harmonic midpoint.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~C/C$-$/C$--$}] Symmetric mirror of A/A+/A++ measured from the
    nearest inter-harmonic midpoint.
\end{description}

\textbf{Von Mises cross-corpus characterisation.}
We fitted $f(\theta \mid \kappa) \propto e^{\kappa\cos\theta}$ (von~Mises + uniform
mixture) to the harmonic phase deviations of two external corpora:
OWTRAD Silk Road nodes ($N = \vmNOwtrad{}$; $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaOwtrad{}$) and
Wikidata Q180987 stupas ($N = \vmNStupa{}$; $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaStupa{}$).
The stupa corpus yields $\hat{\sigma} = \kappa^{-1/2} = \vmSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$;
$0.25\hat{\sigma} = \vmHalfSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A++),
$0.50\hat{\sigma} = \vmOneSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A+),
and $1.00\hat{\sigma} = \vmTwoSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A)
match the tier thresholds to within rounding (Table~\ref{tab:vonmises_tiers}).
The $N$-weighted pooled $\hat{\sigma}$ is $\vmSigmaDegPooled{}\textdegree{}$.
Both external corpora are concentrated in the Eurasian corridor, so
their use here fixes a cross-corpus angular scale within the same
geographic domain rather than providing geography-independent
validation.

\subsection{Test Populations}
\label{sec:test_populations}

Keyword populations are approximate. The raw sweep tolerates known
false positives while the context-validated variant (Test~2x) applies
sentence-level gating for greater precision.
Both populations are reported.

\textbf{Test~1 (full corpus, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; binomial test of
whether the global A+ rate exceeds the \NullRateApPct{}\% geometric null.

\textbf{Test~2 (dome/spherical, primary).}
\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text ($N = \NcircTotal$).
This raw sweep is over-inclusive: it admits sites where the keyword
refers to geological or vernacular features rather than monumental
architecture. The context-validated population ($N = \NcircValidated$)
is reported alongside all primary results; it yields stronger
significance, consistent with the rejected sites being
non-architectural false positives.

\textbf{Test~2b (mound evolution, sensitivity variant).}
Extends Test~2 by adding earthen mound-stage keywords
(\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun}).
Not counted separately in the Bonferroni family.

\textbf{Test~3 (cluster asymmetry, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; tests whether
A+ harmonics host more total sites than non-A+ harmonics, independent
of any morphological keyword.

\textbf{Test~4 (temporal gradient, descriptive).}
Cochran-Armitage trend test on the full corpus based on year of
inscription into the UNESCO corpus, no keyword filter.

\textbf{Test~5 (founding classifier, post-hoc).}
A post-hoc keyword classifier identifies site types beyond domed monuments that fall on harmonic nodes; it carries no weight in the primary evidential chain (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).

\subsection{Multiple-Comparisons Protocol}
\label{sec:bonferroni}

\textbf{Full reported family ($k = \NtotalTests$).}
The multiple-comparisons headline treats the \NtotalTests{} tests in the
FDR audit as the correction family. Six results survive Bonferroni at
$k = \NtotalTests$: dome/spherical A++ (binomial
$p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepCircApp$, with the Null~A
geographic-conditioning result discussed in \S\ref{sec:geo_conc_null}), full-corpus A+
($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterApBinom$), cluster permutation
($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterPermP$), harmonic
Mann-Whitney ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterHarmonicMWp$),
sequential-peak permutation ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepeakSigP$),
and evolution combined A++ (binomial
$p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepEvoApp$).

\textbf{Non-primary Tests~1--3 sensitivity set.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Test 1} (full corpus, $N = \NclusterTotal$):
    global enrichment above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 2} (domed/spherical monuments, $N = \NcircTotal$):
    morphological sub-population enriched above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 3} (cluster asymmetry, full corpus, no keyword filter):
    A+-bearing harmonics host more total sites.
\end{itemize}
This three-test grouping was not pre-specified and is reported only as
a raw-p-value sensitivity summary. The primary multiple-comparisons
report is the full $k = \NtotalTests$ audit above.

\textbf{Other tests.}
Test~4 (temporal gradient, Cochran-Armitage) is descriptive context;
a Mantel-Haenszel stratified test and logistic regression with region as a
covariate assess the gradient robustly (\S\ref{sec:resultstemporal});
it is not used as a Bonferroni-survivor claim.
Test~2b is a sensitivity variant of Test~2.
Test~5 (founding classifier) is post-hoc (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
World religion sub-group analyses (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}) use Fisher exact tests and are not Bonferroni-corrected.
Tests~A, C, D, and E (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) assess the
3\textdegree{} periodicity structure: Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh) is a
boundary-condition test for invariant sinusoidal periodicity;
Tests~C and~D are conditional anchor-specific tests; Test~E is the anchor-discovery
correction. Test~B is tautological by construction and excluded from both
tables.

\subsection{Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) Test}
\label{sec:npc_methods}

The Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) test is a conditional
anchor-concordance test at the candidate 3\textdegree{} period. It does
not evaluate how the candidate frame was found; it asks whether
related monument datasets, including externally curated corpora, yield
similar anchor phases once that period is specified. The period
$T=3^\circ$ is fixed from the candidate-frame analysis, so NPC is a
follow-up test at a stated candidate period rather than a blind search
over periods.
For each corpus $c$ and candidate spacing $T$, site longitudes are
mapped to phases on the $T$-periodic circle and summarized by the mean
phasor
\[
  Z_c(T) = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{i=1}^{N_c}
  \exp\!\left(2\pi i\,\lambda_{ci}/T\right).
\]
The corpus contributes only its direction,
$\widetilde{Z}_c(T)=Z_c(T)/|Z_c(T)|$, so NPC compares recovered
anchors rather than giving larger or more concentrated corpora more
weight. For $C$ corpora,
\[
  K(T)=\left|\frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C}\widetilde{Z}_c(T)\right|
\]
ranges from 0 to 1, with larger values indicating tighter agreement
among recovered anchor phases.

The permutation null preserves each corpus's internal phasor magnitude
but destroys cross-corpus phase agreement by independently rotating
each corpus's unit phasor by a uniform random angle. The resulting
permutation distribution asks whether the observed directions are more
concordant than expected if corpora retained their own internal
structure but had unrelated anchors.

NPC and the full-corpus Rayleigh test address different alternatives.
Rayleigh asks whether one corpus has a global sinusoidal periodicity in
raw longitude at 3\textdegree{}. NPC asks whether several datasets yield
similar anchor phases at a specified period after their phasors are
normalized. A positive NPC result and a null Rayleigh result can
therefore coexist; neither test substitutes for the other.

The monument NPC analysis uses the UNESCO Cultural/Mixed corpus
($N=\npcNUnesco{}$), the context-validated UNESCO domed-monument subset
($N=\npcNDome{}$), and the Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus
($N=\npcNStupa{}$). The UNESCO dome subset is nested within the UNESCO
corpus; the Wikidata stupa corpus is externally curated and represents
the independent-validation data stream whose membership was not under
researcher control.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions: What Is Not Being Claimed}
\label{sec:results_phase_invariant}
\label{sec:results_boundary}

Three checks delimit the scope of the present claim before the
within-frame diagnostics are reported. They tell us what the result is
\textit{not}: it is not an invariant global 3\textdegree{} periodicity
in raw longitude, and it is not a phase ranking that survives a full
anchor-discovery correction. We treat these results as boundary
conditions on the interpretation, not as a verdict on the
selected-frame enrichment or on the NPC anchor-concordance result
reported below.

(i)~The full-corpus Rayleigh test returns null
($p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$~\fullRayleighPermPSig{}). The
Rayleigh/periodogram null tells us what the result is not: it is not
global sinusoidal periodicity in raw longitude at 3\textdegree{}.
The conditional diagnostics ask a different question: whether sparse
node-neighborhood overrepresentation is structured within the
specified candidate frame. Lattice-node proximity and spectral
periodicity are distinct mathematical objects, so a selected-frame
count excess can coexist with a null Rayleigh statistic. The null
Rayleigh result prevents an invariant-period interpretation.

(ii)~A phase-envelope scan over all origins
$\phi \in [0,3\textdegree{})$ (\phaseNphases{} phases at \phaseStep\textdegree{}
resolution) records strong phase localization within the selected
3\textdegree{} circle: full-corpus A+ counts span
\phaseFullMin{}--\phaseFullMax{} (median \phaseFullMedian{}), and
Gerizim's phase ($\phaseGerizimPhase\textdegree{}$ mod 3\textdegree{})
sits at the \phaseFullGerPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile
(\phaseFullGerCount{} A+), the
\phaseDomeGerPctile\textsuperscript{th} for domes
(\phaseDomeGerCount/\NcircTotal{}; range
\phaseDomeMin{}--\phaseDomeMax{}). Within the selected representation,
the chosen phase is unusually good; this is a within-frame
diagnostic, not an invariant claim.

(iii)~A degree-denominated Monte Carlo unit sweep identifies
3\textdegree{} as the dominant period (\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo});
adjacent non-harmonic units are null
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}). The unit sweep does not require an anchor
and is reported here as the unit-side complement to the phase scan:
together they indicate that 3\textdegree{} is the spacing at which
the within-frame count excess concentrates and that the selected
phase is the phase at which the concentration is sharpest, while the
anchor-discovery correction across all anchors is marginal
(Test~E, $p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{};
\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}).

(iv)~NPC is reported as a separate anchor-concordance result rather than
as a substitute for Rayleigh. Rayleigh tests global smooth periodicity
within one corpus; NPC tests similarity of recovered anchors across
datasets at the candidate period. The two results can coexist because
they address different alternatives.

The remainder of the Results follows the evidentiary hierarchy. It
first reports the primary conditional frame evidence in the full
corpus, dome/stupa subset, and harmonic-band structure; it then
reports NPC anchor concordance across related monument datasets and
places robustness and exploratory diagnostics in separate sections.
These sections are not presented as phase- or anchor-marginalized
confirmation.

\paragraph*{Rayleigh and anchor-specific checks.}
\label{sec:results_rayleigh}
The A+-only Rayleigh statistic is omitted from the results because it is a
mathematical consequence of the A+ classification criterion
(\S\ref{sec:circularity}), not an independent test.
The following formal tests address distinct aspects of the periodicity:

\textbf{Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh periodogram — boundary condition).}
The full-corpus Rayleigh $R = \fullRayleighR$
($Z = \fullRayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$,
\fullRayleighPermPSig{}) at $T = 3\textdegree{}$ is null.
We report this as a boundary condition, not a failure of the
primary claim. The Rayleigh periodogram asks whether raw longitudes
show a phase-marginalized sinusoidal periodicity across the corpus; it
is structurally insensitive to sparse, anchor-specific clustering near
a subset of fixed lattice nodes, because sites near nodes and
anti-nodes are both treated as phase information about the same period.
The present analysis therefore treats the Rayleigh null as evidence
against invariant 3\textdegree{} periodicity, while leaving the
selected-frame node-proximity count excess to be evaluated by the
conditional tests below.

\textbf{Test~C (circular-shift anchor sensitivity).}
Displacing the anchor by a uniform random offset in $[0^\circ,
360^\circ)$ and recounting A+ sites yields a null mean of
\anchorShiftNullMu{} vs.\ the observed \anchorShiftApCount{}
($Z = \anchorShiftZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, \anchorShiftPermPSig{}).
A positive anchor-specific test complementary to Test~D.

\textbf{Test~D (targeted von~Mises mixture, full corpus).}
A von~Mises/uniform two-component mixture is fit to all $N = \NoriginCorpus$
folded angles with $\mu_0$ fixed at the Gerizim phase; $\hat{w}$ and
$\hat{\kappa}$ estimated by EM; $D = 2(\hat{\ell} - \ell_0)$ referred to a
parametric bootstrap ($B = \targetedB$) for an empirical $p$-value.
On the full corpus: $D = \targetedDfull$,
$\hat{w} = \targetedWfull$ (i.e.\ ${\approx}\targetedWfullPct\%$ of all
sites), $\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKfull$
(angular SD $\approx \targetedSdFullDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPfull$ (\targetedPfullSig{}).
On the A+ subset alone: $D = \targetedDAp$, $\hat{w} = \targetedWAp$,
$\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKAp$ (angular SD $\approx \targetedSdApDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPAp$ (\targetedPApSig{}).
Tests~C and~D are positive anchor-specific checks within the selected
3\textdegree{} frame. They support the selected-frame characterization
but do not convert it into a phase-free or whole-corpus periodicity
claim.

\textbf{Test~E (anchor discovery correction).}
A global sweep of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors asks whether the
Levantine corridor ranks unusually among all possible anchors
($p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}).
This is a conservative upper bound on the anchor-selection penalty;
the strongest pattern rests on Tests~2 and~3: Test~2 is stable across
the $\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E co-grid anchors, while Test~3 is
keyword-independent but still characterizes the selected
3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E representation
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_circularity}).

35\textdegree{}E). Both tests yield high $K$ values indicating very
similar recovered anchors, but neither reaches conventional $p<0.05$
in the permutation test. This is expected: with $C=2$ corpora, the
phase-randomization null has only two degrees of freedom, and the test
is sensitive to how much leverage each individual corpus's phasor
provides.

\noindent\textbf{Row~3 (three-corpus supplementary).}
Adding the nested dome subset as a third corpus sharpens the permutation
significance ($K=\npcMonK{}$, perm-$p=\npcMonPermP{}$) because the
dome subset's stronger individual Rayleigh $R$ at $T=3^\circ$ adds
leverage to the phasor sum. The drop from Row~3 significance to Rows 1--2
non-significance is itself informative: it shows the permutation test
detects the leverage the nested corpus provides. Rows 1--2 are the
appropriate non-nested tests; Row~3 is retained as context.

\noindent\textbf{Rows 4--5 (controls).}
Row~4 replaces the dome subset with the UNESCO Americas sub-corpus
(geographically outside the signal region, used as a geographic control
in \S\ref{sec:americas_negative_control}): $K$ drops to
$\npcNegThreeK{}$ (perm-$p=\npcNegThreePermP{}$, ns). Row~5 uses
OWTRAD trade-route nodes in place of a monument corpus:
$K=\npcAllK{}$ (perm-$p=\npcAllPermP{}$, ns). OWTRAD's own Rayleigh
statistic at $T=3^\circ$ is not significant
($R=\npcOwR{}$, $p=\npcOwRayleighP{}$, ns), and its preferred anchor
is \npcOwAnchorDelta\textdegree{} from the monument consensus. These
controls confirm that the high $K$ in Rows~1--3 is not a
generic property of any Eurasian longitude dataset.

NPC asks whether corpora recover similar anchors at the candidate
period, not whether tier counts are elevated. The statistic uses
normalized phasor directions, so it is independent of the tier-count
enrichment results and unaffected by the post-hoc corpus construction
concern that applies to the keyword-filter dome subset.

\noindent\textbf{Distributional tests: UNESCO full vs.\ Wikidata stupa.}
Using the full UNESCO Cultural/Mixed corpus ($N=\circTwoSampNUnesco{}$)
and the Wikidata stupa corpus ($N=\circTwoSampNStupa{}$) as the
highest-powered non-nested pair, five circular tests characterize the
relationship between the two distributions.
Watson's $U^2$ two-sample test ($U^2=\circWatsonUSqUnescoStupa{}$,
perm-$p=\circWatsonPUnescoStupa{}$) and the Kuiper two-sample
test ($V=\circKuiperVUnescoStupa{}$,
perm-$p=\circKuiperPUnescoStupa{}$) both reject the null that
UNESCO full and Wikidata stupa have the same phase distribution at
$T=3^\circ$. This is the expected and interpretively important result:
the full UNESCO corpus is a large heterogeneous dataset whose phase
distribution is dominated by non-dome-monument sites and shows no
global concentration ($R=\circConcUnescoR{}$, perm-$p=\circConcUnescoP{}$, ns),
while the Wikidata stupa corpus is morphologically focused and shows
independently significant phase concentration
($R=\circConcStupaR{}$, perm-$p=\circConcStupaP{}$, \circConcStupaPSig{}).
The finding that the two corpora are \emph{different} distributions is
therefore evidence for monument-type specificity: the 3\textdegree{}
phase concentration belongs to hemispherical monument architecture,
not to the heritage list it happens to appear on.
The Fisher combination of the two per-corpus Rayleigh
p-values is significant ($\chi^2(4)=\circConcUnescoStupaFisherChi{}$,
$p=\circConcUnescoStupaFisherP{}$, \circConcUnescoStupaFisherPSig{}),
driven by the stupa corpus. This combination is reported for
completeness; the primary result is the Wikidata stupa concentration
and the confirmed distributional heterogeneity.

\section{Robustness, Sensitivity, and Exploratory Diagnostics}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Unit Sensitivity Sweep}
\label{sec:metrology}

Varying harmonic spacing from 1.5\textdegree{} to 6\textdegree{} (physical threshold
fixed at \ApThreshKm{}~km), the 3\textdegree{} spacing produces the strongest joint
significance across both the dome and full-corpus populations.
The 1.5\textdegree{} and 6\textdegree{} spacings also reach significance, but both are
mathematical consequences of the 3\textdegree{} structure: every 3\textdegree{}
harmonic is also a 1.5\textdegree{} harmonic, and 6\textdegree{} harmonics are a strict
subset, so neither constitutes an independent signal.
Adjacent non-harmonic spacings (1.8\textdegree{}, 2.1\textdegree{}, 2.7\textdegree{}, 3.3\textdegree{}, 3.6\textdegree{}) are
non-significant in either population, the key discriminating observation.
The 2.4\textdegree{} spacing reaches nominal dome significance
($p = \sweepEightDomeP$) but not full-corpus ($p = \sweepEightFullP$, \sweepEightFullPSig{}),
reflecting unsystematic local overlap with the 3\textdegree{} grid rather than
a separate secondary spacing.
Specifically, \sweepEightTenDomeBoth{}/\sweepZeroEightDomeHits{} 
(\sweepEightTenDomePctOfEight\%) of 2.4\textdegree{} dome hits align 
to the 3\textdegree{} grid, indicating the significance is due to overlap.

A degree-denominated Monte Carlo sweep
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) is consistent with this pattern. Candidate intervals
at 4\textdegree{} and 8\textdegree{} reach nominal dome significance, but
inspection shows that 6 of 7 and all 4 of their hits respectively fall on
12\textdegree{} and 24\textdegree{} nodes shared with the 3\textdegree{}
grid, indicating they are consequences of the selected 3\textdegree{}
spacing rather than independent angular units.

\subsection{Sensitivity Slope at the Canonical Unit}
\label{sec:sensitivity_slope_perm}

A fine sweep within $\pm 1\%$ of 3\textdegree{}
shows a $\pm 0.3\%$ shift collapses joint significance in at least one
population. A geographic bootstrap of the full cultural corpus finds no
permutation in \permSlopeSpecN{} trials that reproduces the canonical-unit
sharp peak at the A$^{+}$ precision tier
($p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointFullAP{}$, \permSlopeCanonBestJointSigFullAP{}),
consistent with
3\textdegree{} as the candidate spacing within the selected-frame
audit. The dome-filter specificity test yields marginal results across all
tiers (A$^{++}$: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointDomeAPP{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigDomeAPP{}; A$^{+}$: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointDomeAP{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigDomeAP{}; A: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointDomeA{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigDomeA{}), consistent with the test being underpowered
at $n = \NcircValidated{}$ sites. Restricting to the \permSlopeKwN{}
morphologically unambiguous spherical forms (stupa, tholos) recovers
significance at the A tier
($p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointKwA{}$, \permSlopeCanonBestJointSigKwA{}). Tests~C and~D
(\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) are consistent with 3\textdegree{} structure.

A log-scale threshold sweep (\logSweepNtaus{} values,
\logSweepAregionNsig/\logSweepAregionNtaus{} significant within $\pm$1
log-decade of A+) yields dome-population data-optimal bands
A$^{++}{\approx}\logSweepOptPtauKm{}$~km,
A$^{+}{\approx}\logSweepOptApKm{}$~km,
A${\approx}\logSweepOptAKm{}$~km

the analytically derived tier boundaries (\AppThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}, \ApThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}, \ATierThreshDeg{}\textdegree{})

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Conditional Frame and Scope}
\label{sec:limitations_circularity}
\label{sec:circularity}

\textbf{Conditional status.}
The analysis is conditional on a stated candidate frame: 3\textdegree{}
spacing, the Gerizim-labeled $\sim$35\textdegree{}E phase, the tier
thresholds, and the dome/stupa keyword definitions. The paper does not
claim pre-registered or parameter-free discovery of this frame, and
conditional p-values should not be read as phase-free discovery
statistics. The relevant inferential questions are instead:
(1)~whether the specified frame organizes the
externally curated UNESCO corpus in a statistically nontrivial way;
(2)~whether the subset signal remains structured under
geographically conditioned nulls; (3)~whether related externally
curated monument corpora show anchor concordance at the same
candidate period; and (4)~whether negative controls delimit the scope
of the pattern.

\textbf{Post-hoc corpus construction.}
The dome/stupa keyword filter (seven morphological keywords, context
validation gates, Test~2b mound extensions) was constructed with
knowledge of the UNESCO data. The corpus definition was not
pre-specified or independent of the observed pattern. This creates
researcher degrees of freedom: the filter's precision tiers, keyword
choices, and context gates could in principle have been tuned to
inflate significance. We acknowledge that corpus-definition
independence cannot be fully established for the UNESCO keyword subset.
The Wikidata Q180987 corpus addresses this directly: it was not
assembled, keyword-filtered, or context-validated by the researchers,
and its membership was fixed before any of its statistics were
computed. Readers should weight the UNESCO keyword-corpus results as
exploratory and the Wikidata cross-corpus concordance as the primary
external evidence.

\textbf{Anchor-specificity as a property of the selected representation.}
The within-frame organization characterized in
\S\ref{sec:results_global}--\S\ref{sec:resultscluster} is by
construction anchor-specific: it is a property of the chosen
3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E representation, not a claim
about a phase that survives marginalization over all anchors. The global anchor sweep
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}) is consistent with the enrichment
being a property of the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor: every
$\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E anchor produces the same A+ count, and
both Gerizim and Jerusalem place the corridor in the global top
\anchorSweepTopPct\% of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors. Gerizim
is distinguished within that corridor by pre-existing documentary
evidence---the \textit{tabbur ha-aretz} cosmographic role
(\S\ref{sec:anchor})---predating this analysis. We do not present
this as a unique solution to an inverse problem; we present Gerizim
as one historically motivated point in a corridor whose width is
itself part of the result.

The 3\textdegree{} structure is recoverable by any unanchored
\NharmonicsCoarse-phase scan within the corridor. Dome enrichment
(Test~2) is identical across all \NharmonicsCoarse{}
$\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E anchors. Cluster asymmetry (Test~3)
requires no keyword filter, but it is still defined by the same
A+ classification and therefore characterizes the selected
representation rather than supplying an anchor-independent test. The
anchor-discovery correction (Test~E,
$p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}) is marginal, which we
report as a boundary condition (\S\ref{sec:results_boundary}) on
phase-free claims. It does not negate the conditional question of
whether the specified frame organizes Eurasian monument data.

\subsection{Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias}
\label{sec:limitations_geo}

The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the
Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor of Cultural/Mixed sites. 
The regional distribution is as follows: 
(Europe \& North America: \CorpusPctEurope\%;
Arab States: \CorpusPctArabStates\%;
Asia-Pacific: \CorpusPctAsiaPac\%;
Latin Americas: \CorpusPctLatAm\%;
Africa: \CorpusPctAfrica\%).
Domed/stupa-related monuments are part of this geography, not a
population from which geographic structure must be removed before the
signal can be meaningful. Null~A ($p = \geoNullDomeBootP$,
\geoNullDomeBootPSig{}) shows that the observed dome/stupa enrichment
is reproducible from the dome/stupa sites' own longitude distribution;
the signal is carried by the subset geography itself. Null~C asks a
different question: whether the broader UNESCO background occupying
the same longitude footprint produces comparable A+ enrichment. Across
the tested windows the same-footprint background expects about
\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeantwo{}--\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeanten{} A+
hits rather than the observed \NcircTierAp{}; the resulting p-values
are nominal and should be interpreted as marginal evidence that the subset's
longitude structure exceeds same-footprint background expectations.

The cross-corpus evidence is likewise geographically conditioned.
UNESCO Cultural/Mixed and Wikidata Q180987 stupas are external
curations, but they are historically and geographically related. The
UNESCO dome subset is nested within the UNESCO corpus. NPC therefore
supports monument-corpus anchor concordance within Eurasian monument
geography rather than independent replication outside that geography.

A Monte Carlo unit sweep (\mcNperms{} permutations, \mcNcandidates{} candidate
spacing values from 0.5\textdegree{} to 15\textdegree{}) identifies which
angular interval produces clustering beyond geographic concentration without
assuming any particular unit. The dominant peak is at 3\textdegree{} ($p < 0.001$),
with harmonics at 6\textdegree{} and 12\textdegree{} also significant.
Collapsing harmonics, the dome subset yields one independent
dominant spacing ($P = \mcDomeCollapsedPEqOne{}$ under the geographic null),
consistent with the selected-frame dome count excess concentrating at
3\textdegree{} rather than at adjacent candidate spacings.

\subsection{Americas Negative Control}
\label{sec:americas_negative_control}
The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus ($N = \AmericasN$) shows an A+ rate of
\AmericasApRate\%, below the \NullRateAp{} null
(one-sided depletion: $p = \AmericasOneSidedP$~\AmericasOneSidedStar{}).
The sample is too small to treat this as strong depletion,
but it does not support extension of the pattern outside the Eurasian
footprint.

\subsection{Other Limitations}

\textbf{Geocoding precision.}
UNESCO XML coordinates are administrative centroids, and nomination
boundaries, serial-site representation, and corpus construction can
affect site longitudes. Such noise is not expected to impose a common
directional bias, but it limits the precision with which any
node-neighborhood result should be interpreted.

\textbf{Metrological evidence.}
A post-hoc numerical correspondence with Babylonian sub-units was
noticed and discarded as non-load-bearing
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological};
all thresholds are degree-denominated from cross-corpus angular
dispersion.

These limitations define the claim's scope rather than eliminating it.
The paper reports a conditional, geographically embedded
monument-corpus pattern under a specified representation; it does not
claim metrology, intentional placement, universal periodicity, or a
unique Gerizim origin.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The empirical result is a sparse, structured node-neighborhood
enrichment in Eurasian monument geography under a specified candidate
3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E frame. The evidence comes from
conditional selected-frame diagnostics and NPC monument-corpus anchor
concordance, while the Rayleigh null, marginal anchor-discovery
correction, geographic dependence, and OWTRAD contrast delimit what
the result can mean.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:alt_explanations}

\textbf{(1) Founding-canon and inscription selection.}
UNESCO's founding canon prioritized famous monuments; famous monuments
cluster in well-known cultural centers; and well-known cultural
centers cluster along the Eurasian corridor. This selection pathway is
a leading non-intentional explanation for the elevated early A++ rate
reported in \S\ref{sec:resultstemporal}.

\textbf{(2) Geographic concentration.}
Null~A shows that the dome/stupa signal is embedded in the subset's
own longitude geography. This does not explain the signal away; it
locates the signal in the geography of the monuments under study.
Null~C then compares that subset with the broader UNESCO background
from the same longitude footprint and finds nominal residual
overrepresentation (expected about
\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeantwo{}--\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeanten{} A+
hits versus observed \NcircTierAp{}). The Monte Carlo unit sweep
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) is consistent with 3\textdegree{} without
assuming any historical unit.

\textbf{(3) Morphological recurrence.}
Hemispherical structures recur across cultures
\citep{aveni2001skywatchers,aveni2003archaeoastronomy}, but recurrence
by itself predicts no longitude specificity.

\textbf{(4) Random coincidence.}
The KDE-smoothed null is consistent with dome enrichment ($p = \simKDEP$~\simKDEPSig{});
permutation ($p = \simDomePermP$~\simDomePermPSig{}) and bootstrap
($p = \simDomeBootP$~\simDomeBootPSig{}) are marginal at the A+ level
but highly significant for the A++ sub-population
(permutation $p = \circAppPermP$~\pCircAppPermSig{};
bootstrap $p = \circAppBootP$~\pCircAppBootSig{}), subject to the
Null~A geographic-conditioning result above.
Tests~C and~D (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) are positive
anchor-specific checks within the selected frame, but they do not
convert the conditional result into global periodicity.

\subsection{Interpreting Modest Effect Sizes}
\label{sec:effect_sizes}

The observed enrichment ($\clusterEnrichAp\times$ full corpus,
$\circEnrichAp\times$ domes at A+, $\circEnrichApp\times$ domes at A++,
with the Null~A result locating much of the sub-population pattern in
dome-site geography) is internally consistent under several
sensitivity checks but modest in magnitude. The effect is sparse:
only a minority of sites fall near node neighborhoods. We interpret it
as structured overrepresentation within Eurasian monument geography,
not as strict observance of a 3\textdegree{} placement rule. Several
explanations are consistent with this pattern:

\textbf{(1) Founding-canon selection effects.} UNESCO's earliest
inscriptions emphasized famous monuments. Famous monuments are
disproportionately located in well-known cultural centers, and those
centers are concentrated along the Eurasian corridor. This mechanism
does not require intentional longitude placement and is given the same
evidentiary weight here as logistical or diffusion explanations.

\textbf{(2) Logistical convenience.} Trade routes and way-stations
naturally cluster at intervals determined by travel distance: one
day's journey ($\sim$30\,km at 30\textdegree{}N latitude) corresponds
to roughly $0.3$\textdegree{}, so a 3\textdegree{} grid represents
about ten such intervals and could capture higher-order staging posts
without coordinated placement.

\textbf{(3) Cultural diffusion with local variation.} Buddhist
architectural traditions spread along the Silk Roads with transmission
hubs at specific longitudes (Lumbini, the Borobudur node at
$\approx$110\textdegree{}E), while local adaptation introduces scatter
that flattens the effect size when averaged across the full corpus.

\textbf{(4) Selective preservation beyond the founding canon.} Later
inscription and preservation processes may continue to favor
culturally significant sites at trade junctions or political centers
that happen to align with the corridor, even as the World Heritage
List expands geographically.

These explanations remain speculative. The contribution of this paper
is to document the conditional sparse-node signal, the NPC
anchor-concordance result, and the geographically conditioned tests that
delimit its interpretation; determining any historical mechanism would
require independent lines of evidence beyond spatial statistics alone.

\subsection{Monument-Type Specificity and Related-Corpus Checks}
\label{sec:transmission}

\textbf{A type-specific, not corpus-level, result.}
The full-corpus Rayleigh test ($R = \fullRayleighR$,
$p = \fullRayleighPermP$, \fullRayleighPermPSig{}) confirms that UNESCO
longitudes show no global sinusoidal periodicity at 3\textdegree{}.
The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N=\npcNStupa{}$), by contrast,
shows independently significant Rayleigh phase concentration at this
period ($R=\circConcStupaR{}$, perm-$p=\circConcStupaP{}$,
\circConcStupaPSig{}). Watson's $U^2$ ($U^2=\circWatsonUSqUnescoStupa{}$,
perm-$p=\circWatsonPUnescoStupa{}$) and Kuiper tests
($V=\circKuiperVUnescoStupa{}$, perm-$p=\circKuiperPUnescoStupa{}$)
confirm that the two corpora have detectably different phase
distributions at $T=3^\circ$. The UNESCO dome and stupa keyword
subset shows elevated node-neighborhood clustering at the same
candidate period (\S\ref{sec:resultscircular}). Together these results
point to the same conclusion: the 3\textdegree{} phase concentration
belongs to hemispherical monument architecture, not to the UNESCO
heritage corpus as a whole. The finding is monument-type specific,
not corpus-level concordance.

This is a tighter and more defensible claim than whole-corpus
alignment. It says the signal is carried by the architectural type
and recovers in an independently curated corpus of the same type,
while the full heritage list --- which contains roads, landscapes,
and industrial sites as well as hemispherical structures --- shows
no global periodicity as expected.

\textbf{NPC anchor concordance.}
As reported in \S\ref{sec:results_npc}, monument corpus pairs recover
concordant 3\textdegree{} anchors in the NPC test, with consensus
grid lines within
\npcDomeStupaGridDeltaE\textdegree{}--\npcTwoGridDeltaE\textdegree{}
of 35\textdegree{}E. The non-nested two-corpus tests (Rows~1--2) do
not reach $p<0.05$ individually, consistent with the type-specific
framing. The three-corpus test reaches significance (Row~3) but
includes the nested dome subset. NPC is supplementary evidence for
anchor coherence; the Wikidata stupa concentration result is the
stronger individual statement.
UNESCO and Wikidata are independent in the curatorial sense relevant
here: externally curated databases not manually assembled to fit this
candidate frame. They are not geography-independent, and the UNESCO
dome subset is nested inside the UNESCO corpus. That limits causal or
universal interpretations, but it does not undercut the stated target:
Eurasian monument geography.

\textbf{OWTRAD as contrast corpus.}
OWTRAD Silk Road route nodes ($N=\npcNOwtrad{}$ unique nodes) do not
share the monument NPC anchor: their preferred anchor is
\npcOwAnchorDelta\textdegree{} from the monument consensus, and adding
OWTRAD reduces NPC consensus to $K=\npcAllK{}$
(perm-$p=\npcAllPermP{}$, ns). OWTRAD differs from the monument datasets in
function, construction history, and sampling.

The within-frame OWTRAD results below report whether OWTRAD nodes fall
near the selected UNESCO grid lines when that grid is imposed.
The OWTRAD attested-route network \citep{ciolek2004owtrad}

\subsection{The Mechanism Gap}
\label{sec:mechanism}

A literal reading of the within-frame organization---that builders
across Eurasia placed monuments with sub-degree longitudinal precision
relative to a common reference---would require pre-modern continental
longitude determination at a precision that is not a documented
historical capability. Babylonian astronomical methods support angular
measurement and eclipse timing
\citep{hunger2019mul,neugebauer1975history,robson2008mathematics} but
do not extend to terrestrial longitude across continental scales, and
no surveying instrument calibrated in ancient metrological sub-units
relevant to such a procedure has been recovered. We take this gap
seriously as a constraint on interpretation rather than as a
peripheral caveat: any historical reading of the reported regularity
must favor explanations that do not require coordinated placement.

This is why we frame the empirical contribution as conditional
sparse-node enrichment within a specified representation rather than
as evidence of metrology or coordinated design. The selected
3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E / dome representation interacts with the actual
geographic and cultural distribution of UNESCO sites---trade-route
spacing at characteristic travel intervals, geographic channeling by
river systems and passes, the concentration of inscribable cultural
heritage along the Eurasian corridor, and inscription selection
effects within UNESCO itself---to produce a set of within-frame
characterizations. That the selected-frame count excess is sharper
than chance under permutation and bootstrap nulls drawn from the same
representation is the result we report. Whether the pattern
ultimately reflects the geometry of Eurasian corridor geography under
a particular representational choice, some monument-specific
selection process, or another mechanism is not decided here, and we
explicitly do not propose a historical or causal mechanism.


\section{Conclusion}

This paper follows a two-stage observational workflow. The exploratory
stage identified domed and stupa-related UNESCO monument sites as showing
elevated Tier-A+ node-neighborhood clustering under a
3\textdegree{} / $\sim$35\textdegree{}E candidate longitude frame.
The validation stage applied the same frame, unmodified, to the
externally curated Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus --- assembled entirely
outside this study.

The primary finding is monument-type-specific phase concentration. The
Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N=\npcNStupa{}$) shows independently
significant Rayleigh phase concentration at $T=3^\circ$
($R=\circConcStupaR{}$, perm-$p=\circConcStupaP{}$,
\circConcStupaPSig{}). The full UNESCO Cultural/Mixed corpus shows no
global periodicity at this period ($R=\circConcUnescoR{}$,
perm-$p=\circConcUnescoP{}$, ns), consistent with the full-corpus
Rayleigh null. Watson's $U^2$ and Kuiper tests confirm that the two
corpora have detectably different phase distributions
($p=\circWatsonPUnescoStupa{}$ and $p=\circKuiperPUnescoStupa{}$
respectively). The UNESCO dome and stupa keyword subset shows elevated
node-neighborhood clustering at the same candidate period and nodes,
geographically embedded in the subset's own longitude distribution
(Null~A, $p=\geoNullDomeBootP$).

Taken together, the full UNESCO corpus shows no global periodicity at
3\textdegree{}, confirmed by Rayleigh, confirmed by distributional
difference from the stupa corpus, confirmed by non-significant
individual concentration. Within that globally non-periodic corpus,
dome and stupa related sites show elevated node-neighborhood
clustering. The Wikidata stupa corpus, assembled independently, shows
genuine phase concentration at the same candidate period and 
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E anchor. The finding is monument-type specific,
not corpus-level concordance: the signal belongs to the architectural
type, not to the heritage list it happens to appear on.

Table~\ref{tab:npc_summary} additionally reports NPC anchor
concordance across five monument corpus configurations. All monument
pairs recover high $K \geq \npcDomeStupaK{}$ with consensus grid
lines within \npcDomeStupaGridDeltaE\textdegree{}--\npcTwoGridDeltaE\textdegree{}
of 35\textdegree{}E. Non-nested two-corpus tests do not reach $p<0.05$
individually; the three-corpus test including the nested dome subset
does ($K=\npcMonK{}$, perm-$p=\npcMonPermP{}$). Negative controls
confirm specificity: replacing the dome subset with the Americas
sub-corpus collapses concordance to $K=\npcNegThreeK{}$ (ns), as does
including OWTRAD ($K=\npcAllK{}$, ns).

The effect is not whole-corpus alignment: most UNESCO sites do not
fall near candidate harmonic nodes, and the full-corpus Rayleigh
periodogram is null ($p=\fullRayleighPermP$).
The dome/stupa findings should be read as structure within Eurasian
monument geography, not as a pattern that must be independent of that
geography. Null~A ($p=\geoNullDomeBootP$) shows that the dome/stupa
signal is reproducible from the subset's own longitude distribution.
Null~C asks whether the broader UNESCO background from the same
longitude footprint produces comparable A+ enrichment; it expects
about \geoNullDomeRestrictedMeantwo{}--\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeanten{}
A+ hits across the tested windows rather than the observed
\NcircTierAp{}, with nominal per-window significance that does not
survive correction across three windows.

The result is therefore best read as sparse, structured
node-neighborhood enrichment within a specified 3\textdegree{} /
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E monument-corpus frame, with the additional finding
that the Wikidata stupa corpus independently concentrates at the same
period. It is supported by conditional diagnostics and monument-type
phase concentration, while being bounded by the null global
periodogram, marginal anchor-discovery correction, geographic
dependence, and OWTRAD non-portability. It is not evidence of
universal periodicity,
historical intentionality, whole-corpus alignment, metrology, or a
unique Gerizim origin. Further work should retain the same standards
used here: clear specification of the candidate frame, transparent
conditional tests, externally curated corpora where available,
negative controls, geographically conditioned null models, and
explicit scope statements about Eurasian monument geography. We make
all data, code, and methods freely available
(\S\ref{sec:data_code}; Zenodo DOI
\texttt{10.5281/zenodo.20123709}) so that those conditional claims can
be inspected and reanalyzed.
