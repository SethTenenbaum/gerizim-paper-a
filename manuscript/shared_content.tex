\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

This study applies a reproducible one-dimensional angular enrichment test to the
externally curated UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$).

Sites within $\leq\ApThreshBeru{}$~beru ($\sim\ApThreshKm{}$~km, see 
\S\ref{sec:tier_classification}) of a harmonic node are
designated Tier-A+; the geometric null rate is \NullRateAp{}.
The full corpus yields \NclusterAp{} A+ sites against an expected
\NclusterExpAp{} under this null, a statistically significant excess
that concentrates in identifiable sub-populations: domed monuments show
the strongest enrichment of any architectural class tested (Test~2),
and harmonics with a precisely aligned site are
$\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ more densely populated overall (Test~3).

A global anchor sweep reveals a 3\textdegree{} periodic structure in the
corpus longitude distribution consistent with the 0.1-beru unit; the full
treatment is in \S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global} and \S\ref{sec:limitations_circularity}.

\textbf{Why this design addresses key methodological criticisms.}
Prior metrological investigations were criticized for investigator-assembled
site lists \citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}.
Three features distinguish this study: (1)~the UNESCO site list was compiled
without reference to this hypothesis and is fixed; (2)~the 0.1-beru interval
equals 3~\textit{u\v{s}}, the finest integer multiple of the smallest
attested cuneiform subdivision (1~\textit{u\v{s}} = $\tfrac{1}{30}$~beru)
\citep{hunger2019mul,powell1987metrological}, limiting the parameter space; (3)~all sub-populations 
and results are fully computationally reproducible from public source 
data \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.

\textbf{Positioning within quantitative archaeometry.}
This analysis follows the tradition of probability-based spatial analysis \citep{magli2013,magli2016,ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.

\subsection{The Beru Unit}
\label{sec:beru}

The \textit{beru} (30\textdegree{} of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN
cuneiform compendium \citep{hunger2019mul,watson2011writing}, dated to the late
second or early first millennium~BCE. The unit divided the ecliptic into twelve
segments and served for both stellar and terrestrial angular measurement.
The terrestrial use is explicit in MUL.APIN~II~ii~43--iii~15. The sub-division
into 30~\textit{u\v{s}} (1~u\v{s}~$= 1$\textdegree{}) is well attested
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics}, and the 0.1-beru interval
(3\textdegree{}) tested here corresponds to exactly 3~\textit{u\v{s}}.

The beru also functioned as a unit of linear territorial description: royal
inscriptions record inter-regional distances in beru, \textit{kudurru}
(boundary stones) recording territorial extents were deposited in temples
as legally authoritative records \citep{robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological},
and the Babylonian \textit{Imago Mundi} marks distances beyond the Salt Sea
as beru multiples.
Meridian transit observation and eclipse timing, attested in the cuneiform
record \citep{hunger2019mul,neugebauer1975history}, are sufficient to
derive inter-site angular separations at 1\textdegree{} resolution;
the gnomon \citep{neugebauer1975history} provides an independent means
of establishing true meridian.

\subsection{Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice}

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular measures
across large geographic scales has a long and contested history.
Thom's ``megalithic yard'' \citep{thom1967,thom1971,thom1964}
stimulated statistical refinement \citep{kendall1974} and methodological
critique \citep{freeman1976}. Powell's treatment of Mesopotamian metrology
\citep{powell1987metrological} remains the standard reference. More recently,
Magli \citep{magli2013,magli2016} has applied Monte Carlo methods to
archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have advanced
probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims
\citep{ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.
The ICOMOS-IAU thematic study \citep{ruggles2010icomos} provides
institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage
properties.  Medieval mappamundi and qibla charts similarly employed sacred
meridians (Jerusalem, Mecca) as orienting references \citep{harvey1987mappamundi}.

\subsection{The Anchor: Mount Gerizim}
\label{sec:anchor}

Mount Gerizim (\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E) is the primary reference
anchor because of its documented \textit{tabbur ha-aretz} (``navel of the
earth'') role in Samaritan cosmographic tradition
\citep{purvis1968,macdonald1964,crown1989}, analogous to Babylon's meridian
in cuneiform astronomical records \citep{hunger2019mul}. At 881~m in the
central highlands, it would have served as a natural survey reference point.
No alternative Levantine anchors were tested.

Gerizim's tentative-list coordinate (ref.~5706, DMS \GerizimLonDMS{}) is
externally sourced and does not appear in the UNESCO inscribed corpus
(UNESCO World Heritage Centre, Tentative List ref.~5706, State of Palestine,
submitted 02~April~2012; archived at
\url{https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/}).
Jerusalem (35.2317\textdegree{}E), approximately \GerizimJerusalemKm~km east
of Gerizim, is an inscribed corpus member. It is self-excluded when used as
anchor and produces a similar A+ count (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}).
Both lie within the \textit{Levantine corridor} ($\sim$35\textdegree{}E,
Jordan Valley to Levant), and the enrichment belongs to this corridor,
not to the specific anchor within it.
Mecca ($p \approx \pMeccaAnchor$, \pMeccaAnchorSig{}), Mt.\ Meru ($p \approx \pMeruAnchor$, \pMeruAnchorSig{}),
and Mt.\ Kailash ($p \approx \pKailashAnchor$, \pKailashAnchorSig{}) do not approach significance.

\subsection{Corpus Construction}
\label{sec:corpus}

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset \citep{unescowhc2024xml} contains
$\NwhcTotal$ inscribed nominations. UNESCO designates each site
\textit{Cultural} (criteria i--vi), \textit{Natural} (criteria vii--x),
or \textit{Mixed}; sites with cultural heritage alongside natural values
receive Mixed and are included here.
Retaining Cultural and Mixed ($-\NexcNatural$ Natural-only) and
excluding $\NexcNoCoord$ entries lacking geocoordinates (both linear
route networks) yields $N = \NclusterTotal$ sites.

\subsection{Geodetic Framework}
\label{sec:geodetic}

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at \GerizimLon\textdegree{}E,
from Palestine's UNESCO tentative list (ref.~5706, 2012; see \S\ref{sec:anchor}).
Gerizim is not on the inscribed World Heritage List and therefore does not
appear in the standard corpus.
Jerusalem is an inscribed corpus member, scored as a regular site in
Tests~1--4 (A+ at 4.1~km under Gerizim).
For the anchor-comparison audit and both sensitivity sweeps
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}), Gerizim is added as a synthetic corpus
entry so that each anchor can self-exclude symmetrically: Gerizim removes
itself and tests against the $N = \NclusterTotal$ inscribed sites (including
Jerusalem); Jerusalem removes itself and tests against the same $N$ (now
including Gerizim).

For a site at longitude $\lambda$, the beru-distance is
$B = |\lambda - \GerizimLon| / 30$ and the beru-deviation from the
nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is
$\delta = |B - \operatorname{round}(B, 0.1)|$.
The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion.
Kilometer labels (e.g.\ $\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km for Tier-A+) are equatorial
approximations computed as $\delta \times 30 \times 111$~km/\textdegree{},
becoming smaller at higher latitudes; the angular criterion is consequently
conservative at high latitudes. No latitude correction is applied.

The geometric null for Tier-A+ is $2 \times \ApThreshBeru{} / 0.1 = \NullRateApPct{}\%$,
assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference, but
the primary tests are simulation-based null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null})
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian KDE produces
$\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$ expected A+ (${\sim}\NullRateApPct{}\%$), confirming the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.

\subsection{Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing}
\label{sec:metrology_basis}

The harmonic spacing tested is 0.1~beru (3\textdegree{}).
The \textit{u\v{s}} is the smallest subdivision of the beru in cuneiform
metrology (1~beru $=$ 30~\textit{u\v{s}}), and 0.1~beru $=$ 3~\textit{u\v{s}}
is its finest integer multiple attested in astronomical records
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
It yields a tractable test (\NharmonicsCoarse{} harmonics, \NullRateAp{} geometric null);
coarser spacings are uninformative and finer ones produce no signal
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}).

The 0.05‑beru spacing (1.5\textdegree{}) also reaches significance, but this 
is structurally expected because every 0.10‑beru (3\textdegree{}) node is also 
a node of the finer 0.05‑beru grid; hence 0.05‑beru is a finer sub‑sampling of 
the same periodicity, not an independent signal.


\subsection{Tier Classification}
\label{sec:tier_classification}

\textbf{Metrological basis for the Tier boundaries.}
Thureau-Dangin~\citep[p.~133]{thureau1921} derives the linear
distance-\textit{beru} (French ``la lieue'') from 180
\textit{aslu} (French ``la corde'') of 59.4\,m each, giving
$180 \times 59.4 = 10{,}692$\,m.
At 111\,km/°, 10,692\,m equals 0.00321\, angular-beru.
Two parasangs equal one distance-beru(\citealt[p.~467]{powell1987metrological}),
and one barid equals two distance-berus
(\citealt[vol.~2, p.~63, n.~2]{burton1893pilgrimage}),
giving $2 \times 0.00321 = 0.00642$~angular-beru.

The same longitude offset corresponds to a smaller ground distance at
higher latitudes. The km values are only approximate, while the
angular-beru criterion is the primary, latitude-independent measure.
No cuneiform text explicitly prescribes these exact tolerances, but they
are grounded in Thureau-Dangin’s metrological values.

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A++}] One equatorial parasang, $\delta \leq \AppThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\AppThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A+}] One equatorial Akkadian beru, $\delta \leq \ApThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$ (primary threshold).
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A}] One equatorial barid, $\delta \leq \ATierThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\ATierThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~B}] The middle zone, neither near a harmonic nor near an inter-harmonic midpoint.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~C}] The C-band is the symmetric mirror of the A-band,
    measured by $\delta_\mathrm{mid} = 0.050 - \delta$ (distance from the
    nearest inter-harmonic midpoint rather than from the nearest harmonic):
    $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq \ATierThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\ATierThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~C$-$}] $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq \CMinusThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~C$--$}] $\delta_\mathrm{mid} \leq \CMinusTwoThreshBeru{}$~beru
    ($\lesssim\AppThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$.
\end{description}

C-band tiers are defined here; a full inter-harmonic site listing is in
\texttt{supplementary/audit/interharmonic\_audit.txt}.

\subsection{Test Populations}
\label{sec:test_populations}

Keyword populations are approximate. The raw sweep tolerates known
false positives while the context-validated variant (Test~2x) applies
sentence-level gating for greater precision.
Both populations are reported.

\textbf{Test~1 (full corpus, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; binomial test of
whether the global A+ rate exceeds the \NullRateApPct{}\% geometric null.

\textbf{Test~2 (dome/spherical, primary).}
\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text ($N = \NcircTotal$).
This raw sweep is over-inclusive: it admits sites where the keyword
refers to geological or vernacular features rather than monumental
architecture. The context-validated population ($N = \NcircValidated$)
is reported alongside all primary results; it yields stronger
significance, consistent with the rejected sites being
non-architectural false positives.

\textbf{Test~2b (mound evolution, sensitivity variant).}
Extends Test~2 by adding earthen mound-stage keywords
(\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun}).
Not counted separately in the Bonferroni family.

\textbf{Test~3 (cluster asymmetry, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; tests whether
A+ harmonics host more total sites than non-A+ harmonics, independent
of any morphological keyword.

\textbf{Test~4 (temporal gradient, descriptive).}
Cochran-Armitage trend test on the full corpus based on year of
inscription into the UNESCO corpus, no keyword filter.

\textbf{Test~5 (founding classifier, post-hoc).}
A context-aware keyword classifier (\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py}), refined after initial data inspection, addresses what kinds of sites beyond domed monuments fall on harmonic nodes; it carries no weight in the primary evidential chain (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).

\subsection{Multiple-Comparisons Protocol}
\label{sec:bonferroni}

\textbf{Primary test family ($k = \BonfK$).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Test 1} (full corpus, $N = \NclusterTotal$):
    global enrichment above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 2} (domed/spherical monuments, $N = \NcircTotal$):
    morphological sub-population enriched above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 3} (cluster asymmetry, full corpus, no keyword filter):
    A+-bearing harmonics host more total sites.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Other tests.}
Test~4 (temporal gradient, Cochran-Armitage) is descriptive context;
a Mantel-Haenszel stratified test and logistic regression with region as a
covariate assess the gradient robustly (\S\ref{sec:resultstemporal});
it is not part of the primary family.
Test~2b is a sensitivity variant of Test~2.
Test~5 (founding classifier) is post-hoc (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
World religion sub-group analyses (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}) use Fisher exact tests and are not Bonferroni-corrected.
Tests~A--D (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) assess the 3\textdegree{} periodicity
structure: Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh) is a negative control structurally
insensitive to the sparse A+ sub-population; Tests~C and~D are positive tests;
Test~B is tautological by construction and excluded from both tables.

\subsection{Domed and Spherical Monuments (Test~2, Primary)}
\label{sec:resultscircular}

The keyword filter is described in \S\ref{sec:test_populations} ($N = \NcircTotal$).

\textbf{Tier-A++ concentration (key result).}
\NcircTierApp{} of \NcircTotal{} dome sites (\NcircAppRate\%, $\circEnrichApp\times$ the \NullRateApp{} null)
fall within Tier-A++ ($\delta \leq \AppThreshBeru{}$~beru, $\lesssim\AppThreshKm{}$~km).
This concentration is significant and reproducible across three test statistics:
Fisher OR~$= \circAppFisherOR$, $p = \pCircAppFisher$~\pCircAppFisherSig{};
permutation $Z = \circAppPermZ$, $p = \circAppPermP$~\pCircAppPermSig{};

(\NcircApRate\% [\circApCIlo, \circApCIhi], $\circEnrichAp\times$;

binomial $p = \pCircA$~\pCircASig{}).

\textbf{Geographic-concentration null (dedicated simulation).}
\label{sec:geo_conc_null}
Domed monuments are concentrated in the Eurasian corridor:
\domeEurasianFraction\% fall in 20\textdegree--120\textdegree{}E
versus \fullCorpusEurasianFraction\% of the full corpus.
Two targeted null models test whether this explains the dome enrichment
(code: \texttt{dome\_geographic\_concentration\_test.py}, \simNperms{} trials).

\textbf{Null~A (within-dome bootstrap):} Draw $N = \NcircTotal$ longitudes
from the dome sites' own list. Mean $= \geoNullDomeBootMean$ A+,
$p = \geoNullDomeBootP$ (\geoNullDomeBootPSig{}). The dome sites' positions are concentrated
near harmonic longitudes.

\textbf{Null~C (restricted geographic draw):} Pool all full-corpus sites
within $\pm W$\textdegree{} of any dome site and bootstrap the expected A+
count. Dome sites (\NcircTierAp~A+, \NcircApRate\%) significantly exceed
the broader footprint at all three windows tested:
$\pm 2$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPtwo$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPtwoSig{});
$\pm 5$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPSig{});
$\pm 10$\textdegree{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPten$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPtenSig{}).
Results are window-independent.

\textbf{Reconciliation.}
Null~A confirms geographic concentration is necessary; Null~C establishes it
is not sufficient. Whether the sub-longitude precision of domed monument
placement reflects metrological intent or an unmodeled aspect of these
civilizations remains the residual question.

\paragraph*{Methodological note: Rayleigh phase-lock (Test~B).}
\label{sec:results_rayleigh}
The near-perfect Rayleigh $R$ of the A+ sub-sample is a mathematical
consequence of the A+ classification criterion (\S\ref{sec:circularity})
and is reported as a structural description.
Three formal permutation tests (\NpermRayleigh{} shuffles each, seed~\simSeed{};
\texttt{x18\_periodicity\_formal\_test.py}) address distinct aspects of the
periodicity:

\textbf{Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh, negative control).}
The full-corpus Rayleigh $R = \fullRayleighR$
($Z = \fullRayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$, \fullRayleighPermPSig{})
confirms no spurious global 3\textdegree{} periodicity is present in the
raw longitude file. The signal is concentrated in A+ sites, not a
global artifact.

\textbf{Test~B (A+-only Rayleigh, structural).}
Among the \Nap{} Tier-A+ sites, $R = \rayleighR$
(\rayleighRPctPhrasing{} A+ sites share the same phase modulo
3\textdegree{}; $Z = \rayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} < \rayleighPermP$).
This confirms self-consistency of the A+ classification but is not
independent evidence, as A+ membership is defined by proximity to a
harmonic (\S\ref{sec:circularity}).

\textbf{Test~C (circular-shift anchor sensitivity).}
Displacing the anchor by a uniform random offset in $[0^\circ,
360^\circ)$ and recounting A+ sites yields a null mean of
\anchorShiftNullMu{} vs.\ the observed \anchorShiftApCount{}
($Z = \anchorShiftZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, \anchorShiftPermPSig{}).
This is a positive anchor-specific test complementary to Test~D.

\textbf{Test~D (targeted von~Mises mixture, full corpus).}
Test~A is an omnibus test over all $N = \NoriginCorpus$ sites; the
${\approx}\targetedBgPct\%$ near-uniform background dilutes the A+ phase
signal, making it \emph{structurally insensitive} to the small but
concentrated A+ sub-population.
Test~D addresses this directly.
We fit a von~Mises/uniform two-component mixture to all $N = \NoriginCorpus$
folded angles, with $\mu_0$ fixed at the Gerizim phase on the 3\textdegree{}
circle; $\hat{w}$ and $\hat{\kappa}$ are estimated by EM.
The likelihood-ratio statistic $D = 2(\hat{\ell} - \ell_0)$ is referred to
a parametric bootstrap ($B = \targetedB$ null samples) for an empirical $p$-value.
Fixing $\mu$ rather than searching it targets the test at the alternative of
interest, retaining sensitivity to cluster weights of a few percent.
On the full corpus: $D = \targetedDfull$,
$\hat{w} = \targetedWfull$ (i.e.\ ${\approx}\targetedWfullPct\%$ of all
sites), $\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKfull$
(angular SD $\approx \targetedSdFullDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPfull$ (\targetedPfullSig{}).
On the A+ subset alone: $D = \targetedDAp$, $\hat{w} = \targetedWAp$,
$\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKAp$ (angular SD $\approx \targetedSdApDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPAp$ (\targetedPApSig{}).
Together, Tests~B--D provide convergent, methodologically independent
evidence that the 3\textdegree{} periodicity at the Gerizim anchor is
a real structural feature of the UNESCO corpus and not a statistical
artifact.

\textbf{Test~E (anchor discovery correction).}
A global sweep of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors asks whether the
Levantine corridor ranks unusually among all possible anchors.
The result is marginal ($p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}).

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Unit Sensitivity Sweep}
\label{sec:metrology}

Varying harmonic spacing from 0.05 to 0.20~beru (physical threshold
fixed at \ApThreshKm{}~km), the 0.1-beru spacing produces the strongest joint
significance across both the dome and full-corpus populations.
The 0.05-beru and 0.20-beru spacings also reach significance, but both are
mathematical consequences of the 0.10-beru structure: every 0.10-beru
harmonic is also a 0.05-beru harmonic, and 0.20-beru harmonics are a strict
subset, so neither constitutes an independent signal.
Adjacent non-harmonic spacings (0.06, 0.07, 0.09, 0.11, 0.12~beru) are
non-significant in either population, which is the key discriminating observation.
The 0.08-beru spacing reaches nominal dome significance
($p = \sweepEightDomeP$) but not full-corpus ($p = \sweepEightFullP$, \sweepEightFullPSig{}),
reflecting unsystematic local overlap with the 0.10-beru grid rather than
a genuine secondary periodicity.
Specifically, \sweepEightTenDomeBoth{}/\sweepZeroEightDomeHits{} 
(\sweepEightTenDomePctOfEight\%) of 0.08‑beru dome hits align 
to 0.10‑beru, indicating the significance is due to overlap.

A degree-denominated Monte Carlo sweep
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) confirms this pattern. Candidate intervals
at 4\textdegree{} and 8\textdegree{} reach nominal dome significance, but
inspection shows that 6 of 7 and all 4 of their hits respectively fall on
12\textdegree{} and 24\textdegree{} nodes shared with the 3\textdegree{}
grid, confirming they are consequences of the primary periodicity rather
than independent angular units.

\subsection{Sensitivity Slope at the Canonical Unit}
\label{sec:sensitivity_slope_perm}

A fine sweep within $\pm 1\%$ of 0.10~beru (\texttt{fine\_sweep\_audit.py})
shows a $\pm 0.3\%$ shift collapses joint significance in at least one
population. A geographic bootstrap of the full cultural corpus finds no
permutation in \permSlopeSpecN{} trials that reproduces the canonical-unit
sharp peak at any precision tier ($p \permSlopeCanonBestJointFullAP{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigFullAP{}), confirming 0.10~beru as a specific
address. The dome-filter specificity test yields marginal results across all
tiers (A$^{++}$: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointAPP{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigAPP{}; A$^{+}$: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointAP{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigAP{}; A: $p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointA{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigA{}), consistent with the test being underpowered
at $n = \NcircValidated{}$ sites. Restricting to the \permSlopeKwN{}
morphologically unambiguous spherical forms (stupa, tholos) recovers
significance at the A tier ($p = \permSlopeCanonBestJointKwA{}$,
\permSlopeCanonBestJointSigKwA{}). The 3\textdegree{} periodicity is
independently confirmed by Tests~C and~D (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}).

A log-scale threshold sweep (\logSweepNtaus{} values,
\logSweepAregionNsig/\logSweepAregionNtaus{} significant within $\pm$1
log-decade of A+) yields data-optimal bands
A$^{++}{\approx}\logSweepOptPtau{}$~beru,
A$^{+}{\approx}\logSweepOptApBeru{}$~beru,
A${\approx}\logSweepOptABeru{}$~beru
(geometric ratio $r^{*}{=}\logSweepOptRatio{}$);
the canonical metrological values (\AppThreshBeru{}, \ApThreshBeru{}, \ATierThreshBeru{}~beru)
are the nearest Babylonian sub-unit boundaries to these optima.

\subsection{Global Anchor Sweep}
\label{sec:sensitivity_global}

A global sweep (0--360\textdegree{}E, 0.01\textdegree{} resolution,
\anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors) assesses whether the
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor is unusual globally.
The sweep uses the extended corpus described in \S\ref{sec:geodetic}
($N_\mathrm{ext} = 1012$): each trial anchor self-excludes any entry
within $0.001$\textdegree{}, so every focal anchor tests against
$N = \NgerizimSweepCorpus$.

Both anchors place the corridor in the global top \anchorSweepTopPct\%
(\anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors, 0--360\textdegree{}E;
Gerizim: \GerizimSweepAp{} A+, \anchorSweepPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile;
Jerusalem: \JerusalemSweepAp{} A+, \JerusalemSweepPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile;
full table in Online Supplementary Table~S4), and the result is anchor-invariant
within the \GerizimJerusalemKm\,km corridor window.
No other historical meridian reaches significance.

\subsubsection*{The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic structure}
\label{sec:x18artifact}

The most striking result of the global sweep is that every longitude of
the form $x.18$\textdegree{}E (all \NharmonicsCoarse{} such anchors) produces identically
the maximum A+ count. The spacing between consecutive $x.18$\textdegree{}E
anchors is exactly 3\textdegree{} = 0.1~beru.

The label ``$x.18$\textdegree{}E'' is a rounded shorthand for the phase
$\approx 2.18$\textdegree{} mod 3.0\textdegree{}.
Gerizim's phase ($\GerizimLon \bmod 3.0 = \GerizimPhase$\textdegree{},
gap $\phaseGap$\textdegree{} $\approx \phaseGapKm$~km) is within
coordinate uncertainty.

\textbf{Non-independence note.} \emph{The 3\textdegree{} periodicity and the unit
sensitivity sweep (\S\ref{sec:metrology}) are consistent manifestations of the
same underlying structure rather than independent lines of evidence; their
primary evidential role is as robustness checks for Tests~2 and~3
(\S\ref{sec:circularity}).}

\subsubsection*{Formal statistical tests for the $x.18$\textdegree{} periodicity}
\label{sec:x18_formal}

The full-corpus Rayleigh ($p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$, \fullRayleighPermPSig{}) confirms no
spurious global periodicity; Test~B is tautological.
Tests~C ($p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, \anchorShiftPermPSig{}) and~D ($p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPfull$, \targetedPfullSig{})
are positive anchor-specific tests; Test~E ($p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}, marginal) is the anchor discovery correction.
The unit sensitivity sweep (\S\ref{sec:metrology}) and Monte Carlo unit sweep
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) independently confirm 3\textdegree{} as the dominant
period without assuming any particular unit.

The dome-periodicity audit (\texttt{dome\_periodicity\_audit.py}) does not
distinguish alignment from geographic concentration.
Gerizim scores at the \domeGerizimPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile
among dome sites, dome site phases are approximately uniform
($\chi^2 = \domePhaseChiSq$, df\,=\,9, $p = \domePhaseChiP$, trend-level),
and all \NcircTierAp{} dome A+ sites have phases within
$\pm 0.15$\textdegree{} of the $x.18$\textdegree{} optimal phase.

\textbf{Corridor and landmark ranking.}
Of the \corridorUniqueGer{} A+ sites unique to Gerizim and \corridorUniqueJer{} unique to Jerusalem,
\corridorSharedAp{} are shared; the enrichment belongs to the corridor, not to either
anchor individually. Using each UNESCO site as its own anchor, Gerizim ranks
\#\GerizimTempleRank{} of 1012 (top 2.8\%).

\subsection{Spatial Autocorrelation}
\label{sec:autocorrelation}

A+ harmonics host $\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ as many total UNESCO
sites as non-A+ harmonics ($p\ \clusterHarmonicMWpStr$).
DEFF correction via ICC ($\pm 0.5$\textdegree{}, $\rho = \ICChalf$):
DEFF~$= \DEFFhalf$, $N_\mathrm{eff} = \NeffHalf$,
corrected $p = \pNeffHalf$.
Block bootstrap (3\textdegree{} blocks, \simNperms{} resamples):
95\% CI $[\blockBootCIlo\%, \blockBootCIhi\%]$ excludes the \NullRateAp{} null.
Within-group permutation using UNESCO-region $\times$ longitude bins
reproduced the observed A+ count exactly in every trial, confirming this
test cannot discriminate alignment from geographic concentration;
the Null~A and Null~C simulations (\S\ref{sec:geo_conc_null})
address that question directly.

\subsection{Null Model Validation}
\label{sec:simulation_null}

Three simulation nulls (\simNperms{} trials each, seed~$= \simSeed{}$,

Using the identical anchor and null, the corpus yields

Harmonic nodes with at least one Tier-A site carry $\wikiStupaClusterRatio\times$

Buddhist A/C enrichment in the UNESCO religion audit (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}).
The Java/Sumatra node (105\textdegree--115\textdegree{}E, near the 2.5-beru harmonic at 110.3\textdegree{}E)
concentrates \wikiJavaNodeATierN{} of \wikiJavaNodeN{} sites at Tier-A (\wikiJavaNodeATierRate\%, OR $= \wikiJavaNodeATierOR\times$, $p~\wikiJavaNodeATierP$~\wikiJavaNodeATierPSig{}),
rising to \wikiJavaTightATierN/\wikiJavaTightN{} (\wikiJavaTightATierRate\%, OR $= \wikiJavaTightATierOR\times$, $p~\wikiJavaTightATierP$~\wikiJavaTightATierPSig{}) in the 107\textdegree--112\textdegree{}E window,
where A+ is also elevated at \wikiJavaTightApRate\% (OR $= \wikiJavaTightApOR\times$, $p = \wikiJavaTightApP$~\wikiJavaTightApPSig{}).
The physical heartland, India/Nepal (70\textdegree--90\textdegree{}E) and Myanmar/Cambodia
(90\textdegree--105\textdegree{}E), is A-tier depleted (combined OR $= \wikiHeartlandATierOR\times$,
$p~\wikiHeartlandATierP$~\wikiHeartlandATierPSig{}, one-sided depletion test)
but strongly enriched in the inter-harmonic C-band:
Myanmar/Cambodia reaches \wikiMyanmarCbandRate\% (OR $= \wikiMyanmarCbandOR\times$, $p~\wikiMyanmarCbandP$~\wikiMyanmarCbandPSig{}) and the
combined heartland \wikiHeartlandCbandRate\% (OR $= \wikiHeartlandCbandOR\times$, $p~\wikiHeartlandCbandP$~\wikiHeartlandCbandPSig{}).
The anti-correlation is consistent with the A/C bimodal pattern observed in the religion audit (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}).
The non-heartland Wikidata stupas (outside 70\textdegree--110\textdegree{}E,
$N = \wikiNonHeartlandN{}$) show the same A-enriched, C-depleted profile as the UNESCO
domed monument corpus: \wikiNonHeartlandATierRate\% reach Tier-A (OR $= \wikiNonHeartlandATierOR\times$, $p~\wikiNonHeartlandATierP$~\wikiNonHeartlandATierPSig{})
with no C-band enrichment (OR~$= \wikiNonHeartlandCbandOR\times$, $p = \wikiNonHeartlandCbandP$~\wikiNonHeartlandCbandPSig{}), suggesting that UNESCO inscription preferentially
captures the most precisely aligned examples within each tradition.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment}
\label{sec:limitations_circularity}
\label{sec:circularity}

Pre-registration cannot address circularity for analyses of permanent
public databases. The UNESCO coordinate file and Mount Gerizim are both
fully public, so any investigator could run this pipeline at any time
and temporal priority is unverifiable. The relevant questions are whether
the pattern exists and whether the anchor was chosen on independent grounds.

Both are answered directly. The global anchor sweep (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global})
confirms that the enrichment is a property of the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E
corridor. Every $x.18$\textdegree{}E anchor produces the same result, and
Gerizim and Jerusalem both place the corridor in the global top
\anchorSweepTopPct\% of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors.
What distinguishes Gerizim
is pre-existing documentary evidence, the \textit{tabbur ha-aretz}
cosmographic role (\S\ref{sec:anchor}), that predates this analysis entirely.

The 3\textdegree{} periodic structure is recoverable by any unanchored
\NharmonicsCoarse-phase scan without reference to Gerizim. The dome
enrichment (Test~2) is identical across all \NharmonicsCoarse{}
$x.18$\textdegree{}E anchors. The cluster asymmetry (Test~3) requires
no keyword filter and no anchor. Test~B is tautological by construction
(\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) and is noted as such.

This ranking is marginal under the discovery correction (Test~E,
$p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}); the primary evidence is
Tests~2 and~3, which require no anchor.

\subsection{Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias}
\label{sec:limitations_geo}

The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the
Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor of Cultural/Mixed sites. 
The regional distribution is as follows: 
(Europe \& North America: \CorpusPctEurope\%;
Arab States: \CorpusPctArabStates\%;
Asia-Pacific: \CorpusPctAsiaPac\%;
Latin Americas: \CorpusPctLatAm\%;
Africa: \CorpusPctAfrica\%).
Permutation tests show geographic concentration can reproduce the
sensitivity peak (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_slope_perm}), but domed
monuments exceed the A+ rate of other monuments from the same longitude
footprint (Null~C, $p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPSig{}), establishing
that the enrichment is carried by a specific building type rather than
the geographic distribution alone. Although UNESCO nomination criteria
make no reference to longitude, it cannot be ruled out that the
inscription process carries some of the same longitudinal structure
observed here.

A Monte Carlo unit sweep (\mcNperms{} permutations, \mcNcandidates{} candidate
spacing values from 0.5\textdegree{} to 15\textdegree{}) identifies which
angular interval produces clustering beyond geographic concentration without
assuming any particular unit. The dominant peak is at 3\textdegree{} ($p < 0.001$),
with harmonics at 6\textdegree{} and 12\textdegree{} also significant.
Collapsing harmonics, the dome subset yields \mcDomeNsigCollapsed{} independent
dominant spacings ($P = \mcDomeCollapsedPEqOne{}$ under the geographic null),
supporting 3\textdegree{} as the period to which domed monuments are preferentially aligned.

\subsection{Americas Negative Control}
\label{sec:americas_negative_control}
The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus ($N = \AmericasN$) shows a 
modestly lower A+ rate (\AmericasApRate\%) than the
\NullRateAp{} null. The one-sided binomial depletion test 
gives $p = \AmericasOneSidedP$~\AmericasOneSidedStar{}, but 
the result is suggestive rather than definitive.

\subsection{Other Limitations}

\textbf{Geocoding precision.}
UNESCO XML coordinates are administrative centroids, introducing
unsystematic noise but not directional bias.

\textbf{Metrological evidence.}
No physical surveying instrument calibrated in beru subdivisions has been 
recovered; documentary sources attest the beru 
system \citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
This study therefore treats the coordinate alignment of Mount Gerizim as
a novel empirical observation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:alt_explanations}

\textbf{(1) Independent convergence.}
Hemispherical structures are a recurring architectural solution across cultures \citep{aveni2001skywatchers,aveni2003archaeoastronomy},
but independent convergence predicts no longitude specificity.

\textbf{(2) Geographic concentration.}
Null~C directly tests this: other monuments from the same longitude footprint
are significantly less enriched ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPSig{}),
establishing that the signal is carried by architectural type, not location.
The Monte Carlo unit sweep (\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) independently
confirms 3\textdegree{} as the dominant angular period without assuming
any unit.

\textbf{(3) Random coincidence.}
The KDE-smoothed null confirms dome enrichment ($p = \simKDEP$~\simKDEPSig{});
permutation ($p = \simDomePermP$~\simDomePermPSig{}) and bootstrap
($p = \simDomeBootP$~\simDomeBootPSig{}) are marginal at the A+ level
but highly significant for the A++ sub-population
(permutation $p = \circAppPermP$~\pCircAppPermSig{};
bootstrap $p = \circAppBootP$~\pCircAppBootSig{}).
Tests~C and~D (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) confirm anchor specificity and periodicity structure.

\subsection{Architectural Stratification}
\label{sec:transmission}

The corpus-wide A+ enrichment (Test~1) is not uniform across architectural
types. Among keyword-defined sub-populations, domed and spherical
monuments show the strongest alignment: \NcircTierAp/\NcircTotal{} reach
Tier-A+ ($\circEnrichAp\times$ the null, Bonferroni $p_\mathrm{adj} =
\pAdjTestTwo$~\pAdjTestTwoSig{}), and the Tier-A++ concentration
(\NcircTierApp/\NcircTotal{}, $\circEnrichApp\times$) survives full Bonferroni
correction at $k = \NtotalTests$ (\S\ref{sec:multiple_comparisons}).
Within the dome-evolution corpus, the stupa sub-stage achieves an A++ rate
of \evoStupaAppRate\% (\NevoStupaApp/\NevoStupa{} sites,
OR~$= \evoStupaAppFisherOR\times$, $p = \pEvoStupaAppFisher$~\pEvoStupaAppFisherSig{}),
the highest of any keyword-defined sub-population and more than five times
the geometric null.
The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus (\S\ref{sec:stupas_q180987}) reproduces
dome-level A-tier enrichment on an independently curated dataset,
confirming that the pattern is not an artifact of UNESCO curation.

The religion audit (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}) shows that both Buddhist and
Hindu sites display an A/C bimodal structure. Sites in each tradition
concentrate either near harmonic nodes (A-tier) or near inter-harmonic
midpoints (C-band), with relatively few in the intermediate B-zone.
Buddhist sites show a joint A-tier/C-band signal
(multivariate hypergeometric $p = \pBudJoint$~\pBudJointSig{}); Hindu sites show a significant A+/C-
joint probability (multivariate hypergeometric $p = \pHinApCminus$~\pHinApCminusSig{}).
This bimodal is not observed in other religion sub-groups and is consistent
with the 0.1-beru grid producing distinct attractor positions rather than
a continuous distribution.
The cluster asymmetry (Test~3) holds across the full corpus with no
architectural keyword filter, indicating the longitude structure is a
property of the corpus as a whole, not only of the dome sub-population
(Figure~\ref{fig:geo_trail}).

The mechanism underlying this stratification is beyond the scope of this analysis.

\subsection{Conclusion}

The UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus shows significant enrichment at
harmonics of the Babylonian beru anchored at Gerizim, with all three
primary tests surviving Bonferroni correction at $k = \BonfK$.
Longitude bands containing a precisely aligned monument are
$\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ more densely populated with other heritage
sites than bands without one, indicating that the most celebrated monuments
and the broader heritage record share the same longitudinal structure.
Among architectural sub-populations, spherically shaped monuments show
the strongest alignment of any class tested, exceeding what geography alone
predicts.

The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus reproduces the same pattern independently
(\S\ref{sec:stupas_q180987}).

Geographically, Mount Gerizim lies between the Mediterranean to the west and
the overland Silk Road \citep{hansen2012silkroad} to the east, placing it
at a natural node of the corridors through which architectural canons
circulated in antiquity (Figure~\ref{fig:geo_trail}).

\section{Keyword Audit Summary}
\label{sec:kwdaudit}

Full site-by-site audit files are in the archived code repository

\subsection{Dome/Spherical Monument Filter (Tests~2, 2b, 2x, 2bx)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_dome}

\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text. All \NcircTotal{} matching
sites are included in Test~2 without context gating.

For Test~2x, ambiguous keywords (\textit{dome, domed, domes, spherical})
are subject to a two-gate context check: Gate~1 (\NdomeGateNeg{} negative
patterns) rejects non-architectural uses; Gate~2 (\NdomeGatePos{} positive
patterns) requires architectural confirmation. Unambiguous keywords
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}) are accepted without validation.
The seven
rejected sites are: Hiroshima Peace Memorial, Richtersveld, Gyeongju,
Vi\~{n}ales Valley, Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, Uluru-Kata Tju\d{t}a,
and Diqu\'{i}s Stone Spheres.

\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{Test~2b} adds earthen mound-stage
keywords (\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun} unambiguous;
\textit{mound} context-validated) to trace the hemispherical tradition
from burial mound through stupa to architectural dome. Test~2bx applies
the same architectural context gates to the extended population.
Statistics for all four variants are reported in
\hyperref[sec:resultscircular]{§\ref*{sec:resultscircular}} and
\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{§\ref*{sec:resultsevolution_inline}}.

\subsection{Founding-Origin Classifier (Test~5, Post-Hoc)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_founding}

\textbf{Results.} The classifier finds \foundKwApRate\% of A+ sites
carry a founding keyword versus \foundKwBaseRate\% in the full corpus
(Fisher OR~$= \foundKwFisherOR$, $p = \pFoundKwFisher$~\pFoundKwFisherSig{}).
The A++ sub-population (\NfoundKwTotalApp{} sites) shows a stronger
signal: \foundKwAppRate\% carry a founding keyword
(OR~$= \foundKwAppFisherOR$, $p = \pFoundKwAppFisher$~\pFoundKwAppFisherSig{}),
consistent with the most precisely aligned sites being disproportionately
historically foundational monuments.
This carries no weight in the primary evidential chain as the classifier
was iteratively tuned after data inspection.

The classifier assigns sites to five founding-related categories; full
keyword definitions and the site-by-site listing are in the supplementary
archive \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.
