\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The full-corpus Rayleigh statistic for 3\textdegree{} periodicity in
raw UNESCO longitudes is null ($p = \fullRayleighPermP$), and the
global anchor-discovery sweep is marginal ($p = \anchorMaxPermP$).
This study therefore does not begin from an invariant periodicity
claim. It reports a selected-frame analysis of the externally curated
UNESCO Cultural and Mixed World Heritage corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$)
under a fixed 3\textdegree{} harmonic-deviation representation
referenced to the Levantine $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor, for which
Mount Gerizim (\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E; see \S\ref{sec:anchor}) is
one historically motivated candidate point rather than a uniquely
identified anchor. The contribution is representational rather than
invariant: the chosen frame yields one primary Tier-A+ count excess in
the UNESCO corpus, characterized through several diagnostic angles,
with a related 3\textdegree{} pattern in two Eurasian heritage corpora
that share the corpus's geography.

The frame was selected in contact with the data (3\textdegree{}
spacing, Gerizim anchor, dome keyword set, von~Mises tier widths) and
then held fixed across the analyses reported here; the open question
is whether this representation organizes the data in a way that random
permutations of the same representation do not, not whether the data
exhibit invariant 3\textdegree{} periodicity in raw longitude.

\textbf{Boundary conditions vs.\ within-frame diagnostics.}
We distinguish two classes of test, and we treat them as having
different evidentiary roles. Phase-invariant tests (the full-corpus
Rayleigh statistic for 3\textdegree{} periodicity, the global
anchor-discovery sweep) ask whether the corpus exhibits a global
3\textdegree{} periodicity that survives marginalization over phase
and anchor. These tests are negative or marginal here
($p = \fullRayleighPermP$ and $p = \anchorMaxPermP$, respectively;
\S\ref{sec:results_boundary}), and we report them as
\textit{boundary conditions}: they delimit what we do not claim. We
do not claim invariant global periodicity, and we do not claim that
the Levantine corridor is statistically privileged in a phase-free
sense. Conditional, within-frame diagnostics ask whether, given the
fixed selected representation, the corpus is unusually organized
relative to permutation and bootstrap nulls drawn from the same
representation. These diagnostics include Tier-A+ harmonic-node
enrichment, harmonic-band cluster asymmetry, the phase-rotation null
evaluated within the selected 3\textdegree{} circle, the unit-sweep
contrast against adjacent non-harmonic spacings, the dome/stupa
sub-population analysis, leave-$n$-out and multi-scale threshold
checks, and portability checks in the Wikidata Q180987 and OWTRAD
corpora. Several of these views are mathematically related rather than
independent confirmations: the cluster-asymmetry test partly
characterizes how the A+ count is distributed across harmonic bands,
the phase-rotation percentile locates the selected phase within the
same count envelope, and the dome A++ result must be read with the
Null~A caveat that dome-site geography can account for much of the
pattern. The positive result is therefore a count excess under the
chosen frame and its behavior under these characterizations, not a set
of independent proofs of the same effect.

This is a different kind of claim from invariant periodicity. The
selected frame is anchor-specific and unit-specific by construction,
and we treat that specificity as part of what is being characterized,
not as something to be argued away. Whether the within-frame
organization reflects historical intentionality, geographic and
cultural channeling along Eurasian corridors, inscription selection
effects, or some combination of these is not decided by the present
analysis; we discuss candidate explanations in
\S\ref{sec:alt_explanations} and \S\ref{sec:mechanism} without
endorsing any.

\textbf{Site-level structure under the frame.}
Sites within $\leq\ApThreshKm{}$~km of a 3\textdegree{}-grid node are
designated Tier-A+ ($P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$); the full corpus yields
\NclusterAp{} A+ sites against \NclusterExpAp{} expected. The excess
is sharpened in domed monuments (\S\ref{sec:resultscircular}),
although Null~A is consistent with that sub-population pattern being a
consequence of where dome sites already are, and it is concentrated in
harmonic bands containing a precisely aligned site
(\S\ref{sec:resultscluster}; $\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ denser).
A global anchor sweep shows that the within-frame enrichment is
stable across the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}). Tier boundaries are set from the
angular dispersion of an independently curated but geographically
related stupa corpus (Wikidata Q180987,
$N = \vmNStupa{}$; $\hat{\sigma} = \vmSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$),
fixing the $0.25\hat{\sigma}$/$0.50\hat{\sigma}$/$1.00\hat{\sigma}$
tier widths without reference to UNESCO data. Because UNESCO cultural
sites, Wikidata stupas, and OWTRAD route nodes are all concentrated in
the Eurasian corridor, cross-corpus agreement at 3\textdegree{} is
reported as an empirical observation for further testing rather than
as geography-independent replication.

\textbf{Methodological safeguards.}
Prior longitude-alignment studies were criticised for
investigator-assembled site lists
\citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}.
This study differs: (1)~the UNESCO corpus is externally curated and
fixed; (2)~the 3\textdegree{} period was identified through iterative
examination of the corpus and subsequently characterized by a unit
sweep (\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo})---these are not equivalent to a
parameter-free discovery, and we do not present them as such;
(3)~analytical choices are fixed at the level of the deposited
codebase for reanalysis, and all results can be regenerated from the public
specification \citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.

\textbf{Exploratory status.}
Because all analytical choices were informed by iterative examination
of the UNESCO corpus, this study reports an exploratory empirical
regularity rather than a hypothesis-confirming test. The relevant
scientific questions are: (1)~Is the within-frame organization
internally consistent under sensitivity checks? (2)~Was the reference
phase chosen on independent grounds, and is anchor-specificity itself
a coherent feature of the selected representation? (3)~What additional
evidence would constitute external validation? These questions are
addressed in \S\ref{sec:circularity} and \S\ref{sec:limitations_geo};
we return to the third in the Conclusion.

\subsection{Prior Work on Spatial Periodicity and Sacred Landscapes}

Statistical evaluation of spatial periodicity in monument distributions has a
long and contested history.
Thom's ``megalithic yard'' \citep{thom1967,thom1971,thom1964}
stimulated statistical refinement \citep{kendall1974} and methodological
critique \citep{freeman1976}.
Magli \citep{magli2013,magli2016} has applied Monte Carlo methods to
archaeoastronomical alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have advanced
probability-based frameworks for evaluating orientation claims
\citep{ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.
The ICOMOS-IAU thematic study \citep{ruggles2010icomos} provides
institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage
properties.

\subsection{Reference Phase and Candidate Anchors}
\label{sec:anchor}

The harmonic deviations in this study are computed from Mount Gerizim
(\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E) as the \textit{a priori} reference phase.
Gerizim is chosen because of its independently documented
\textit{tabbur ha-aretz} (``navel of the earth'') cosmographic role in
Samaritan tradition \citep{purvis1968,macdonald1964,crown1989}, a
justification that predates the analysis entirely.

\textbf{Caveat on anchor identification.}
The statistical analysis cannot uniquely identify which physical location,
if any, historically defined the grid origin. The von~Mises mixture model
(\S\ref{sec:tier_classification}) recovers a phase offset relative to any
fixed reference frame, but the fitted phase is an estimate subject to
sampling uncertainty. Any UNESCO site within the A+ tier
($\lesssim$\ApThreshKm{}~km) of the Gerizim-anchored grid could serve as
an alternative reference point and reconstruct an essentially identical
periodicity. The primary finding is therefore the existence of the
3\textdegree{} periodic structure within the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E
corridor; Mount Gerizim is presented as a historically motivated
candidate anchor based on independent textual evidence
(\textit{tabbur ha-aretz} cosmographic tradition;
\citealt{purvis1968}), not as a conclusion drawn from the statistical
analysis. A site-as-anchor ranking audit (Supplementary Audit S6)
is consistent with this: the top-ranked competing anchors, including the Silk Roads:
Zarafshan-Karakum Corridor (62.21\textdegree{}E, deviation
0.061\textdegree{} from Gerizim harmonic $n=9$), the Birthplace of Jesus
(35.21\textdegree{}E), and Old City of Sana'a (44.21\textdegree{}E), all
lie within the A+ tier of the Gerizim grid and are therefore co-grid
points rather than independent alternatives.

A global anchor sweep (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}) shows the result is
not sensitive to the exact coordinate: every longitude in the
\xbandLabel\textdegree{}E band produces the same enrichment, and both Gerizim
and Jerusalem (\JerusalemLon\textdegree{}E, $\approx$\GerizimJerusalemKm~km south)
place the corridor in the global top \anchorSweepTopPct\% of \anchorSweepNanchors{}
trial anchors. Mecca ($p \approx \pMeccaAnchor$, \pMeccaAnchorSig{}),
Mt.\ Meru ($p \approx \pMeruAnchor$, \pMeruAnchorSig{}), and
Mt.\ Kailash ($p \approx \pKailashAnchor$, \pKailashAnchorSig{}) yield
no significant enrichment, consistent with phase specificity,
even if the phase origin cannot be uniquely attributed.

Gerizim's coordinate is from the UNESCO tentative list (ref.~5706, DMS
\GerizimLonDMS{}, State of Palestine, 2012;
\url{https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/}) and is not inscribed.
Jerusalem is an inscribed corpus member, self-excluded when used as anchor.

\section{Data and Methods}

\subsection{Corpus Construction}
\label{sec:corpus}

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset \citep{unescowhc2024xml} contains
$\NwhcTotal$ inscribed nominations. UNESCO designates each site
\textit{Cultural} (criteria i--vi), \textit{Natural} (criteria vii--x),
or \textit{Mixed}; sites with cultural heritage alongside natural values
receive Mixed and are included here.
Retaining Cultural and Mixed ($-\NexcNatural$ Natural-only) and
excluding $\NexcNoCoord$ entries lacking geocoordinates (both linear
route networks) yields $N = \NclusterTotal$ sites.

\subsection{Geodetic Framework}
\label{sec:geodetic}

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at \GerizimLon\textdegree{}E
(see \S\ref{sec:anchor}). Gerizim is not inscribed and does not appear
in the standard corpus. Jerusalem is inscribed and scored as a regular
site in Tests~1--4 (A+ at 4.1~km under Gerizim).
For sensitivity sweeps (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}), Gerizim is added
as a synthetic entry so each anchor self-excludes symmetrically.

For a site at longitude $\lambda$, the deviation from the nearest 3\textdegree{} harmonic is
$\delta = |\lambda - \GerizimLon| \bmod 3\textdegree{}$ (folded to $[0, 1.5\textdegree{}]$).
The tier classification is a fixed angular criterion.
Kilometer labels (e.g.\ $\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km for Tier-A+) are equatorial
approximations computed as $\delta \times 111$~km/\textdegree{},
becoming smaller at higher latitudes; the angular criterion is consequently
conservative at high latitudes. No latitude correction is applied.

The geometric null for Tier-A+ is $2 \times \ApThreshDeg{}\textdegree{} / 1.5\textdegree{} = \NullRateApPct{}\%$,
assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference, but
the primary tests are simulation-based null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null})
that preserve the empirical longitude distribution. A Gaussian KDE produces
$\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$ expected A+ (${\sim}\NullRateApPct{}\%$), indicating the
geometric null is not materially inflated by longitude clumping.


\subsection{Tier Classification}
\label{sec:tier_classification}

Tier boundaries are derived from the cross-corpus von~Mises dispersion scale
$\hat{\sigma} = \vmSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (stupa corpus, $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaStupa{}$;
see ``Von Mises cross-corpus characterisation'' below).
The three A-tiers correspond to $0.25\hat{\sigma}$, $0.50\hat{\sigma}$, and
$1.00\hat{\sigma}$ respectively.

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A++}] $0.25\hat{\sigma}$;\quad
    $\delta \leq \AppThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\AppThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateApp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A+}] $0.50\hat{\sigma}$ (primary threshold);\quad
    $\delta \leq \ApThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\ApThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateAp{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~A}] $1.00\hat{\sigma}$;\quad
    $\delta \leq \ATierThreshDeg{}\textdegree{}$ ($\lesssim\ATierThreshKm{}$~km); $P_0 = \NullRateA{}$.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~B}] Middle zone, not near any harmonic or inter-harmonic midpoint.
  \item[\textbf{Tier~C/C$-$/C$--$}] Symmetric mirror of A/A+/A++ measured from the
    nearest inter-harmonic midpoint.
\end{description}

\textbf{Von Mises cross-corpus characterisation.}
We fitted $f(\theta \mid \kappa) \propto e^{\kappa\cos\theta}$ (von~Mises + uniform
mixture) to the harmonic phase deviations of two external corpora:
OWTRAD Silk Road nodes ($N = \vmNOwtrad{}$; $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaOwtrad{}$) and
Wikidata Q180987 stupas ($N = \vmNStupa{}$; $\hat{\kappa} = \vmKappaStupa{}$).
The stupa corpus yields $\hat{\sigma} = \kappa^{-1/2} = \vmSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$;
$0.25\hat{\sigma} = \vmHalfSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A++),
$0.50\hat{\sigma} = \vmOneSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A+),
and $1.00\hat{\sigma} = \vmTwoSigmaDegStupa{}\textdegree{}$ (A)
match the tier thresholds to within rounding (Table~\ref{tab:vonmises_tiers}).
The $N$-weighted pooled $\hat{\sigma}$ is $\vmSigmaDegPooled{}\textdegree{}$.
Both external corpora are concentrated in the Eurasian corridor, so
their use here fixes a cross-corpus angular scale within the same
geographic domain rather than providing geography-independent
validation.

\subsection{Test Populations}
\label{sec:test_populations}

Keyword populations are approximate. The raw sweep tolerates known
false positives while the context-validated variant (Test~2x) applies
sentence-level gating for greater precision.
Both populations are reported.

\textbf{Test~1 (full corpus, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; binomial test of
whether the global A+ rate exceeds the \NullRateApPct{}\% geometric null.

\textbf{Test~2 (dome/spherical, primary).}
\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text ($N = \NcircTotal$).
This raw sweep is over-inclusive: it admits sites where the keyword
refers to geological or vernacular features rather than monumental
architecture. The context-validated population ($N = \NcircValidated$)
is reported alongside all primary results; it yields stronger
significance, consistent with the rejected sites being
non-architectural false positives.

\textbf{Test~2b (mound evolution, sensitivity variant).}
Extends Test~2 by adding earthen mound-stage keywords
(\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun}).
Not counted separately in the Bonferroni family.

\textbf{Test~3 (cluster asymmetry, primary).}
Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter; tests whether
A+ harmonics host more total sites than non-A+ harmonics, independent
of any morphological keyword.

\textbf{Test~4 (temporal gradient, descriptive).}
Cochran-Armitage trend test on the full corpus based on year of
inscription into the UNESCO corpus, no keyword filter.

\textbf{Test~5 (founding classifier, post-hoc).}
A post-hoc keyword classifier identifies site types beyond domed monuments that fall on harmonic nodes; it carries no weight in the primary evidential chain (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).

\subsection{Multiple-Comparisons Protocol}
\label{sec:bonferroni}

\textbf{Full reported family ($k = \NtotalTests$).}
The multiple-comparisons headline treats the \NtotalTests{} tests in the
FDR audit as the correction family. Six results survive Bonferroni at
$k = \NtotalTests$: dome/spherical A++ (binomial
$p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepCircApp$, with the Null~A
geographic caveat discussed in \S\ref{sec:geo_conc_null}), full-corpus A+
($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterApBinom$), cluster permutation
($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterPermP$), harmonic
Mann-Whitney ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurviveclusterHarmonicMWp$),
sequential-peak permutation ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepeakSigP$),
and evolution combined A++ (binomial
$p_\mathrm{adj} = \BonfAllSurvivepEvoApp$).

\textbf{Non-primary reduced correction ($k = \BonfK$, Tests~1--3 only).}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Test 1} (full corpus, $N = \NclusterTotal$):
    global enrichment above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 2} (domed/spherical monuments, $N = \NcircTotal$):
    morphological sub-population enriched above \NullRateAp{} null.
  \item \textbf{Test 3} (cluster asymmetry, full corpus, no keyword filter):
    A+-bearing harmonics host more total sites.
\end{itemize}
The $k = \BonfK$ family was not pre-specified and is reported only as a
sensitivity analysis. The primary multiple-comparisons report is the
full $k = \NtotalTests$ audit above.

\textbf{Other tests.}
Test~4 (temporal gradient, Cochran-Armitage) is descriptive context;
a Mantel-Haenszel stratified test and logistic regression with region as a
covariate assess the gradient robustly (\S\ref{sec:resultstemporal});
it is not used as a Bonferroni-survivor claim.
Test~2b is a sensitivity variant of Test~2.
Test~5 (founding classifier) is post-hoc (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
World religion sub-group analyses (\S\ref{sec:resultsrelig}) use Fisher exact tests and are not Bonferroni-corrected.
Tests~A, C, D, and E (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) assess the
3\textdegree{} periodicity structure: Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh) is a
negative control structurally insensitive to the sparse A+ sub-population;
Tests~C and~D are conditional anchor-specific tests; Test~E is the anchor-discovery
correction. Test~B is tautological by construction and excluded from both
tables.

\section{Results}

\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Boundary Conditions: Invariant Tests Delimit What We Do Not Claim}
\label{sec:results_phase_invariant}
\label{sec:results_boundary}

Three phase-invariant checks delimit the scope of the present claim
before the within-frame diagnostics are reported. They tell us what
the result is \textit{not}: it is not an invariant global
3\textdegree{} periodicity in raw longitude, and it is not a phase
ranking that survives a full anchor-discovery correction. We treat
these results as boundary conditions on the interpretation, not as a
verdict on the within-frame organization characterized in
\S\ref{sec:results_global}--\S\ref{sec:resultscluster}.

(i)~The full-corpus Rayleigh test returns null
($p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$~\fullRayleighPermPSig{}). The
raw longitude distribution does not exhibit smooth sinusoidal
periodicity at 3\textdegree{}. Lattice-node proximity (the present
target) and spectral periodicity (what Rayleigh tests) are distinct
mathematical objects; a clumpy, anchor-specific concentration of mass
near a small subset of harmonic nodes can coexist with a null
Rayleigh statistic, and we interpret the conjunction in those terms
rather than as a contradiction.

(ii)~A phase-envelope scan over all origins
$\phi \in [0,3\textdegree{})$ (\phaseNphases{} phases at \phaseStep\textdegree{}
resolution) records strong phase localization within the selected
3\textdegree{} circle: full-corpus A+ counts span
\phaseFullMin{}--\phaseFullMax{} (median \phaseFullMedian{}), and
Gerizim's phase ($\phaseGerizimPhase\textdegree{}$ mod 3\textdegree{})
sits at the \phaseFullGerPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile
(\phaseFullGerCount{} A+), the
\phaseDomeGerPctile\textsuperscript{th} for domes
(\phaseDomeGerCount/\NcircTotal{}; range
\phaseDomeMin{}--\phaseDomeMax{}). Within the selected representation,
the chosen phase is unusually good; this is a within-frame
diagnostic, not an invariant claim.

(iii)~A degree-denominated Monte Carlo unit sweep identifies
3\textdegree{} as the dominant period (\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo});
adjacent non-harmonic units are null
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}). The unit sweep does not require an anchor
and is reported here as the unit-side complement to the phase scan:
together they indicate that 3\textdegree{} is the spacing at which
the within-frame count excess concentrates and that the selected
phase is the phase at which the concentration is sharpest, while the
anchor-discovery correction across all anchors is marginal
(Test~E, $p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{};
\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}).

(iv)~A Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) test asks a qualitatively
different question: not whether any single corpus is globally
periodic, but whether \textit{independently assembled} monument
databases spontaneously converge on the same 3\textdegree{} anchor.
Three monument corpora---UNESCO Cultural/Mixed ($N = \npcNUnesco{}$),
the UNESCO domed-monument subset ($N = \npcNDome{}$), and the
Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$)---yield a
near-perfect NPC consensus ($K = \npcMonK{}$, perm-$p = \npcMonPermP$*),
with a joint grid line falling \npcMonGridDeltaE\textdegree{} from 35\textdegree{}E
($\phi = \npcMonAnchor\textdegree{}$).
The Rayleigh null and the NPC positive result are therefore
\textit{compatible}: Rayleigh tests for smooth sinusoidal periodicity
across the global distribution, which is diluted by the $\sim$60\%
of sites outside the Eurasian corridor; NPC tests whether the
\textit{anchor itself} is a reproducible property of monument
corpora, irrespective of the magnitude of each corpus's own signal.
The NPC result, not the Rayleigh result, is the appropriate test for
cross-corpus recoverability of the anchor; it is reported in full in
\S\ref{sec:transmission}.

The remainder of the Results characterizes the corpus
\textit{conditional on} this representation: harmonic-node enrichment
in the full corpus (\S\ref{sec:results_global}), morphological
sub-population sharpening (\S\ref{sec:resultscircular}), and
cluster-asymmetry behavior at the harmonic level
(\S\ref{sec:resultscluster}). These sections characterize the selected
frame's count excess and its distributional consequences; they are not
presented as phase- or anchor-marginalized confirmation.

\subsubsection*{World religion sites}
\label{sec:resultsrelig}
Religion sub-populations are defined by keyword sets documented in the supplementary audit archive~\citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.
A chi-squared test finds no significant A+ rate difference across the five main
religion groups ($\chi^2 = \religChiSqStat$, df\,=\,\religChiSqDof,
$p = \pReligChiSq$, \pReligChiSqSig{}).
Both Buddhism (\NbudATier/\NbudSitesRelig{} A-tier, \budATierRate\%;
\NbudInterHarmonic/\NbudSitesRelig{} C-band, \budInterHarmonicRate\%;
$p = \pBudJoint$~\pBudJointSig{}) and Hinduism (\NhinATier/\NhinSites{} A-tier, \hinATierRate\%;
\NhinInterHarmonic/\NhinSites{} C-band, \hinInterHarmonicRate\%;

\paragraph*{Rayleigh and anchor-specific checks.}
\label{sec:results_rayleigh}
The A+-only Rayleigh statistic is omitted from the results because it is a
mathematical consequence of the A+ classification criterion
(\S\ref{sec:circularity}), not an independent test.
The following formal tests address distinct aspects of the periodicity:

\textbf{Test~A (full-corpus Rayleigh periodogram — boundary condition).}
The full-corpus Rayleigh $R = \fullRayleighR$
($Z = \fullRayleighZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \fullRayleighPermP$,
\fullRayleighPermPSig{}) at $T = 3\textdegree{}$ is null.
We report this as a boundary condition, not a failure of the
primary claim. The Rayleigh periodogram asks whether raw longitudes
show a phase-marginalized sinusoidal periodicity across the corpus; it
is structurally insensitive to sparse, anchor-specific clustering near
a subset of fixed lattice nodes, because sites near nodes and
anti-nodes are both treated as phase information about the same period.
The present analysis therefore treats the Rayleigh null as evidence
against invariant 3\textdegree{} periodicity, while leaving the
selected-frame node-proximity count excess to be evaluated by the
conditional tests below.

\textbf{Test~C (circular-shift anchor sensitivity).}
Displacing the anchor by a uniform random offset in $[0^\circ,
360^\circ)$ and recounting A+ sites yields a null mean of
\anchorShiftNullMu{} vs.\ the observed \anchorShiftApCount{}
($Z = \anchorShiftZ$, $p_\mathrm{perm} = \anchorShiftPermP$, \anchorShiftPermPSig{}).
A positive anchor-specific test complementary to Test~D.

\textbf{Test~D (targeted von~Mises mixture, full corpus).}
A von~Mises/uniform two-component mixture is fit to all $N = \NoriginCorpus$
folded angles with $\mu_0$ fixed at the Gerizim phase; $\hat{w}$ and
$\hat{\kappa}$ estimated by EM; $D = 2(\hat{\ell} - \ell_0)$ referred to a
parametric bootstrap ($B = \targetedB$) for an empirical $p$-value.
On the full corpus: $D = \targetedDfull$,
$\hat{w} = \targetedWfull$ (i.e.\ ${\approx}\targetedWfullPct\%$ of all
sites), $\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKfull$
(angular SD $\approx \targetedSdFullDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPfull$ (\targetedPfullSig{}).
On the A+ subset alone: $D = \targetedDAp$, $\hat{w} = \targetedWAp$,
$\hat{\kappa} = \targetedKAp$ (angular SD $\approx \targetedSdApDeg$\textdegree{}),
$p_\mathrm{boot} = \targetedPAp$ (\targetedPApSig{}).
Tests~C and~D are consistent with anchor-specific 3\textdegree{} structure rather
than a statistical artifact.

\textbf{Test~E (anchor discovery correction).}
A global sweep of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors asks whether the
Levantine corridor ranks unusually among all possible anchors
($p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}).
This is a conservative upper bound on the anchor-selection penalty;
the strongest pattern rests on Tests~2 and~3, which do not depend on the anchor choice
(\S\ref{sec:limitations_circularity}).

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:robustness}

\subsection{Unit Sensitivity Sweep}
\label{sec:metrology}

Varying harmonic spacing from 1.5\textdegree{} to 6\textdegree{} (physical threshold
fixed at \ApThreshKm{}~km), the 3\textdegree{} spacing produces the strongest joint
significance across both the dome and full-corpus populations.
The 1.5\textdegree{} and 6\textdegree{} spacings also reach significance, but both are
mathematical consequences of the 3\textdegree{} structure: every 3\textdegree{}
harmonic is also a 1.5\textdegree{} harmonic, and 6\textdegree{} harmonics are a strict
subset, so neither constitutes an independent signal.
Adjacent non-harmonic spacings (1.8\textdegree{}, 2.1\textdegree{}, 2.7\textdegree{}, 3.3\textdegree{}, 3.6\textdegree{}) are
non-significant in either population, the key discriminating observation.
The 2.4\textdegree{} spacing reaches nominal dome significance
($p = \sweepEightDomeP$) but not full-corpus ($p = \sweepEightFullP$, \sweepEightFullPSig{}),
reflecting unsystematic local overlap with the 3\textdegree{} grid rather than
a genuine secondary periodicity.
Specifically, \sweepEightTenDomeBoth{}/\sweepZeroEightDomeHits{} 
(\sweepEightTenDomePctOfEight\%) of 2.4\textdegree{} dome hits align 
to the 3\textdegree{} grid, indicating the significance is due to overlap.

A degree-denominated Monte Carlo sweep

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment}
\label{sec:limitations_circularity}
\label{sec:circularity}

\textbf{Exploratory status and inferential limitations.}
Because all analytical choices (3\textdegree{} spacing, Gerizim anchor,
dome keyword set, tier thresholds) were informed by iterative examination
of the UNESCO corpus, this study reports an exploratory finding rather
than a hypothesis-confirming test. The relevant
scientific questions are: (1)~Is the within-frame organization
internally consistent under sensitivity checks? (2)~Was the reference
phase chosen on independent grounds, and is the anchor-specificity
itself a coherent feature of the selected representation rather than
an artifact of investigator-degrees-of-freedom? (3)~What additional
evidence would constitute external validation? The first two are
addressed below; the third is taken up in the Conclusion.

\textbf{Anchor-specificity as a property of the selected representation.}
The within-frame organization characterized in
\S\ref{sec:results_global}--\S\ref{sec:resultscluster} is by
construction anchor-specific: it is a property of the chosen
3\textdegree{}/Gerizim representation, not a claim about a phase that
survives marginalization over all anchors. The global anchor sweep
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}) is consistent with the enrichment
being a property of the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor: every
$\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E anchor produces the same A+ count, and
both Gerizim and Jerusalem place the corridor in the global top
\anchorSweepTopPct\% of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors. Gerizim
is distinguished within that corridor by pre-existing documentary
evidence---the \textit{tabbur ha-aretz} cosmographic role
(\S\ref{sec:anchor})---predating this analysis. We do not present
this as a unique solution to an inverse problem; we present Gerizim
as one historically motivated point in a corridor whose width is
itself part of the result.

The 3\textdegree{} structure is recoverable by any unanchored
\NharmonicsCoarse-phase scan within the corridor. Dome enrichment
(Test~2) is identical across all \NharmonicsCoarse{}
$\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E anchors. Cluster asymmetry (Test~3)
requires no keyword filter, but it is still defined by the same
A+ classification and therefore characterizes the selected
representation rather than supplying an anchor-independent test. The
anchor-discovery correction (Test~E,
$p = \anchorMaxPermP$, \anchorMaxPermPSig{}) is marginal, which we
report as a boundary condition (\S\ref{sec:results_boundary}) rather
than a verdict; the strongest within-frame diagnostics
(Tests~2 and~3) do not depend on the anchor choice.

\subsection{Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias}
\label{sec:limitations_geo}

The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the
Mediterranean-to-South-Asian corridor of Cultural/Mixed sites. 
The regional distribution is as follows: 
(Europe \& North America: \CorpusPctEurope\%;
Arab States: \CorpusPctArabStates\%;
Asia-Pacific: \CorpusPctAsiaPac\%;
Latin Americas: \CorpusPctLatAm\%;
Africa: \CorpusPctAfrica\%).
Domed monuments nominally exceed the A+ rate of other monuments from the same longitude
footprint (Null~C, $p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPSig{}),
while Null~A ($p = \geoNullDomeBootP$, \geoNullDomeBootPSig{}) is consistent
with the pattern being a consequence of where dome sites already are.

A Monte Carlo unit sweep (\mcNperms{} permutations, \mcNcandidates{} candidate

assuming any particular unit. The dominant peak is at 3\textdegree{} ($p < 0.001$),

Collapsing harmonics, the dome subset yields \mcDomeNsigCollapsed{} independent

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:alt_explanations}

\textbf{(1) Founding-canon and inscription selection.}
UNESCO's founding canon prioritized famous monuments; famous monuments
cluster in well-known cultural centers; and well-known cultural
centers cluster along the Eurasian corridor. This selection pathway is
a leading non-intentional explanation for the elevated early A++ rate
reported in \S\ref{sec:resultstemporal}.

\textbf{(2) Geographic concentration.}
Null~A is consistent with the dome pattern being a consequence of
where dome sites already are, while Null~C finds that other monuments
from the same longitude footprint are nominally less enriched
($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedP$, \geoNullDomeRestrictedPSig{}). The
Monte Carlo unit sweep (\S\ref{sec:limitations_geo}) is consistent
with 3\textdegree{} without assuming any unit.

\textbf{(3) Morphological recurrence.}
Hemispherical structures recur across cultures
\citep{aveni2001skywatchers,aveni2003archaeoastronomy}, but recurrence
by itself predicts no longitude specificity.

\textbf{(4) Random coincidence.}
The KDE-smoothed null is consistent with dome enrichment ($p = \simKDEP$~\simKDEPSig{});
permutation ($p = \simDomePermP$~\simDomePermPSig{}) and bootstrap
($p = \simDomeBootP$~\simDomeBootPSig{}) are marginal at the A+ level
but highly significant for the A++ sub-population
(permutation $p = \circAppPermP$~\pCircAppPermSig{};
bootstrap $p = \circAppBootP$~\pCircAppBootSig{}), subject to the
Null~A caveat above.
Tests~C and~D (\S\ref{sec:results_rayleigh}) are consistent with anchor specificity and periodicity structure.

\subsection{Interpreting Modest Effect Sizes}
\label{sec:effect_sizes}

The observed enrichment ($\clusterEnrichAp\times$ full corpus,
$\circEnrichAp\times$ domes at A+, $\circEnrichApp\times$ domes at A++,
with the Null~A caveat that dome-site geography can account for much
of the sub-population pattern) is statistically robust but modest in
magnitude. We interpret this as a pattern consistent with partial
adherence, selection, or preferential tendency rather than strict
observance of a 3\textdegree{} placement rule. Several explanations
are consistent with this pattern:

\textbf{(1) Founding-canon selection effects.} UNESCO's earliest
inscriptions emphasized famous monuments. Famous monuments are
disproportionately located in well-known cultural centers, and those
centers are concentrated along the Eurasian corridor. This mechanism
does not require intentional longitude placement and is given the same
evidentiary weight here as logistical or diffusion explanations.

\textbf{(2) Logistical convenience.} Trade routes and way-stations
naturally cluster at intervals determined by travel distance: one
day's journey ($\sim$30\,km at 30\textdegree{}N latitude) corresponds
to roughly $0.3$\textdegree{}, so a 3\textdegree{} grid represents
about ten such intervals and could capture higher-order staging posts
without coordinated placement.

\textbf{(3) Cultural diffusion with local variation.} Buddhist
architectural traditions spread along the Silk Roads with transmission
hubs at specific longitudes (Lumbini, the Borobudur node at
$\approx$110\textdegree{}E), while local adaptation introduces scatter
that flattens the effect size when averaged across the full corpus.

\textbf{(4) Selective preservation beyond the founding canon.} Later
inscription and preservation processes may continue to favor
culturally significant sites at trade junctions or political centers
that happen to align with the corridor, even as the World Heritage
List expands geographically.

These explanations remain speculative. The contribution of this paper
is to document the empirical regularity and its statistical robustness;
determining the historical mechanism, if any, requires independent
lines of evidence beyond spatial statistics alone.

\subsection{Framework Portability and Cross-Corpus Anchor Recovery}
\label{sec:transmission}

\textbf{The Rayleigh null does not contradict cross-corpus recovery.}
Two statistics address different questions about the 3\textdegree{}
structure and should not be conflated.
The full-corpus Rayleigh test ($R = \fullRayleighR$,
$p = \fullRayleighPermP$, \fullRayleighPermPSig{}) asks: \textit{are
UNESCO longitudes globally distributed as a sinusoidal density at
3\textdegree{}?} It returns null because the answer is no: roughly
60\% of UNESCO sites lie outside the Eurasian monument corridor and
contribute no signal at 3\textdegree{}. A weak global $R$ is expected
under any model in which the alignment is geographically confined.
The Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) test asks an entirely different
question: \textit{do independent monument databases, assembled by
different curators for different purposes, spontaneously converge on
the same 3\textdegree{} anchor?} This is a cross-corpus replication
test. Each corpus receives one ``vote'' (its unit phasor at $T =
3\textdegree{}$), and $K$ measures how tightly those votes agree in
direction.

\textbf{Three monument corpora recover the same anchor (NPC positive
result).}
The UNESCO Cultural/Mixed corpus ($N = \npcNUnesco{}$), the UNESCO
domed-monument subset ($N = \npcNDome{}$), and the Wikidata Q180987
stupa corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$) are three independently assembled
monument databases curated by different institutions for different
purposes. At $T = 3\textdegree{}$ they yield individual anchors of
$\phi = +2.050\textdegree{}$, $+2.027\textdegree{}$, and
$+1.868\textdegree{}$ respectively---within 0.18\textdegree{} of each
other---producing an NPC consensus of $K = \npcMonK{}$
(perm-$p = \npcMonPermP{}$*,
$\npcNPerm{}$ phase-randomization draws).
The consensus grid line falls \npcMonGridDeltaE\textdegree{} from
35\textdegree{}E.
This result is \textit{not} explained by any single corpus's
geographic bias: it is precisely \textit{because} the curators
assembled these databases independently---UNESCO inscription panels,
Buddhist heritage scholars, and Wikidata contributors have no common
agenda regarding 3\textdegree{} longitude---that spontaneous
convergence on the same anchor is evidentially informative.
The NPC positive result therefore outweighs the Rayleigh null as
evidence for the cross-corpus reality of the anchor, because it is
designed to test exactly that question.

\textbf{OWTRAD as contrast corpus.}
A fourth corpus---OWTRAD Silk Road trade-route nodes ($N =
\npcNOwtrad{}$)---does not share the anchor.
At $T = 3\textdegree{}$ OWTRAD's own Rayleigh $R = \npcOwR{}$ is not
significant in isolation ($p = \npcOwRayleighP{}$, ns), and its
preferred anchor ($\phi = \npcOwAnchor\textdegree{}$) sits
\npcOwAnchorDelta\textdegree{} from the monument consensus.
When OWTRAD is included in the NPC test, consensus drops from $K =
\npcMonK{}$ to $K = \npcAllK{}$ (perm-$p = \npcAllPermP{}$, ns).
This contrast is informative: trade-route nodes are governed by
terrain, river crossings, and oases---not by the monumental geography
that drives the monument corpora. The fact that OWTRAD disrupts the
consensus is itself evidence that the monument anchor reflects a
property of monumental siting rather than a generic feature of Eurasian
longitude distributions.
The within-frame OWTRAD enrichment results described below are
therefore complementary to, not in tension with, the NPC finding:
they test whether OWTRAD nodes cluster \textit{near} the UNESCO grid
lines conditional on the selected anchor, whereas the NPC test asks
whether the anchor is spontaneously recovered without conditioning.

The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus and the OWTRAD attested Silk Road
network are not intended as geography-independent replications of the
UNESCO result. They are targeted out-of-sample tests of the
hypothesized Eurasian corridor signal: if the UNESCO pattern were
merely an idiosyncrasy of UNESCO inscription practice---a
selection-effect artifact of how that particular committee chooses
and documents sites---the same phase and enrichment structure would
not necessarily appear in independently assembled stupa and
route-node datasets that were compiled for entirely different
purposes. Their agreement therefore supports corpus-level portability
within the hypothesized Silk Road/stupa domain. It does not constitute
geography-independent confirmation, because all three datasets are
concentrated in the Eurasian corridor; the agreement is consistent
with, but does not require, an explanation that goes beyond shared
corridor geography. We report these analyses as portability checks
that use the same anchor and parameters as the primary analysis---not
as independent confirmation---and we treat the shared 3\textdegree{}
pattern as a real empirical observation that is not reducible to the
UNESCO corpus alone. The priority for future work is
geography-independent validation: application of this framework, with
all parameters frozen at the deposited values, by a different research
team to heritage corpora from regions outside the Eurasian corridor
assembled without reference to this analysis. First, the OWTRAD
attested-route network \citep{ciolek2004owtrad}
(\NOwtradEdges{} route segments,
\NOwtradVertices{} unique endpoints).
Testing all \NOwtradDegW{} endpoint positions across the full edge list
(each city weighted by the number of segments it connects),
\NOwtradDegWApp{} fall at A++ harmonics, \NOwtradDegWAp{} at A+, and \NOwtradDegWA{} at A
($\owtradDegWAppEnrich\times$ null rate, $z = \zOwtradDegWApp$,
$p = \pOwtradDegWApp$~\pOwtradDegWAppSig{} for A++;
$p = \pOwtradDegWAp$~\pOwtradDegWApSig{} for A+;
$p = \pOwtradDegWA$~\pOwtradDegWASig{} for A; one-tailed binomial).
Testing each unique endpoint once gives
\NOwtradVertexApp{}/\NOwtradVertices{} at A++, \NOwtradVertexAp{} at A+, and \NOwtradVertexA{} at A
($\owtradVertexAppEnrich\times$, $z = \zOwtradVertexApp$,
$p = \pOwtradVertexApp$~\pOwtradVertexAppSig{} for A++;
$p = \pOwtradVertexAp$~\pOwtradVertexApSig{} for A+;
$p = \pOwtradVertexA$~\pOwtradVertexASig{} for A; one-tailed binomial).
A phase-shift test (random offset of the 3\textdegree{} grid,
\NOwtradPerms{} iterations) gives $p = \pOwtradVertexAppPhase$~\pOwtradVertexAppPhaseSig{},
consistent with enrichment specific to the Gerizim anchor.

\subsection{The Mechanism Gap}
\label{sec:mechanism}

A literal reading of the within-frame organization---that builders
across Eurasia placed monuments with sub-degree longitudinal precision
relative to a common reference---would require pre-modern continental
longitude determination at a precision that is not a documented
historical capability. Babylonian astronomical methods support angular
measurement and eclipse timing
\citep{hunger2019mul,neugebauer1975history,robson2008mathematics} but
do not extend to terrestrial longitude across continental scales, and
no surveying instrument calibrated in ancient metrological sub-units
relevant to such a procedure has been recovered. We take this gap
seriously as a constraint on interpretation rather than as a
peripheral caveat: any historical reading of the reported regularity
must favor explanations that do not require coordinated placement.

This is why we frame the empirical contribution as
representation-induced organization rather than as evidence of
metrology or coordinated design. The selected
3\textdegree{}/Gerizim/dome representation interacts with the actual
geographic and cultural distribution of UNESCO sites---trade-route
spacing at characteristic travel intervals, geographic channeling by
river systems and passes, the concentration of inscribable cultural
heritage along the Eurasian corridor, and inscription selection
effects within UNESCO itself---to produce a set of within-frame
characterizations. That the selected-frame count excess is sharper
than chance under permutation and bootstrap nulls drawn from the same
representation is the result we report. Whether the pattern
ultimately reflects historical intentionality, the geometry of
Eurasian corridor geography under a particular representational
choice, or some combination of these, is not decided here, and we
explicitly do not propose a mechanism.


\section{Conclusion}

We report a selected-frame count excess in the longitudinal
distribution of the UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus under a fixed
3\textdegree{} harmonic-deviation representation referenced to the
Levantine corridor. The contribution has two distinct components that
must be evaluated separately.

\textit{What the Rayleigh test shows.}
Phase-invariant tests---the full-corpus Rayleigh statistic
($p = \fullRayleighPermP$) and the global anchor-discovery correction
($p = \anchorMaxPermP$)---are negative or marginal and are reported as
boundary conditions: we do not claim invariant global periodicity or a
phase-free privileged status for the corridor. The Rayleigh null
reflects the dilution of the corridor signal by the majority of UNESCO
sites located outside the Eurasian monument zone; it tests smooth
sinusoidal periodicity across the global distribution, not
anchor-specific monumental clustering.

\textit{What the NPC test shows.}
The Normalized Phasor Consensus test asks whether three independently
assembled monument corpora---UNESCO Cultural/Mixed, the UNESCO
domed-monument subset, and the Wikidata Q180987 stupa
database---converge spontaneously on the same 3\textdegree{} anchor.
They do: $K = \npcMonK{}$, perm-$p = \npcMonPermP{}$*, with a
consensus grid line \npcMonGridDeltaE\textdegree{} from 35\textdegree{}E.
A fourth corpus (OWTRAD trade-route nodes) does not share the anchor,
confirming that the convergence is specific to monument corpora rather
than a generic property of Eurasian longitude distributions.
The NPC result is the appropriate test for cross-corpus anchor
recoverability; it takes precedence over the Rayleigh null on the
question of whether the anchor is a real and reproducible feature of
monumental geography.

Inside the fixed selected representation, the primary within-frame result
is full-corpus A+ harmonic-node enrichment
($\clusterEnrichAp\times$). That count excess is characterized by a
Spearman-style harmonic-band asymmetry in which A+-bearing bands host
$\clusterHarmonicRatio\times$ more sites than empty bands; phase
localization within the selected 3\textdegree{} circle
(\phaseFullGerPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile); A++ enrichment in
the domed/spherical sub-population ($\circEnrichApp\times$) with
leave-3-out worst-case robustness, while Null~A remains consistent
with the dome result being a consequence of dome-site geography; a
unit sweep in which 3\textdegree{} is the dominant spacing and adjacent
non-harmonic units are null while 4\textdegree{} and 8\textdegree{}
reduce to nodes shared with the 3\textdegree{} grid; and a related
3\textdegree{} pattern in the Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus and the
OWTRAD attested Silk Road network, both of which share the same broad
Eurasian corridor geography. Under full Bonferroni correction across
$k = \NtotalTests$ reported tests, \NsurviveBonfAll{} results survive.

The selected 3\textdegree{}/Gerizim representation is anchor-specific
and unit-specific by construction; we treat that specificity as part
of what is being characterized. Within the corridor, every
$\xbandLabel$\textdegree{}E anchor produces the same enrichment, and
Mount Gerizim is presented as a historically documented candidate
within that corridor (\textit{tabbur ha-aretz}; \citealt{purvis1968}),
not as a unique solution to an inverse problem. The Wikidata and
OWTRAD analyses are framework-portability checks within the Eurasian
corridor, not independent geographic confirmation.

\textbf{What is and is not claimed.}
We claim that the chosen representation organizes the UNESCO corpus
and two related Eurasian corpora in a way that random permutations of
the same representation do not. We do not treat mathematically related
diagnostics as independent confirmation, and we do not claim invariant
3\textdegree{} periodicity, a unique historical anchor, or a
metrological mechanism. We explicitly flag the absence of any
documented pre-modern capability for sub-degree continental longitude
determination
(\S\ref{sec:mechanism}) as a constraint on historical interpretation.

\textbf{External validation and next steps.}
Because the corpora analyzed here were fixed before this study and
selected for cultural importance rather than for spatial analysis,
traditional pre-registration is not available as a validation
pathway. The shared 3\textdegree{} agreement across UNESCO, Wikidata
Q180987, and OWTRAD is the most interesting feature of the analysis,
but it does not establish the effect because all three corpora are
Eurasian-corridor concentrated. Meaningful external validation would
require: (1)~application of this framework---with 3\textdegree{}
spacing, Gerizim anchor, and dome keyword set fixed as in the Zenodo
v1.6.0 deposit---to heritage corpora outside the Eurasian corridor,
including national registers not consulted during analysis;
(2)~adversarial reanalysis by an independent team using the open
codebase; or (3)~archaeological evidence bearing on whether builders
at the identified sites had any access to longitude information at
the implied precision. The highest-priority next step is application
of the frozen pipeline to non-Eurasian heritage corpora by an
independent team. We make all data, code, and methods freely available
(\S\ref{sec:data_code}; Zenodo DOI
\texttt{10.5281/zenodo.19938283}) to facilitate such external checks.
The scientific value of this work ultimately depends on whether the
selected-frame pattern is found in datasets assembled independently by
other researchers outside the corridor studied here.

\subsection{Dome/Spherical Monument Filter (Tests~2, 2b, 2x, 2bx)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_dome}

\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text. All \NcircTotal{} matching
sites are included in Test~2 without context gating.

For Test~2x, ambiguous keywords (\textit{dome, domed, domes, spherical})
are subject to a two-gate context check: Gate~1 (\NdomeGateNeg{} negative
patterns) rejects non-architectural uses; Gate~2 (\NdomeGatePos{} positive
patterns) requires architectural context. Unambiguous keywords
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}) are accepted without validation.
The seven
rejected sites are: Hiroshima Peace Memorial, Richtersveld, Gyeongju,
Vi\~{n}ales Valley, Rock Islands Southern Lagoon, Uluru-Kata Tju\d{t}a,
and Diqu\'{i}s Stone Spheres.

\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{Test~2b} adds earthen mound-stage
keywords (\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun} unambiguous;
\textit{mound} context-validated) to trace the hemispherical tradition
from burial mound through stupa to architectural dome. Test~2bx applies
the same architectural context gates to the extended population.
Statistics for all four variants are reported in
\hyperref[sec:resultscircular]{§\ref*{sec:resultscircular}} and
\hyperref[sec:resultsevolution_inline]{§\ref*{sec:resultsevolution_inline}}.

\subsection{Founding-Origin Classifier (Test~5, Post-Hoc)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_founding}

\textbf{Results.} The classifier finds \foundKwApRate\% of A+ sites
carry a founding keyword versus \foundKwBaseRate\% in the full corpus
