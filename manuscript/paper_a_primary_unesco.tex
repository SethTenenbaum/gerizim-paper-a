\begin{abstract}
Whether any spatial regularity in the placement of ancient monumental
sites reflects shared metrological knowledge, rather than independent
local practice or statistical coincidence, is a question that has
resisted rigorous evaluation, largely because prior studies rely on
investigator-selected site lists.
This study tests the longitudinal distribution of UNESCO World Heritage
Cultural and Mixed sites ($N = \NclusterTotal$) against the angular
interval attested in Babylonian astronomical records, the \textit{beru}
(30\textdegree{} of arc, MUL.APIN, c.~1000~BCE), with Mount Gerizim
(\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E) as a pre-specified anchor.
Domed and spherical monuments show 12.2\% Tier-A+ alignment
($\leq$6.7~km from a 0.1-beru harmonic), confirmed by permutation
($Z = \simDomePermZ$, $p = \simDomePermP$) and KDE simulation
($Z = \simKDEZ$, $p = \simKDEP$), surviving Bonferroni correction
($k = \BonfK$, $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$).
The 0.1-beru spacing is the only tested interval producing joint
significance in both the dome and full-corpus populations, and
$\pm 0.3\%$ shifts collapse significance.
The results identify departures from null expectation not explained by
known geographic or curatorial biases.
The underlying mechanism, whether metrological transmission, latent
geographic structure, or an unidentified artifact, remains an open
question requiring independent replication.
All data, code, and random seeds are publicly archived
(\S\ref{sec:methods}).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The organization of sacred and monumental space is one of the oldest
problems in the archaeology of religion and landscape.
Across cultures and millennia, societies have oriented temples,
tombs, and civic structures toward celestial bodies, cardinal
directions, or prominent topographic features, embedding
cosmological assumptions into the built environment in ways that
survive long after the belief systems that generated them have
transformed beyond recognition
\citep{aveni2001skywatchers,ruggles2015handbook,magli2013}.
Whether any of these orientations reflects shared metrological
knowledge transmitted across the ancient world, including
standardized angular divisions attested in early astronomical
traditions, rather than independent local practice, is a question
that has proven resistant to rigorous evaluation, primarily because
most attempts to answer it rely on site lists assembled by the same
investigators who proposed the hypothesis.

The resulting methodological impasse is structural.
Most prior studies of spatial regularity at ancient sites rely
on small, investigator-selected samples and unconstrained geometric
degrees of freedom, making it difficult to distinguish genuine
structure from post-hoc patterning
\citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}.
Selection bias and the flexibility to choose reference frames,
intervals, and thresholds after inspecting the data have rightly
drawn sustained methodological criticism
\citep{kendall1974,ruggles1999astronomy}.
What has been lacking is a test conducted against a large, externally
curated site corpus, with the hypothesis, test statistic, and
reference frame all fixed before the data are examined.

More broadly, methodological critiques in this literature, particularly
concerning selection bias and post-hoc geometric flexibility, have largely
functioned as diagnostic tools for rejecting spurious claims rather than
as the basis for constructive, pre-specified tests.
This study builds on those critiques by incorporating them directly into
the design of a reproducible statistical framework.
We test whether the longitudinal distribution of UNESCO World Heritage
Cultural and Mixed sites exhibits statistically detectable departures
from null expectation consistent with a fixed angular interval
attested in ancient Babylonian astronomical records, the \textit{beru}
(30\textdegree{} of arc), anchored to a Levantine reference frame
chosen to reflect the historical prominence of the ancient Near
East in early metrological and astronomical traditions
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics}.
The UNESCO World Heritage corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$) is independently
curated, externally fixed, and was not compiled by the author.
It cannot be modified to suit any hypothesis, and its global
coverage makes geographic sampling bias tractable through simulation.

A pre-specified statistical framework is applied to the full corpus,
permitting reproducible evaluation of spatial patterning under
controlled conditions (\S\ref{sec:methods}).
The interpretive framework distinguishes three classes of explanation
for any observed pattern (metrological transmission, latent geographic
structure, and statistical artifact), which are evaluated in the
Discussion (\S\ref{sec:framework}, \S\ref{sec:discussion}).

The monument classes examined here, including mounds, stupas, and domed
structures, are widely interpreted as encoding cosmological and
metrological concepts across the historical record, and form the
primary empirical focus of the morphological sub-tests
(\S\ref{sec:methodscircular}, \S\ref{sec:methodsevolution}).
Whether these specific form classes exhibit disproportionate spatial
regularity, above and beyond the full-corpus signal, is the central
typological question.

Known sources of UNESCO selection bias produce geographic
\emph{clustering}, but not regularly spaced longitudinal periodicity.
The two are distinct phenomena, and the observed signal persists under
permutation tests and morphological sub-filtering.
The analysis identifies statistically significant departures from null
expectation but does not, on its own, constitute direct evidence of
knowledge transmission or deliberate coordination.
Its contribution is to demonstrate that these regularities are unlikely
to arise from random or geographically constrained processes, and are
instead more consistent with structured mechanisms, potentially
including cultural transmission, while remaining agnostic about any
single causal account.
The three competing classes of explanation that structure the Discussion
are introduced in \S\ref{sec:framework}.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:prior}

\subsection{The Beru Unit}
\label{sec:beru}

The \textit{beru} (30\textdegree{} of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN
cuneiform compendium \citep{hunger2019mul,watson2011writing}, the composition of which is
dated on broad grounds to the late second or early first millennium~BCE.
The unit divided the ecliptic into twelve equal segments and served for
both stellar and terrestrial angular measurement.
The terrestrial use is attested explicitly in MUL.APIN~II~ii~43--iii~15
(the ``shadow-length'' table), which converts beru of daylight into
ground distances \citep[pp.~2--15]{hunger2019mul}.
The sub-division of the beru into 30~\textit{uš}
(1~uš~$= 1$\textdegree{}) is well attested \citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics},
and the 0.1-beru interval (3\textdegree{}) tested here corresponds to
one-tenth of a \textit{beru}, or exactly 3~\textit{uš}, an integer
number of the smallest standard angular unit.

The beru also functioned as a unit of territorial description in
administrative and cosmological contexts beyond astronomical tables.
Royal inscriptions and administrative texts record inter-regional
distances in beru between major cities and outlying territories
(\textit{nagû}) \citep{neugebauer1975history,robson2008mathematics}.
On the Babylonian \textit{Imago Mundi} (the so-called Map of the
World), the distances between the ``islands'' beyond the encircling
Salt Sea are explicitly marked as multiples of beru, placing the unit
in a cosmographic as well as a practical geographic register.
The parallel institution of the \textit{kudurru} (boundary stone)
records land-grant limits and territorial extents in cuneiform
inscriptions; many kudurru were deposited in temples as
legally authoritative records \citep{robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
The combination of a fractional angular unit employed at cosmological
scales and a tradition of temple-deposited territorial records is
consistent with the hypothesis that beru-based longitude spacing could
have been embedded in the placement of major religious and civic
foundations, though the present study tests the statistical pattern
rather than the mechanism of transmission.

\subsection{Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice}

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular measures
across large geographic scales has a long and contested scholarly history.
Thom's ``megalithic yard'' \citep{thom1967,thom1971,thom1964}
stimulated statistical refinement \citep{kendall1974} and methodological
critique: Freeman showed Thom's quantum analysis was sensitive to
selection effects \citep{freeman1976}, and Kendall cautioned that
apparent quanta can arise from rounding in the source data \citep{kendall1974}.
Powell's treatment of Mesopotamian metrology \citep{powell1987metrological}
remains the standard reference for the system within which the beru is embedded.
More recently, Magli \citep{magli2013,magli2016} has applied Monte Carlo
permutation methods to archaeoastronomical alignments,
and Ruggles and Silva have advanced probability-based frameworks for
evaluating orientation claims
\citep{ruggles1999astronomy,ruggles2015handbook,silva2020statistical}.
The ICOMOS--IAU thematic study \citep{ruggles2010icomos} provides the
institutional context for archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage
properties; Aveni \citep{aveni2001skywatchers,aveni2003archaeoastronomy}
and Neugebauer \citep{neugebauer1975history} supply the broader
disciplinary framework.

The present study inherits this tradition but departs from it in
several respects.
First, the site list is externally curated (the UNESCO World Heritage
List), eliminating the selection bias that undermines many
archaeoastronomical claims.
Second, the test is one-dimensional (longitude only), yielding a
well-defined geometric null expectation (4\%) that requires no free
parameters.
Third, the analysis is fully computational and publicly archived,
permitting exact reproduction
\citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.

\subsection{Distinction from Ley-Line Studies}

Ley-line hypotheses \citep{watkins1925} have been criticized for
post-hoc site selection, unconstrained geometric degrees of freedom,
and the absence of well-defined null models
\citep{williamson1983leylines,hutton1991pagan,kendall1974}.
This study avoids all three problems. The site list is frozen before
analysis (the UNESCO registry, not compiled by the author), the test
statistic is pre-specified, and the geometric null is analytically
derived.
The distinction is structural, not rhetorical.

\subsection{The Anchor: Mount Gerizim}
\label{sec:anchor}

Mount Gerizim was selected as the reference anchor on substantive
historical and metrological grounds, prior to computing any beru
distances.
In Samaritan tradition, Gerizim is the \textit{tabbur ha-aretz}
(``navel of the earth''), the cosmic center from which the earth was
created and around which sacred geography is organized
\citep{purvis1968,macdonald1964,crown1989}.
This geographic-centrality tradition is directly analogous to the
role Babylon and its meridian play in cuneiform astronomical records
\citep{hunger2019mul}, making Gerizim the most historically motivated
single Levantine anchor point within the beru metrological framework.
No optimization across alternative Levantine candidate locations was
performed in the primary analysis; the anchor was fixed before
any site deviations were examined.

The six pre-specified enrichment tests are computed relative to the
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor rather than to any single point.
Jerusalem (35.2317\textdegree{}E) and Gerizim (\GerizimLon\textdegree{}E),
separated by $\sim$\GerizimJerusalemKm~km, produce near-identical
aggregate A+ counts (56 vs.\ 55 when Jerusalem is removed from
Gerizim's corpus; \S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}),
confirming that the corridor result is not anchor-dependent.
Both carry independent geographic-centrality traditions:
Jerusalem carries the Jewish and Christian \textit{omphalos} tradition
\citep{harvey1987mappamundi}.

Gerizim ranks at the 97.2\textsuperscript{nd} percentile in the global
anchor sweep; Jerusalem at the 98.3\textsuperscript{rd}.
This means $\sim$3\% of trial anchors produce equal or greater A+ counts,
corresponding to $\sim$1{,}000 of 36{,}000 trial longitudes.
These competing anchors are not randomly scattered but cluster near
the $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic artifact (\S\ref{sec:x18artifact}),
a structural property of the discrete UNESCO longitude distribution.
The percentile ranking is therefore best understood as placing
the Levantine corridor in an unusual but not unique class of longitudes.
The evidential weight rests on the pre-specified historical motivation
for the anchor and the morphological and temporal sub-structure of the
signal, not on the anchor's global rank alone.

Gerizim is used throughout because its tentative-list coordinate
(ref.~5706, DMS \GerizimLonDMS{}) is externally sourced and not in the
UNESCO XML test corpus, eliminating circularity by construction.
At A++ precision ($\leq$0.67~km), both Gerizim and Jerusalem yield 7 hits
(Jerusalem excluding self-hit); Gerizim's A++ set includes several key
founding-class sites (Sacri~Monti 80~m, Khoja~Ahmed~Yasawi 230~m,
Palmyra 260~m). This comparison is noted as a post-hoc observation and does not
bear on the pre-specified results.
The Wikipedia/GeoHack summit (35.2733\textdegree{}E) is within $\sim$500~m.

\subsection{Interpretive Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

This paper is a pattern-detection study, not a historical attribution.
Three broad classes of explanation could account for a positive result.
(1)~\textbf{Metrological transmission:} an angular reference system
anchored near the Levant was transmitted through trade, imperial
administration, or religious networks.
(2)~\textbf{Latent geographic structure:} the clustering reflects
underlying spatial patterns in where civilizations built monumental
architecture.
(3)~\textbf{Statistical artifact:} an unidentified systematic bias
in the UNESCO corpus, geocoding, or longitude distribution.
The simulation null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null}) test hypothesis~(2)
directly, and the dome sub-population survives.
The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic artifact (\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global})
is the primary example of mechanism~(3) and is accounted for explicitly.
Adjudicating between these three classes requires independent evidence
beyond the scope of this study.

\subsection{Geodetic Framework}

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at \GerizimLon\textdegree{}E,
from Palestine's UNESCO tentative list (ref.~5706, DMS \GerizimLonDMS{}, 2012).
Gerizim is not on the inscribed World Heritage List and does not appear
in the test-site corpus, avoiding circularity by construction.
In each sweep the anchor is excluded from the test population;
Jerusalem is also excluded in both sweeps (\S\ref{sec:anchor}).

For a site at longitude $\lambda$, the beru-distance is
$B = |\lambda - \GerizimLon| / 30$ and the beru-deviation from the
nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is
$\delta = |B - \operatorname{round}(B, 0.1)|$.
Longitude differences are in angular measure, and no
cosine-of-latitude correction is applied.
The geometric null for Tier-A+ is $2 \times 0.002 / 0.1 = 4\%$.
A Gaussian KDE fitted to the observed longitude distribution produces
$\simKDEMean \pm \simKDEStd$ expected A+ out of \NclusterTotal{} sites
($\sim$4.0\%), confirming the geometric null is not inflated by
longitude clumping (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null}).

Throughout this paper, exact binomial $p$-values are reported as a
transparent reference baseline against the geometric null.
The \emph{primary} tests against geographic structure are the
simulation-based null models (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null}), including longitude
permutation, bootstrap, and KDE-smoothed null, which preserve the
empirical longitude distribution and do not assume i.i.d.\ sampling.
Where simulation and binomial p-values diverge, the simulation figure
takes precedence.
Binomial p-values should be read as convenient approximations that
assume uniform longitude spacing. This assumption is violated by the
UNESCO corpus, and the simulation models exist precisely to relax it.

\subsection{Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing}
\label{sec:metrology_basis}

The harmonic spacing tested throughout this paper is 0.1~beru
(3\textdegree{} of arc), not a free parameter but the smallest
integer sub-division of the beru attested in cuneiform metrology
(\S\ref{sec:beru}).  The 0.1-beru interval equals exactly
3~\textit{u\v{s}} (3\textdegree{}), an integer multiple of the
smallest standard angular unit in the sexagesimal system
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
No alternative spacing was considered or tested before the primary
analysis, and the choice is pre-specified on metrological grounds alone.

The empirical unit sensitivity sweep
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}, Tables~\ref{tab:unitsweep}--\ref{tab:unitsweep_fine})
serves as verification that the metrological basis is empirically supported,
as it is the only spacing that produces joint significance in both the
domed/spherical monument population and the full UNESCO corpus.
A $-0.5\%$ shift from the canonical value collapses dome significance
from $p = 0.0005$ to $p > 0.11$, and a $\pm 0.3\%$ shift collapses
joint significance in both populations simultaneously.
Adjacent spacings (0.06, 0.07, 0.09, 0.12~beru) are non-significant
in both populations.
This sweep is reported as empirical corroboration of the pre-specified
metrological argument, not as an independent route to the same conclusion.

\subsection{Tier Classification (fixed before data analysis)}

Sites are classified by $\delta$, the angular deviation from the
nearest 0.1-beru harmonic (interval $\approx 3$\textdegree{}).
All tier boundaries and the six test populations were defined and
frozen before any beru values were computed. The corpus was not
compiled by the author and cannot be modified.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tier A++}: $\delta \leq 0.0002$~beru ($\leq 0.67$~km).
    $P_0 = 0.4\%$. Exploratory; reported descriptively.
  \item \textbf{Tier A+}: $\delta \leq 0.002$~beru ($\leq 6.7$~km).
    $P_0 = 4\%$. Primary test threshold.\footnote{$2 \times 0.002 / 0.1 = 4\%$:
    window width divided by harmonic spacing.}
  \item \textbf{Tier A}: $\delta \leq 0.010$~beru ($\leq 33$~km).
    $P_0 = 20\%$.
  \item \textbf{Tier B}: $\delta \leq 0.050$~beru.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Domed and Spherical Monuments (Test 2)}
\label{sec:methodscircular}

\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV web-page text.
All \NcircKeywords{} keywords are treated identically (positive whole-word
match, no context gating), making the population maximally pre-specified
and immune to any accusation of post-hoc selection bias.
Final population: $N = \NcircTotal$.
Tests: exact binomial A+ ($> 4\%$) and A ($> 20\%$), $\chi^2$
uniformity, anchor sweep.

A context-validated version of this test (Test~2x, Exploratory) applies
sentence-level architectural confirmation. Gate~1 (28 negative patterns)
rejects geological, vernacular, and non-architectural uses, while Gate~2
(86 positive patterns) requires architectural confirmation.
This reduces the population to $N = 83$
(\NcircContextRejected{} sites rejected; e.g.\ Uluru ``rock domes,''
Hiroshima ``Genbaku Dome''; full list in Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit_dome}).
The validated version yields $p = 0.0005$, $3.31\times$ enrichment, which is stronger
than the raw sweep ($p = 0.0009$, $3.06\times$), confirming that the
7 rejected sites are non-architectural matches diluting the signal, not
driving it.
Because the validation rules were developed after the initial keyword sweep,
Test~2x is classified Exploratory and excluded from the confirmatory
Bonferroni family. The raw-sweep p-value is the conservative, pre-specified
figure reported for Test~2.

\subsection{Hemispherical Mound Evolution (Test 2b [Sensitivity Variant])}
\label{sec:methodsevolution}

Extends the core domed/spherical test (Test~2) by adding earthen mound-stage keywords
(\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun, mound}).
The categories are grouped on the basis of shared hemispherical or
quasi-hemispherical geometry, a form widely associated with cosmological
and funerary symbolism across cultures, not on the basis of a claimed
architectural lineage.
The label ``evolution'' refers to a morphological sequence in the
statistical test design (mound $\to$ stupa $\to$ dome as progressively
formalized built expressions of the same form class); it is not a
claim that any category is architecturally derived from another.

The primary version (Test~2b) applies a raw positive keyword
sweep: all \NevoTotal{} Cultural/Mixed sites matching any keyword in
\{\textit{tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun, mound, stupa, stupas,
dagoba, chorten, tholos, dome, domed, domes, spherical}\} are included
without context gating, for the same pre-specification reasons as Test~2.
A context-validated version (Test~2bx, Exploratory) applies the same
architectural gates as Test~2x, reducing the population to $N = 126$.
The validated version yields $p = 0.0005$, $2.78\times$ enrichment, which is stronger
than the raw sweep ($p = 0.0009$, $2.65\times$), again demonstrating that
the extra sites in the raw sweep are diluting rather than driving the signal.

Test~2b is classified as a \emph{sensitivity variant} of Test~2 rather
than an independent hypothesis, because its population shares the entire
dome/stupa core ($N = \NcircTotal$) with Test~2 and adds only
\NevoMoundOnly{} mound-stage sites, which show no significant
enrichment on their own (\evoMoundApRate\%, $p = \pEvoMound$, ns).
It is not counted separately in the Bonferroni family ($k = \BonfK$).
Tests: exact binomial A+ on combined and per-stage populations.

\subsection{Cluster Asymmetry (Test 3)}

Full corpus ($N = \NclusterTotal$), no keyword filter.
If the beru-harmonic hypothesis is correct, ancient surveyors would have
preferentially founded or selected significant sites at harmonic longitudes,
and subsequent construction, urban growth, and heritage accumulation would
have clustered around those initial seed sites.
This predicts that Tier-A+ harmonics should host more UNESCO sites in
total, not just more A+ sites, than harmonics without any A+ hit.
For each site, count other UNESCO sites within $\pm 0.3$\textdegree{}
longitude.
Compare cluster sizes for A+ vs.\ non-A+ sites (Mann-Whitney $U$,
permutation test).
At the harmonic level, compare per-harmonic site counts for A+-bearing
vs.\ non-A+ harmonics.

\subsection{Founding-Site Enrichment (Test E [Exploratory])}
\label{sec:methodsfounding_origin}

Two independent keyword analyses on the full corpus ($N = \NoriginCorpus$),
no keyword filtering for the primary tests:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{World religion founding sites.}
    Keyword match for Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, Judaism,
    Hinduism, Zoroastrianism in site name/XML description.
  \item \textbf{UNESCO founding language.}
    Sites where UNESCO uses ``first capital,'' ``birthplace of,''
    ``founded in,'' etc.\ in the XML description.
\end{enumerate}
A context-aware keyword classifier
(\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py}) was applied to all sites.
This classifier was iteratively tuned after examining misclassified
sites (full disclosure in \S\ref{sec:filter_methodology}). Because
refinement occurred after data inspection, the enrichment result
from this classifier should be treated as \emph{Exploratory}.
The inscription-year gradient and world-religion sub-tests do not
depend on this classifier.

\subsection{Temporal Gradient (Test 4)}
\label{sec:methodstemporal}

Cochran-Armitage trend test on the full corpus ($N = \NoriginCorpus$),
no keyword filter.
A+ rate is tested for a monotonic decline as UNESCO inscription year
increases (earliest inscriptions reflect greatest site importance).
Three-cohort grouping (1978--1984, 1985--1999, 2000--2025) is primary;
five-cohort grouping (1978--1984, 1985--1991, 1992--1999, 2000--2009,
2010--2025) is secondary.
The three-cohort bins are formed by merging the two middle five-cohort
bins (1985--1991 and 1992--1999) into a single 1985--1999 bin.
Code: \texttt{analysis/unesco/temporal\_gradient\_test.py}.

\subsection{Multiple-Comparisons Protocol}
\label{sec:bonferroni}

Four pre-specified hypotheses (Tests~1, 2, 3, 4) are corrected as a single
Bonferroni family ($k = \BonfK$, family-wise $\alpha = 0.05$, per-test
threshold $\alpha / k = 0.0125$).
Test~E (founding-site classifier enrichment) is \emph{Exploratory} and
excluded from this family: the classifier (\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py})
was iteratively refined after data inspection, so its enrichment result is
post-hoc (\S\ref{sec:filter_methodology}).
Test~2b (hemispherical mound evolution) is classified as a
\emph{sensitivity variant} of Test~2 rather than an independent
hypothesis, because its population shares the entire dome/stupa core
with Test~2 and adds only the mound stage, which is non-significant
on its own (\evoMoundApRate\%, $p = \pEvoMound$, ns).
Test~2b therefore dilutes rather than extends the Test~2 signal,
and counting it separately in the Bonferroni family would artificially
inflate the number of ``surviving'' results.
It is reported as a robustness check showing that the Test~2 signal
is not driven by the dome/stupa keyword boundary.
Adjusted p-values are generated by \texttt{analysis/unesco/bonferroni\_correction.py}.

\textit{Test dependence.}
Tests~1 and~3 share the 56 A+ sites that drive both signals;
$k = \BonfK$ Bonferroni correction is therefore somewhat liberal for this pair.
However, the Bonferroni-surviving result that carries primary
weight, Test~2 (dome/spherical, $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$),
uses a morphologically filtered sub-corpus independent of Tests~1 and~3.

Test~1 (full corpus, $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$) survives
correction at $k = \BonfK$ ($p_\mathrm{adj} < 0.05$), though the margin
is modest.
A methodological asymmetry bears on the interpretation: Gerizim is
an external anchor not present in the 1011-site inscribed corpus, so
its own longitude does not contribute a data point when counting
quantized pairs.
Jerusalem, by contrast, is an inscribed corpus member and is
self-excluded from its own evaluation (N\,=\,1010).
Both anchors produce the same marginal result ($p \approx 0.010$),
and the corridor between them, the \GerizimJerusalemKm\,km window
that produces 56~A+ sites from either vantage point
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}), is the more robust finding,
although the corridor comparison itself is post-hoc.
Test~4 (temporal gradient, $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestFour$) does not
survive correction and is treated throughout as supporting evidence.

Results surviving Bonferroni correction:
dome/spherical ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$),
full corpus enrichment ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$),
cluster asymmetry ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestThree$).
A comprehensive FDR audit (\NtotalTests{} tests;
\citealt{benjamini1995controlling}) retains \NsurviveFDR{} at $q < 0.05$;
\NsurviveBonfAll{} survive Bonferroni at $k = \NtotalTests$.
All data, code, and random seeds are publicly archived
\citep{tenenbaum2026audit}.

\subsection{Keyword Filter Methodology and Post-Hoc Tuning Disclosure}
\label{sec:filter_methodology}

The dome/stupa filter (\S\ref{sec:methodscircular}) required minimal iteration:
the keyword set follows directly from architectural typology and is
effectively pre-specified.
The founding-site filter (\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py}) was
developed iteratively by examining misclassified sites.
All corrections used domain-level vocabulary; no site-specific
exclusions and no longitude-based decisions were made at any stage.
The founding-enrichment result ($p = \pFoundKwFisher$) is directly
affected by this tuning.
The three Bonferroni-surviving results (Tests~1, 2, 3) and the
keyword-independent sub-tests of Test~E and Test~4 (full-corpus enrichment,
dome population, hemispherical evolution, inscription-year gradient,
world-religion keywords, corridor precision, simulation nulls) are
not affected by the founding classifier in any way.
Full keyword lists, gate logic, and per-site audit trails are
deposited as supplementary material (Appendix~\ref{sec:kwdaudit}).

\subsection{Null Model Validation}
\label{sec:methods_simulation}

Three simulation approaches (code: \texttt{simulation\_null\_model.py},
100{,}000 trials each):
(1)~longitude permutation,
(2)~bootstrap from empirical distribution,
(3)~KDE-smoothed null.
Results in \S\ref{sec:simulation_null}.

As an independent external control, the UNESCO corpus is compared against
the Wikidata P1435 global heritage corpus
(property \texttt{P1435} = ``heritage designation'';
$N = \WDctrlN$ globally-distributed structures fetched via the Wikidata
Query Service in April~2026).
Unlike the UNESCO corpus, the P1435 dataset includes structures of
all significance levels and is geographically broader. It serves as a
baseline to test whether UNESCO enrichment reflects generic
heritage-site distribution rather than a corpus-specific signal.
Code: \texttt{data/scripts/fetch\_p1435\_global.py}.

The enrichment is not an artifact of geographic concentration.

\section{Robustness Checks}
\label{sec:robustness}

Results surviving Bonferroni correction ($k = \BonfK$):
domed/spherical ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$),
full corpus enrichment ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$),
harmonic cluster asymmetry ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestThree$).

evidence only.
Test~2b (hemispherical evolution) is a sensitivity variant of Test~2
and is not counted separately.

sensitivity sweep, is presented first (\S\ref{sec:metrology}),

\subsubsection*{The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic artifact}
\label{sec:x18artifact}

The global maximum A+ count recurs at every longitude of the form
$x.18$\textdegree{}E: all 120 anchors at this phase produce exactly
\anchorSweepGlobalMax{} A+ (Sweep~B) or \anchorSweepGlobalMaxA{} A+
(Sweep~A).
The one-site difference arises because Jerusalem
(35.2317\textdegree{}E, $\bmod\,3.0 = 2.23$\textdegree{}) falls near
this phase and is present only in Sweep~B.

This is a major structural feature of the UNESCO longitude distribution
and must be addressed directly.
The corpus already contains an inherent periodic pattern at
3\textdegree{} spacing, the same interval as 0.1~beru, that is a
mathematical property of where UNESCO sites happen to be concentrated,
not evidence for any particular historical meridian.
Any anchor near the $x.18$\textdegree{} phase will produce a high A+
count regardless of historical motivation.

Three consequences follow:
(1)~the raw global maximum is not meaningful as an anchor-selection
criterion, and the A+ count alone cannot distinguish a historically
motivated anchor from a lucky phase alignment;
(2)~Gerizim's phase ($\GerizimPhase$ mod~3.0\textdegree{}) is offset
from the optimal phase by \phaseGap\textdegree{} ($\sim$\phaseGapKm~km),
so Gerizim does not sit at the mathematical optimum, and its A+ count
(55--56) is slightly below the artifact maximum (59--60);
(3)~critically, the $x.18$\textdegree{}E artifact does \emph{not}
explain the sub-population findings that carry the primary
evidential weight: it does not predict that \emph{domed monuments
specifically} should concentrate at harmonics (the dome sub-population
shows $3.06\times$ enrichment while the full corpus shows only
$1.38\times$), that significance should collapse for sub-percent
deviations from the canonical 0.1-beru spacing
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}), or that the signal should be concentrated
in the earliest UNESCO inscription cohorts
(\S\ref{sec:resultstemporal}).
The morphological specificity, unit sensitivity, and temporal gradient
are independent of the phase artifact and constitute the primary
evidence against a purely structural explanation.

Code: \texttt{peak\_geography\_audit.py},
\texttt{verify\_x18\_periodicity.py}.

\subsubsection*{Dome-specific periodicity test}
\label{sec:dome_periodicity}

A reviewer might ask whether the dome sub-population's longitude
distribution carries the same inherent 3\textdegree{} periodicity as
the full corpus, in which case the $x.18$\textdegree{}E artifact could
explain Test~2 without invoking beru-specific placement.
This was tested explicitly
(\texttt{analysis/global/dome\_periodicity\_audit.py}).

\textbf{Part~1 (dome-specific anchor sweep).}
Sweeping all 36,000 trial anchors against the $N = \NcircTotal$ dome
sites only, Gerizim scores at the
\domeGerizimPctile\textsuperscript{th} percentile
(i.e.\ it ties for the global maximum in the dome sweep).
All \domeArtTotal{} $x.18$\textdegree{}E anchors score
$\leq \domeArtMaxAp$ A+, while Gerizim scores $\NcircTierAp$.
\textbf{None of the 120 artifact-phase anchors outperforms Gerizim}
(\domeArtBeatGer{} of \domeArtTotal{} beat Gerizim)---the $x.18$\textdegree{}E
phase provides no advantage over Gerizim when the sweep is restricted
to dome sites.

\textbf{Part~2 (phase distribution).}
Dome site phases (longitude $\bmod\,3.0$\textdegree{}) are
approximately uniform ($\chi^2 = \domePhaseChiSq$, df\,=\,9,
$p = \domePhaseChiP$, trend-level), in contrast to the full corpus,
which shows stronger phase non-uniformity
($p = \fullPhaseChiP$~*).
The KS test between dome and full-corpus phase distributions is
non-significant ($D = \domePhaseKSD$, $p = \domePhaseKSP$, ns),
indicating that dome sites share the full corpus's underlying
geographic distribution.

\textbf{Parts~3--4 (residual analysis).}
However, a more targeted test reveals a sharper finding: all
\NcircTierAp{} dome A+ sites have longitudes whose phase
(mod~3.0\textdegree{}) falls within $\pm 0.15$\textdegree{} of the
$x.18$\textdegree{} artifact window
(\domeArtifactN{} of the \NcircTotal{} dome sites fall in this band;
$\domeResidualAp/\domeResidualN$ A+ outside it;
$p_\mathrm{res} = \domeResidualP$, ns).
This means the dome A+ sites are concentrated precisely at the same
longitude phases that generate the full-corpus $x.18$\textdegree{} artifact.

\textbf{Two interpretations are consistent with this finding:}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{Artifact explanation.}
    Domed monuments are built in world regions (South Asia, the Near
    East, Central Asia, Italy) whose longitudes happen to cluster near
    the $x.18$\textdegree{} phase band.
    The dome enrichment would then be a consequence of the same
    geographic structure that drives the full-corpus artifact.

  \item \textit{Beru-alignment explanation.}
    If ancient builders placed domed monuments at beru harmonics from
    the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor, those sites would cluster at
    the very longitude phases the artifact captures.
    Under this reading, the $x.18$\textdegree{} artifact is a
    \emph{consequence} of widespread beru alignment rather than an
    independent confound.
    The fact that Gerizim's own phase (2.269\textdegree{}) lies
    within the enriched band (2.18\textdegree{}--2.33\textdegree{})
    is consistent with this.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Part~5 (morphological specificity within the band).}
To distinguish these, dome and non-dome sites within the
$x.18$\textdegree{} band are compared directly.
Within the band, dome sites show \domeBandRate\%{} A+
($N = \domeBandN$, $\domeBandAp$ hits) and non-dome sites show
\nonDomeBandRate\%{} A+ ($N = \nonDomeBandN$, $\nonDomeBandAp$ hits;
Fisher OR~$= \domeBandFisherOR$, $p = \domeBandFisherP$, ns).
Both groups are highly enriched within the band (confirming the
artifact), and their rates do not differ significantly.
This means the dome-specific $3.06\times$ enrichment reported in
Test~2 cannot be attributed to morphological specificity within the
band; the dome sites are enriched \emph{because} they fall in the band,
not because domed forms are selectively placed at beru harmonics above
and beyond what non-dome sites achieve at the same longitudes.

\textbf{Revised assessment.}
The original claim that ``the $x.18$\textdegree{}E artifact does not
explain the dome result'' must be qualified: all dome A+ sites fall
within the artifact window, and dome sites within the window are not
more enriched than non-dome sites within the same window.
The dome $3.06\times$ enrichment primarily reflects the fact that
domed monuments are concentrated in the Eurasian regions that generate
the $x.18$\textdegree{} artifact.
The unit-sensitivity result
(\S\ref{sec:metrology}) remains the most robust independent argument
against a pure artifact explanation, since the
collapse at $\pm 0.3\%$ unit deviation is not predicted by any
geographic concentration model.
Independent replication using a non-UNESCO dome corpus is the
necessary next step.

\textbf{Anchor and unit.}

a methodological asymmetry that slightly disadvantages Gerizim
relative to any inscribed anchor.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Keyword selection.}
Test~2 uses a mechanical morphological filter (full audit: \S\ref{sec:kwdaudit}).
No site was excluded on the basis of its beru deviation.
Test~3 requires no keywords.

\textbf{Non-uniform publication density.}
The simulation null (\S\ref{sec:simulation_null}) preserves the
observed longitude distribution; any density gradient affects A+ and
non-A+ sites equally.

\textbf{Anchor corridor.}
The full-corpus enrichment ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$) survives
Bonferroni correction at $k = \BonfK$, though the margin is modest.
A methodological asymmetry affects interpretation. Gerizim is not an
inscribed corpus member, so its own longitude does not contribute a
data point when counting quantized pairs and can only be assessed as
an external reference against the 1011 inscribed sites.
Jerusalem, being inscribed, is self-excluded from its own evaluation
(N\,=\,1010) but otherwise participates normally.
Both anchors produce $p \approx 0.010$ (raw), and the corridor between
them is stable at 56~A+ from either vantage point, placing the
$\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor in the global top~3\%
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}).
This corridor-level invariance carries more evidential weight than
either individual p-value because it demonstrates the signal is not
an artifact of the specific anchor; however, the corridor comparison
itself is post-hoc and was not part of the pre-specified test battery.
Gerizim was selected a priori on the basis of the \textit{tabbur ha-aretz}
geographic-centrality tradition \citep{macdonald1964,crown1989},
before any beru distances were computed; no optimization across
alternative Levantine anchors was performed.
The Lumbini precision differential is a post-hoc observation and
carries no weight in the pre-specified results.

\textbf{Founding-site classifier.}
Test~E uses \texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py}, which was iteratively
tuned after examining misclassified sites (\S\ref{sec:kwdaudit_founding}).
Because the classifier was refined after inspecting the data, the
enrichment result ($p = \pFoundKwFisher$) should be treated as
\emph{Exploratory}.
This reclassification has no effect on the confirmatory results:
the three Bonferroni-surviving results (Tests 1, 2, 3) use no
founding classifier.

\textbf{Spatial autocorrelation.}
Design-effect correction: $N_\mathrm{eff} = \NeffHalf$
(DEFF~$= \DEFFhalf$; corrected $p = \pNeffHalf$).
Block bootstrap CI $[\blockBootCIlo\%, \blockBootCIhi\%]$ excludes
the 4\% null.

\textbf{Americas as negative control.}
The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus ($N = 144$, A+ = 2.1\%, ns) shows
depletion consistent with the prediction, though depletion is also
expected for any Eurasian-origin grid applied to the Americas.
A larger Wikidata P1435 Americas corpus ($N = \AmericasCtrlN$,
A+ = \AmericasCtrlRate\%) confirms directional depletion.

\textbf{Geocoding precision.}
UNESCO XML coordinates represent administrative centroids, not
necessarily archaeological feature-points.
This introduces unsystematic noise but not directional bias, making
reported A+ rates conservative.

\textbf{Geographic and cultural selection bias.}
The UNESCO list over-represents Europe, and sites are concentrated
in the Mediterranean--Near Eastern--South Asian corridor.
This geographic concentration is the most important confound in the
study, because any longitude band that happens to be site-dense will
produce elevated hit rates under any periodic grid, regardless of
historical motivation.
The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic artifact
(\S\ref{sec:x18artifact}) demonstrates this directly. The UNESCO
longitude distribution already contains 3\textdegree{} periodicity
as a structural property, and $\sim$3\% of arbitrary anchors produce
A+ counts comparable to Gerizim's.

The full-corpus enrichment ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$) is
therefore the finding most vulnerable to this confound, since it
measures overall hit rates without controlling for site density.
Simulation nulls that preserve the observed longitude distribution
(permutation, bootstrap, KDE) absorb this fixed geographic bias
and confirm that the dome sub-population survives all three models
(permutation $Z = \simDomePermZ$, $p = \simDomePermP$).
However, the simulation nulls test whether the dome sites are
unusually concentrated at harmonics \emph{relative to the existing
UNESCO longitude distribution}; they do not test whether that
distribution itself is structured.

The primary evidence against a purely geographic explanation is
threefold.
First, the morphological specificity. Domed monuments show $3.06\times$
enrichment while the undifferentiated corpus shows only $1.38\times$.
A geographic artifact predicts no such typological concentration.
Second, the unit-sensitivity collapse. Significance drops to
non-significance for sub-percent shifts ($\pm 0.3\%$) from the
canonical 0.1-beru spacing, a property not predicted by any model of
geographic concentration.
Third, the temporal gradient. The signal is strongest in the earliest
inscription cohorts and weakest in later ones, contrary to what would be
expected if the pattern simply reflected the accumulation of globally
distributed sites.
None of these sub-findings are explained by the $x.18$\textdegree{}E
artifact or by European over-representation in the corpus.

\textbf{Wikidata P1435 control: interpretation caveats.}
The Wikidata P1435 corpus ($N = \WDctrlN$) serves as a directional
sanity check, confirming that the UNESCO enrichment is not a generic
property of all heritage databases, but it is not a matched control.
P1435 includes structures of all significance levels (local heritage
listings, minor listed buildings) and has a different geographic
distribution.
The significant UNESCO--P1435 difference
(OR~$= \WDctrlOR$, $p = \WDctrlZp$) could reflect genuine
corpus-specific structure or simply the different composition of the
two datasets.
This comparison should be interpreted as evidence against the null
hypothesis that all heritage lists show equal enrichment, not as
strong evidence for the beru hypothesis specifically.

\textbf{UNESCO listing and non-metrological correlates.}
Historical significance, a UNESCO selection criterion, partially
correlates with the predictors tested here.
The unit-sensitivity result, where sub-percent shifts collapse
joint significance, is the single finding most resistant to
any curation-based explanation, since no known UNESCO selection
process operates at 0.1-beru precision.

\textbf{Temporal gradient: alternative interpretation.}
The gradient ($p = \pCochranThree$, $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestFour$)
could reflect a UNESCO curation artifact in which the founding cohort
(1978--1984) is non-representative.
An additional confound is geographic composition. The founding canon is
concentrated in Europe and the Mediterranean corridor, precisely the
Eurasian heartland where a beru-harmonic effect would be strongest,
while later cohorts add proportionally more sites from regions where
the null hypothesis predicts depletion (Africa, Americas, Oceania).
The declining gradient could therefore partly reflect a shift in
regional composition rather than a temporal signal.
The Wikidata P1435 control provides indirect evidence against a purely
compositional account (enrichment is not a generic property of heritage
lists: OR $= \WDctrlOR$), and the dome result spans multiple continents
with inscriptions from 1988 to 2014.
Nevertheless, within-region cohort comparisons controlling for geography
are needed to fully isolate a temporal component. This is noted as a
limitation pending non-UNESCO replication.
The temporal gradient is treated as supporting evidence only throughout.

\textbf{Geographic artifact ($x.18$\textdegree{}E periodicity).}
\label{sec:xdot18_artifact}
The UNESCO longitude distribution contains an inherent 3\textdegree{}
periodic structure (the $x.18$\textdegree{}E artifact;
\S\ref{sec:x18artifact}), which inflates the A+ count for any anchor
near the optimal phase.
Gerizim does not sit at this optimum
($\Delta = \phaseGap$\textdegree{}, $\sim$\phaseGapKm~km offset).
The full-corpus enrichment is partially explained by this structural
feature.

The dome-specific periodicity audit (\S\ref{sec:dome_periodicity})
reveals that all \NcircTierAp{} dome A+ sites fall within the
$x.18$\textdegree{} phase window ($\pm 0.15$\textdegree{}), and
within that window dome sites are not significantly more enriched
than non-dome sites (OR~$= \domeBandFisherOR$, $p = \domeBandFisherP$).
The dome $3.06\times$ enrichment is therefore better attributed to
domed monuments being geographically concentrated in the Eurasian
regions that generate the $x.18$\textdegree{} artifact, rather than
to morphology-specific beru placement above and beyond that structure.
The unit-sensitivity result (\S\ref{sec:metrology})---where
$\pm 0.3\%$ shifts collapse joint significance---remains the finding
most resistant to a purely geographic explanation, since no known
geographic concentration model predicts sensitivity at sub-percent
angular precision.

\textbf{Metrological evidence: documentary vs.\ instrumental.}
No archaeological instrument explicitly calibrated in beru subdivisions
has been recovered; the evidence for beru-based angular practice is
entirely documentary (MUL.APIN, royal inscriptions, the Babylonian
\textit{Imago Mundi}, kudurru)
\citep{hunger2019mul,robson2008mathematics,powell1987metrological}.
For context, scaling a 1.665~cm Babylonian finger by $10^6$ yields
$\approx 166.5$~km, which matches the arc length subtended by
1.5\textdegree{} on a mean-Earth radius ($R = 6{,}371$~km:
$s = R\,\Delta\theta = 6{,}371 \times 0.02618 \approx 166.7$~km).
The $<0.2$~km ($\approx 0.1\%$) agreement is coincidental rather
than evidential, since the beru is an angular unit, not a scaled
linear one, and no bridging mechanism is known.
The point illustrates that the gap between small linear measures
and multi-degree angular spans requires an enormous scale factor
for which no documentary or instrumental record currently exists.
The beru hypothesis therefore rests on the textual and statistical
evidence presented here, not on any physical artefact chain.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

The central results are morphological and temporal.
Domed and spherical monuments ($N = \NcircTotal$, 12.2\% Tier-A+,
$p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$) show the strongest enrichment of any
sub-population, confirmed by simulation-based null models
(permutation $Z = \simDomePermZ$, $p = \simDomePermP$;
bootstrap $p = \simDomeBootP$).
The A+ rate declines from the earliest inscriptions
(1978--1984: \canonApRate\%) to recent ones
(2010--2025: \cohortErate\%, indistinguishable from the null).
Overall, \NclusterAp{} of \NclusterTotal{} sites (\clusterApRate\%)
fall in Tier-A+ windows
($p = \clusterApBinom$; $p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestOne$).
Both Gerizim and Jerusalem independently produce the same
$p \approx 0.010$, and the corridor between them, stable at 56~A+
from either anchor, ranks in the global top~3\%
(\S\ref{sec:sensitivity_global}).
This corridor-level invariance, although post-hoc, carries more
evidential weight than either individual-anchor p-value because it
shows the signal is not an artifact of the specific anchor choice.

The stupa stage ($5.56\times$, $p = \pEvoStupa$, $N = \NevoStupa$)
is the single most striking sub-population result.
The fact that stupa construction was institutionally coordinated under
the Mauryan state, which possessed a documented surveying
apparatus \citep{kangle1965arthashastra}, is consistent with
deliberate placement, though the small corpus may amplify noise.

\subsection{What the Results Do and Do Not Show}

The results establish a statistical pattern: an excess of UNESCO sites
near beru-harmonic longitudes from the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor,
concentrated in specific sub-populations.
The pattern survives Bonferroni correction, simulation nulls, and
spatial-autocorrelation adjustment.
The results do not, by themselves, establish a mechanism or demonstrate
intentional placement.
A statistical test can identify regularity but cannot determine whether
that regularity arose from deliberate surveying, cultural transmission,
latent geographic structure, or some combination of these.
The competing explanations are evaluated in \S\ref{sec:alt_explanations}.
The argument for metrological transmission rests on the failure of
alternatives against the data, not on the pattern alone.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\label{sec:alt_explanations}

Each alternative is stated and evaluated against the data.

\textbf{(1) Independent convergence.}
Hemispherical structures are a recurring architectural solution.
Independent convergence predicts no consistent longitude specificity.
The unit-sensitivity result directly constrains this, since the
clustering is locked to the 0.1-beru spacing and sub-percent shifts
collapse significance.

\textbf{(2) Engineering necessity.}
Terrain, hydrology, or resource access may attract monumental
construction at certain longitudes. This predicts enrichment across
all monument types, not specifically domed forms.
The morphological specificity and the unit-sensitivity collapse
are not properties of any terrain-driven model.

\textbf{(3) UNESCO curation bias.}
The list over-represents Europe and prestigious Eurasian sites.
The $x.18$\textdegree{}E periodic artifact (\S\ref{sec:x18artifact})
demonstrates that the UNESCO longitude distribution already contains
3\textdegree{} periodicity as a structural feature, and $\sim$3\%
of arbitrary anchors match Gerizim's A+ count.
The dome-specific periodicity audit (\S\ref{sec:dome_periodicity})
further shows that all \NcircTierAp{} dome A+ sites fall within the
$x.18$\textdegree{} phase window, and dome sites are not significantly
more enriched within that window than non-dome sites
(OR~$= \domeBandFisherOR$, $p = \domeBandFisherP$, ns).
This substantially strengthens the curation-bias alternative for
Test~2: the dome $3.06\times$ enrichment is consistent with domed
monuments being geographically concentrated in the Eurasian regions
that drive the $x.18$\textdegree{} artifact.
The remaining independent evidence against this account is the
unit-sensitivity collapse (\S\ref{sec:metrology}): the artifact predicts
no preference for any specific harmonic spacing, yet significance
collapses at $\pm 0.3\%$ from the canonical 0.1-beru value.
The Wikidata P1435 comparison ($N = \WDctrlN$, OR $= \WDctrlOR$)
provides a directional check that the UNESCO enrichment is not a generic
property of all heritage lists, though this control is not a matched
comparison (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\textbf{(4) Random coincidence.}
Three simulation approaches (permutation, bootstrap, KDE) preserve
the empirical longitude distribution and fail to reproduce the dome
enrichment (permutation $Z = \simDomePermZ$, $p = \simDomePermP$;
bootstrap $p = \simDomeBootP$).
The Bonferroni family ($k = \BonfK$) controls the family-wise error rate.

\textbf{Working assessment.}
Independent convergence is constrained by unit sensitivity,
engineering necessity by morphological specificity,
curation bias by external controls, and random coincidence by
simulation nulls.
No single alternative fully accounts for the combination of
morphological specificity, unit sensitivity, and temporal gradient.
The pattern is therefore consistent with structured placement
potentially reflecting metrological transmission, but cannot be
distinguished from other structured processes on statistical grounds
alone.
This is offered as a quantitative basis for further investigation,
not as a historical claim.

\subsection{Directions for Future Work}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Independent replication} using a non-UNESCO heritage corpus.
  \item \textbf{Regional sub-analyses} for Mesoamerican, North American, and Australian corpora.
  \item \textbf{Textual evidence} from cuneiform, Sanskrit, and Hellenistic geographic sources.
  \item \textbf{Latitude dimension}, though the geometric null becomes more complex on the sphere.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Conclusion}

The UNESCO corpus shows significant excess of Tier-A+ sites at 0.1-beru
harmonic longitudes from the $\sim$35\textdegree{}E corridor, concentrated
in domed/spherical monuments ($p_\mathrm{adj} = \pAdjTestTwo$) and in
the earliest inscriptions.
The unit-sensitivity collapse ($\pm 0.3\%$ collapses joint significance)
is the strongest single argument against statistical artifact;
the morphological specificity is the strongest argument against
geographic structure alone.
The temporal gradient provides directional evidence that the signal
is concentrated in older, more historically significant sites.

The pattern is consistent with structured placement reflecting
metrological knowledge, but the present study cannot distinguish
deliberate placement from other structured processes that might
produce longitudinal regularity.
The results establish a quantitative basis for investigation, not a
historical attribution.

\subsubsection*{What would constitute confirmation?}

Independent replication using a non-UNESCO heritage corpus
(e.g., a global archaeological database assembled without reference
to this hypothesis) would provide the strongest statistical evidence.
Beyond statistics, the hypothesis would gain substantially from:
(a)~cuneiform or other ancient texts explicitly assigning beru-longitude
coordinates to distant sites or describing a beru-based geographic grid;
(b)~archaeological recovery of survey instruments calibrated in beru
sub-divisions; or
(c)~textual evidence from intermediary traditions (Hellenistic,
Sanskrit, or Islamic geographic sources) referencing a Levantine-centered
angular reference frame.
Without such independent evidence, the pattern remains a statistical
observation awaiting historical explanation.

All data, code, and random seeds are publicly archived at
\url{https://github.com/SethTenenbaum/gerizim-geodetic-analysis}.

\section{Keyword Audit Summary}
\label{sec:kwdaudit}

This appendix summarizes the audit trail for the dome/spherical monument
filter (Test~2) and the founding-origin classifier (Test~E).
The full site-by-site audit, including exact UNESCO sentences, gate
decisions, and rejection rationale for all dome/spherical
sites and all \NfoundKwAll{} founding-classified sites, is deposited
as online supplementary material and is reproducible by running:\\
\texttt{python3 analysis/unesco/spherical\_monument\_raw\_sweep.py}\\
\texttt{python3 analysis/unesco/spherical\_monument\_test.py --audit}

\subsection{Dome/Spherical Monument Filter (Test~2 and Test~2x)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_dome}

\NcircKeywords{} morphological keywords from four roots
(\textit{stupa/stupas}, \textit{tholos}, \textit{dome/domed/domes},
\textit{spherical}) are matched as whole words against each site's
name, XML description, and extended OUV text.

\textbf{Test~2 (primary confirmatory):} All \NcircTotal{} matching sites
are included without any context gating.  This raw positive sweep is the
confirmatory version because the population is fully determined by the
keyword list alone, with no post-hoc filtering decisions.

\textbf{Test~2x (Exploratory robustness check):} Unambiguous keywords
(\textit{stupa, stupas, tholos}) are accepted without context validation.
Ambiguous keywords (\textit{dome, domed, domes, spherical}) are
subject to a two-gate context check:
Gate~1 (28 negative patterns covering geology, vernacular housing,
non-architectural objects, and war memorials) rejects non-architectural
uses; Gate~2 (86 positive patterns covering building types,
architectural elements, construction terms, and period/style markers)
requires architectural confirmation.
A site is included if at least one matching sentence passes both gates.
Full gate lists: \texttt{lib/dome\_filter.py}.

Of the \NcircTotal{} raw-sweep sites, \NcircContextRejected{} are
rejected by context validation in Test~2x: Hiroshima Peace Memorial
(``dome'' refers to the pre-war industrial exhibition hall;
UNESCO inscribes the site solely as a peace memorial, not as
architectural heritage), Richtersveld (pastoral shelters), Gyeongju
(earthen tumuli, captured separately in Test~2b), Vi\~{n}ales Valley
(karst formations), Rock Islands Southern Lagoon (natural coral),
Uluru-Kata Tju\d{t}a (geological monoliths), and Diqu\'{i}s Stone
Spheres (carved petrospheres).
In every case, the exclusion is driven by the semantic domain of the
keyword match, not by any property of the site's longitude.
Test~2x yields $N = 83$, $p = 0.0005$, $3.31\times$ enrichment, an
equal-or-stronger signal than the raw sweep, confirming that the
7 rejected sites are longitude-diluting false positives, not true hits.

\subsection{Founding-Origin Classifier (Test~E)}
\label{sec:kwdaudit_founding}

The founding-origin classifier (\texttt{lib/founding\_filter.py})
assigns sites to five categories: Founding Capital (F), Sacred Origin
(S), Founding Monument (M), Founding Axis (X), and Ancient Continuous
Landscape (L).
Each category uses unambiguous keywords (accepted on sight) and
ambiguous keywords (requiring positive and negative context gates).
Full keyword lists: \texttt{config.json}.

This classifier was iteratively tuned by examining misclassified
sites (\S\ref{sec:filter_methodology}).
Tuning required one pass of keyword-grouped corrections;
no site was excluded individually, and no longitude-based decisions
were made at any stage.
The complete version history is preserved in the project's
\texttt{git} commit log and is auditable at the public repository
(\url{https://github.com/SethTenenbaum/gerizim-geodetic-analysis}).
Tuning corrections used domain-level vocabulary (no site-specific
exclusions, no longitude-based decisions).
The founding-enrichment result ($p = \pFoundKwFisher$) is directly
affected by this tuning; the inscription-year gradient and
world-religion tests are not.

Known false negatives (e.g., Florence, Timbuktu) affect A+ and
non-A+ sites equally; the automated enrichment test is a conservative
lower bound.
The founding-site evidence that does not depend on keyword
classification, namely the inscription-year gradient ($p = \pCochranThree$)
and world-religion keyword match ($p = \pReligUnion$), both reach
significance independently.
