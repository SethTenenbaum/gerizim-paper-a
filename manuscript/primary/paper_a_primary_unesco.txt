Abstract
[ABSTRACT] A one-dimensional angular enrichment test is applied to the
UNESCO World Heritage Cultural and Mixed corpus (N = 1011) using the 0.1-bēru
interval (3°), the smallest integer subdivision of the Babylonian unit (MUL.APIN,
c. 1000 BCE). Mount Gerizim (35.269°E) serves as anchor on historical grounds
(§6.1). Domed monuments (N = 90) within ≤ 0.00321 bēru (∼ 10.7 km) of a
harmonic node show significant enrichment (Bonferroni padj = 0.015, k = 3), and
cluster asymmetry independently reaches significance (padj = 0.001). The anchor is
historically motivated; the pipeline is fully reproducible.
Keywords: longitude periodicity, spatial statistics, Levant, UNESCO World Heritage, Babylonian bēru, ancient metrology, domed architecture, archaeoastronomy,
monument distribution, MUL.APIN

1

Introduction

This study applies a one-dimensional angular enrichment test to the externally
curated UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus (N = 1011), using a reproducible
computational pipeline (see the Data Availability Statement). The approach employs
a geometric null with no free parameters and is intended as a reusable framework
for spatial pattern-detection studies in archaeological heritage data.
Sites within ≤ 0.00321 beru (∼ 10.7 km, see §3.4) of a harmonic node are
designated Tier-A+; the geometric null rate is 6.42%. The full corpus yields 88 A+
sites against an expected 64.9 under this null. The excess of roughly 23 sites is
modest globally but concentrates in identifiable sub-populations. Domed monuments
carry a disproportionate share (Test 2), and harmonics with a precisely aligned site
are 4.45× more densely populated overall (Test 3).
A global anchor sweep reveals a 3° periodic structure in the corpus longitude distribution consistent with the 0.1-beru unit; the full treatment and its methodological
implications are in §5.3 and §6.1.

Background

2.1

The Beru Unit

The beru (30° of arc) is attested in the MUL.APIN cuneiform compendium (Hunger
& Steele, 2019; Watson & Horowitz, 2011), dated to the late second or early first
millennium BCE. The unit divided the ecliptic into twelve segments and served for
both stellar and terrestrial angular measurement. The terrestrial use is explicit in
MUL.APIN II ii 43–iii 15. The sub-division into 30 uš (1 uš = 1°) is well attested
(Hunger & Steele, 2019; Robson, 2008), and the 0.1-beru interval (3°) tested here
corresponds to exactly 3 uš.
The beru also functioned as a unit of linear territorial description: royal inscriptions record inter-regional distances in beru, kudurru (boundary stones) recording
territorial extents were deposited in temples as legally authoritative records (Robson,
2008; Powell, 1987), and the Babylonian Imago Mundi marks distances beyond the
Salt Sea as beru multiples. Systematic meridian transit observation and eclipse
timing, both attested in the cuneiform record (Hunger & Steele, 2019; Neugebauer,
1975), are sufficient to derive inter-site angular separations at 1° resolution; the
gnomon, attested across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and Indian traditions (Neugebauer, 1975), provides an independent means of establishing true meridian. This
combination provides the documentary basis for the hypothesis.

2.2

Ancient Metrology and Geodetic Practice

Whether pre-modern societies employed standardized angular measures across large
geographic scales has a long and contested history. Thom’s “megalithic yard”
(Thom, 1967, 1971, 1964) stimulated statistical refinement (Kendall, 1974) and
methodological critique (Freeman, 1976). Powell’s treatment of Mesopotamian
metrology (Powell, 1987) remains the standard reference. More recently, Magli
(Magli, 2013, 2016) has applied Monte Carlo methods to archaeoastronomical
alignments, and Ruggles and Silva have advanced probability-based frameworks
for evaluating orientation claims (Ruggles, 1999, 2015; Silva, 2020). The ICOMOSIAU thematic study (Ruggles & Cotte, 2010) provides institutional context for
archaeoastronomical analysis of World Heritage properties. Medieval cartographic
traditions also employed directional reference systems anchored to sacred geography,
with Jerusalem and Mecca serving as orienting meridians in mappamundi and
qibla charts (Harvey, 1987), providing historical context for the Levantine corridor
hypothesis.

2

2.3

The Anchor: Mount Gerizim

Mount Gerizim (35.269°E) is the primary reference anchor because of its documented
tabbur ha-aretz (“navel of the earth”) role in Samaritan cosmographic tradition
(Purvis, 1968; MacDonald, 1964; Crown, 1989), analogous to Babylon’s meridian in
cuneiform astronomical records (Hunger & Steele, 2019). As a prominent mountain
(881 m) in the central highlands, it would have served as a natural survey reference
point. No optimization across alternative Levantine anchors was performed.
Gerizim’s tentative-list coordinate (ref. 5706, DMS E35 16 08) is externally
sourced and does not appear in the UNESCO inscribed corpus (UNESCO World
Heritage Centre, Tentative List ref. 5706, State of Palestine, submitted 02 April 2012;
archived at https://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5706/). Jerusalem
(35.2317°E), approximately 4.1 km east of Gerizim, is an inscribed corpus member.
It is self-excluded when used as anchor and produces a similar A+ count (§5.3).
Both lie within the Levantine corridor (∼35°E, Jordan Valley to Levant), and the
enrichment belongs to this corridor, not to the specific anchor within it. Mecca
(p ≈ 0.1358, ns), Mt. Meru (p ≈ 0.4120, ns), and Mt. Kailash (p ≈ 0.1972, ns) do
not approach significance.

3

Data and Methods

3.1

Corpus Construction

The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset (UNESCO World Heritage Centre,
2024) contains 1248 inscribed nominations across all categories. Two outcome-blind
filters yield the analysis corpus: (1) Natural-only sites are excluded (−235, retaining
Cultural and Mixed); (2) entries lacking geocoordinates are excluded (−2). The
result is N = 1011 sites with valid longitudes.

3.2

Geodetic Framework

The reference anchor is Mount Gerizim at 35.269°E, from Palestine’s UNESCO
tentative list (ref. 5706, 2012; see §2.3). Gerizim is not on the inscribed World
Heritage List and therefore does not appear in the standard corpus. Jerusalem is
an inscribed corpus member, scored as a regular site in Tests 1–4 (A+ at 4.1 km
under Gerizim). For the anchor-comparison audit and both sensitivity sweeps (§5.3),
Gerizim is added as a synthetic corpus entry so that each anchor can self-exclude
symmetrically: Gerizim removes itself and tests against the N = 1011 inscribed
sites (including Jerusalem); Jerusalem removes itself and tests against the same N
(now including Gerizim).
For a site at longitude λ, the beru-distance is B = |λ − 35.269|/30 and the
beru-deviation from the nearest 0.1-beru harmonic is δ = |B − round(B, 0.1)|. The
tier classification is a fixed angular criterion. Kilometer labels (e.g. ≲ 10.7 km for
Tier-A+) are equatorial approximations computed as δ × 30 × 111 km/°, becoming
smaller at higher latitudes. This makes the angular criterion conservative at high
latitudes. A geodesic sensitivity check confirms that applying a strict physical
threshold would increase the A+ count to 109, strengthening all primary results.
No latitude correction is applied.
The geometric null for Tier-A+ is 2 × 0.00321/0.1 = 6.42%, assuming uniform longitudes. This serves as a convenient reference, but the primary tests are

3.3

Metrological Basis for the 0.1-Beru Spacing

The harmonic spacing tested is 0.1 beru (3°), the smallest integer sub-division of the
beru attested in cuneiform metrology, equalling exactly 3 uš (Hunger & Steele, 2019;
Robson, 2008; Powell, 1987). It was selected because it is the finest metrologically
attested integer sub-division that yields a tractable test (120 harmonics, 6.42%
geometric null). Coarser spacings (0.5 beru, 1 beru) are uninformative; finer subdivisions (0.02 beru) do not produce the signal (§5.1). No spacing was selected
post-hoc.
The 0.05-beru spacing (1.5°) also reaches significance, but this is structurally
expected because every 0.10-beru (3°) node is also a node of the finer 0.05-beru grid;
hence 0.05-beru is a finer sub-sampling of the same periodicity, not an independent
signal.

3.4

Tier Classification

Metrological basis for the Tier boundaries. Thureau-Dangin (Thureau-Dangin,
1921, p. 133) derives the linear distance-beru (French “la lieue”) from 180 aslu (French
“la corde”) of 59.4 m each, giving 180 × 59.4 = 10,692 m. At 111 km/°, 10,692 m
equals 0.00321 angular-beru. One-half a distance-beru is one parasang (Powell 1987,
p. 467), and one barid is four parasangs (Burton 1893, vol. 2, p. 63, n. 2).
The same longitude offset corresponds to a smaller ground distance at higher
latitudes. The km values are only approximate, while the beru criterion is the
primary, latitude-independent measure. No cuneiform text explicitly prescribes
these exact tolerances, but they are grounded in Thureau-Dangin’s metrological
values.
Tier A++: One equatorial parasang, δ ≤ 0.0016 beru (≲ 5.33 km), P0 =
3.2%. Tier A+: One equatorial Akkadian beru, δ ≤ 0.00321 beru (≲ 10.7 km),
P0 = 6.42% (i.e. 2 × 0.00321/0.1), primary threshold. Tier A: One equatorial
barid, δ ≤ 0.00942 beru (≲ 31 km), P0 = 18.84%. Tier B: the middle zone
is neither near a harmonic nor near a midpoint. The C-band is the symmetric
mirror of the A-band, measured as δmid = 0.050 − δ (distance from the nearest
inter-harmonic midpoint rather than from the nearest harmonic): Tier C: δmid ≤
0.00942 beru (≲ 31 km), P0 = 18.84%. Tier C−: δmid ≤ 0.00321 beru (≲
10.7 km), P0 = 6.42%. Tier C−−: δmid ≤ 0.0016 beru (≲ 5.33 km), P0 = 3.2%,
descriptive only. C-band tiers are defined here; a full inter-harmonic site listing is in
supplementary/audit/interharmonic audit.txt.

3.5

Test Populations

Keyword populations are approximate. The raw sweep tolerates known false positives
while the context-validated variant (Test 2x) applies sentence-level gating for greater
precision. Both populations are reported.
Test 1 (full corpus, primary). Full corpus (N = 1011), no keyword filter;
binomial test of whether the global A+ rate exceeds the 4% geometric null.
Test 2 (dome/spherical, primary). 7 morphological keywords from four roots
(stupa/stupas, tholos, dome/domed/domes, spherical ) are matched as whole words

4

against each site’s name, XML description, and extended OUV text (N = 90). This
raw sweep is over-inclusive: it admits sites where the keyword refers to geological or
vernacular features rather than monumental architecture. The context-validated
population (N = 83) is reported alongside all primary results; it yields stronger
significance, consistent with the rejected sites being non-architectural false positives.
Test 2b (mound evolution, sensitivity variant). Extends Test 2 by adding
earthen mound-stage keywords (tumulus, tumuli, barrow, barrows, kofun). Not
counted separately in the Bonferroni family.
Test 3 (cluster asymmetry, primary). Full corpus (N = 1011), no keyword
filter; tests whether A+ harmonics host more total sites than non-A+ harmonics,
independent of any morphological keyword.
Test 4 (temporal gradient, descriptive). Cochran-Armitage trend test on
the full corpus based on year of inscription into the UNESCO corpus, no keyword
filter.
Test 5 (founding classifier, post-hoc). A context-aware keyword classifier
(lib/founding filter.py), refined after initial data inspection, addresses what
kinds of sites beyond domed monuments fall on harmonic nodes; it carries no weight
in the primary evidential chain (Appendix A.2).

3.6

Multiple-Comparisons Protocol

Primary test family (k = 3).
• Test 1 (full corpus, N = 1011): global enrichment above 6.42% null.
• Test 2 (domed/spherical monuments, N = 90): morphological sub-population
enriched above 6.42% null.
• Test 3 (cluster asymmetry, full corpus, no keyword filter): A+-bearing harmonics host more total sites.
All three pass the Bonferroni bound at k = 3 (full corpus padj = 0.008 **, dome
padj = 0.015 *, cluster padj = 0.001 ***).
Other tests. Test 4 (temporal gradient, Cochran-Armitage) is descriptive
context; a Mantel-Haenszel stratified test and logistic regression with region as a
covariate assess the gradient robustly (§4.5); it is not part of the primary family.
Test 2b is a sensitivity variant of Test 2. Test 5 (founding classifier) is post-hoc
(Appendix A.2). World religion sub-group analyses (§4.1) are exploratory. Tests A–D
(§4.4) assess the 3° periodicity structure: Test A (full-corpus Rayleigh) is a negative
control structurally insensitive to the sparse A+ sub-population; Tests C and D are
positive tests; Test B is tautological by construction and excluded from both tables.

3.7

Simulation Null Models

Three simulation approaches (code: simulation null model.py, 100,000 trials
each) and two dedicated geographic-concentration nulls are described with their
results in §5.5.

UNESCO sites within ±0.3° longitude; compare cluster sizes for A+ vs. non-A+ sites

Methodological note: Rayleigh phase-lock (Test B). The near-perfect
Rayleigh R of the A+ sub-sample is a mathematical consequence of the A+ classification criterion (§1) and is reported as a structural description, not independent evidence. Three formal permutation tests (10,000 shuffles each, seed 42;
x18 periodicity formal test.py) address distinct aspects of the periodicity:
Test A (full-corpus Rayleigh, negative control). The full-corpus Rayleigh
R = 0.0224 (Z = 1.06, pperm = 0.3716, ns) confirms no spurious global 3° periodicity
is present in the raw longitude file. The signal is concentrated in A+ sites, not a
global artifact.
Test B (A+-only Rayleigh, structural). Among the 88 Tier-A+ sites,
R = 0.9935 (essentially all A+ sites share the same phase modulo 3°; Z = 19.00,
pperm < 0.0001). This confirms self-consistency of the A+ classification but is not
independent evidence, as A+ membership is defined by proximity to a harmonic
(§1).
Test C (circular-shift anchor sensitivity). Displacing the anchor by a
uniform random offset in [0◦ , 360◦ ) and recounting A+ sites yields a null mean
of 64.82 vs. the observed 88 (Z = 2.67, pperm = 0.0194, *). This is a positive
anchor-specific test complementary to Test D.
Test D (targeted von Mises mixture, full corpus). Test A is an omnibus
test over all N = 1011 sites; the ≈97% near-uniform background dilutes the A+
phase signal, making it structurally insensitive to the small but concentrated A+
sub-population. Test D addresses this directly. We fit a von Mises/uniform twocomponent mixture to all N = 1011 folded angles, with µ0 fixed at the Gerizim
phase on the 3° circle; ŵ and κ̂ are estimated by EM. The likelihood-ratio statistic
D = 2(ℓ̂ − ℓ0 ) is referred to a parametric bootstrap (B = 200 null samples) for an
empirical p-value. Fixing µ rather than searching it targets the test at the alternative
of interest, retaining sensitivity to cluster weights of a few percent. On the full
corpus: D = 8.6103, ŵ = 0.0312 (i.e. ≈3.1% of all sites), κ̂ = 36.70 (angular SD
≈ 0.0788°), pboot = 0.0199 (*). On the A+ subset alone: D = 454.7491, ŵ = 1.0000,
κ̂ = 76.63 (angular SD ≈ 0.0545°), pboot = 0.0010 (***). Together, Tests B–D
provide convergent, methodologically independent evidence that the 3° periodicity
at the Gerizim anchor is a real structural feature of the UNESCO corpus and not a
statistical artifact.

5

Robustness Checks

5.1

Unit Sensitivity Sweep

Varying harmonic spacing from 0.05 to 0.20 beru (physical threshold fixed at
10.7 km), the 0.1-beru spacing produces the strongest joint significance across
both the dome and full-corpus populations. The 0.05-beru and 0.20-beru spacings
also reach significance, but both are mathematical consequences of the 0.10-beru
structure: every 0.10-beru harmonic is also a 0.05-beru harmonic, and 0.20-beru
harmonics are a strict subset, so neither constitutes an independent signal. Adjacent
non-harmonic spacings (0.06, 0.07, 0.09, 0.11, 0.12 beru) are non-significant in either
population, which is the key discriminating observation. The 0.08-beru spacing
reaches nominal dome significance (p = 0.0330) but not full-corpus (p = 0.1956, ns),
reflecting unsystematic local overlap with the 0.10-beru grid rather than a genuine
secondary periodicity. Specifically, 6/12 (50.0%) of 0.08-beru dome hits align to
0.10-beru, indicating the significance is due to overlap.
A degree-denominated Monte Carlo sweep (§6.2) confirms this pattern: candidate
intervals at 4° and 8° reach nominal dome significance, but inspection shows that 6
of 7 and all 4 of their hits respectively fall on 12° and 24° nodes shared with the
3° grid, confirming they are consequences of the primary periodicity rather than
independent angular units.

5.2

Sensitivity Slope at the Canonical Unit

A fine sweep within ±1% of 0.10 beru (fine sweep audit.py) shows a ±0.3% shift
collapses joint significance in at least one population. Geographic concentration
alone reproduces the sharp-peak and canonical-unit ranking (p = 0.1037, ns); the
sensitivity slope provides no independent evidential value beyond the primary tests.
The 3° periodicity is independently confirmed by Tests C and D (§4.4).

5.3

Global Anchor Sweep

A global sweep (0–360°E, 0.01° resolution, 36,000 trial anchors) assesses whether the
∼35°E corridor is unusual globally. The sweep uses the extended corpus described
in §3.2 (Next = 1012): each trial anchor self-excludes any entry within 0.001°, so
every focal anchor tests against N = 1010.
Both anchors place the corridor in the global top 2.0% (36,000 trial anchors,
0–360°E; Gerizim: 88 A+, 98.0th percentile; Jerusalem: 88 A+, 98.0th percentile;
full table in Online Supplementary Table S4), and the result is anchor-invariant
within the 4.1 km corridor window. No other historical meridian reaches significance
except Mecca (74 A+, p = 0.1358 † ).

11

The x.18°E periodic structure
The most striking result of the global sweep is that every longitude of the form
x.18°E (all 120 such anchors) produces identically the maximum A+ count. The
spacing between consecutive x.18°E anchors is exactly 3° = 0.1 beru.
The label “x.18°E” is a rounded shorthand for the phase ≈ 2.18° mod 3.0°.
Gerizim’s phase (35.269 mod 3.0 = 2.269°, gap 0.059° ≈ 5.6 km) is within coordinate
uncertainty.
The choice of Gerizim was made on historical grounds (§2.3); the corridor result
is anchor-invariant within the band.
Non-independence note. The 3° periodicity and the unit sensitivity sweep
(§5.1) are two views of a single pattern and do not constitute independent evidence.
Neither provides evidential value beyond the dome enrichment (Test 2) and cluster
asymmetry (Test 3).
Two interpretations apply: (1) a global grid with 3° spacing anchored to the
∼35°E corridor would produce exactly this pattern; (2) Eurasian heritage sites
cluster in longitude bands that happen to align with the x.18° phase — this cannot
be refuted on the UNESCO corpus alone.
Code: peak geography audit.py, verify x18 periodicity.py.

Formal statistical tests for the x.18° periodicity
The full-corpus Rayleigh (pperm = 0.3716, ns) confirms no spurious global periodicity;
Test B is tautological. Tests C (pperm = 0.0194, *) and D (pboot = 0.0199, *) are
positive anchor-specific tests; discovery correction (Test E, p = 0.0564, † ) places the
optimum within the expected range over 36,000 anchors. The unit sensitivity sweep
(§5.1) and Monte Carlo unit sweep (§6.2) independently confirm 3° as the dominant
period without assuming any particular unit.
The dome-periodicity audit (dome periodicity audit.py) is consistent with
beru-alignment but equally consistent with geographic concentration: Gerizim
scores at the 97.3th percentile among dome sites, dome site phases are approximately
uniform (χ2 = 14.89, df = 9, p = 0.0940, trend-level), and all 13 dome A+ sites have
phases within ±0.15° of the x.18° optimal phase. The dome enrichment surviving
all three simulation nulls remains the primary evidence against a purely geographic
account.
Corridor and landmark ranking. Of the 18 A+ sites unique to Gerizim and
19 unique to Jerusalem, 38 are shared; the enrichment belongs to the corridor, not
to either anchor individually. Using each UNESCO site as its own anchor, Gerizim
ranks #26 of 1012 (top 2.8%).

5.4

Spatial Autocorrelation

A+ harmonics host 4.45× as many total UNESCO sites as non-A+ harmonics
(p < 0.0001). DEFF correction via ICC (±0.5°, ρ = 0.110): DEFF = 1.495,
Neff = 676, corrected p = 0.011. Block bootstrap (3° blocks, 100,000 resamples):
95% CI [6.69%, 10.59%] excludes the 6.42% null. Within-group permutation using
UNESCO-region × longitude bins reproduced the observed A+ count exactly in
every trial, confirming this test cannot discriminate alignment from geographic
concentration; the Null A and Null C simulations (§4.2) address that question
directly.

5.5

Null Model Validation

Three simulation nulls (100,000 trials each, seed = 42, Good 2005): (1) Longitude
permutation: dome (N = 83) pperm = 0.095 (**); 1978-1984 canon (N = 138)
p = 0.419 ns; pre-2000 (N = 501) p = 0.136 († ). (2) Bootstrap from full corpus:
dome pboot = 0.106 (*). (3) KDE-smoothed null: expected 64.9 ± 7.8, observed 88,
Z = 2.96, p = 0.003 (*). Dome enrichment survives all three models. The geographicconcentration nulls (Null A, Null C; §4.2) show that proximity to harmonic longitudes
is necessary but not sufficient to explain the dome enrichment.

5.6 Pipeline Portability: Wikidata Q180987 Stupa Corpus
The pipeline was applied to N = 229 Wikidata-attested stupas (class Q180987)
independently of the UNESCO corpus (Tenenbaum, 2026). The Kedu Plain (Java)
hosts the densest cluster at the 2.5-beru harmonic, anchored by Borobudur (c.
800 CE, 7.23 km, Tier A+) (Miksic, 1990); the World Peace Pagoda at Lumbini
achieves A++ precision (∼555 m) uniquely from Gerizim.
Using the identical anchor and null, the corpus yields 67/229 Tier-A sites (29.3%,
1.55× null; binomial p < 0.001, ***; permutation Z = 1.60, pperm = 0.075, ∼). No
Tier-A+ enrichment is found (p = 0.102, ns), consistent with cluster-level rather than
individual-site precision. Harmonic nodes with at least one Tier-A site carry 2.56×
more stupa sites on average than empty nodes, mirroring the cluster-asymmetry
result of Test 3.
Geographic tier breakdown and bimodal structure. A sub-regional
breakdown reveals a bimodal pattern consistent with the Buddhist A/C enrichment
in the UNESCO religion audit (§4.1). The Java/Sumatra node (105°–115°E, near
the 2.5-beru harmonic at 110.3°E) concentrates 42 of 83 sites at Tier-A (50.6%, OR
= 4.96×, p = 0.000 ***), rising to 41/54 (75.9%, OR = 18.07×, p = 0.000 ***) in the
107°–112°E window, where A+ is also elevated at 20.4% (OR = 4.72×, p = 0.002 **).
The physical heartland, India/Nepal (70°–90°E) and Myanmar/Cambodia (90°–
105°E), is A-tier depleted (OR = 0.15–0.68×, ns) but strongly enriched in the interharmonic C-band: Myanmar/Cambodia reaches 34.3% (OR = 4.18×, p = 0.000 ***)
and the combined heartland 28.3% (OR = 5.76×, p = 0.000 ***). The anticorrelation is consistent with a grid-level account (see §7.2). The non-heartland
Wikidata stupas (outside 70°–110°E, N = 102) show the same A-enriched, C-depleted
profile as the UNESCO domed monument corpus: 49.0% reach Tier-A (OR = 6.22×,
p = 0.000 ***) with no C-band enrichment (0.20×, p = 1.000 ns), suggesting that
UNESCO inscription preferentially captures the most precisely aligned examples
within each tradition.

Limitations

6.1

Circularity: Full Canonical Treatment

Pre-registration cannot address circularity for analyses of permanent public
databases. The UNESCO coordinate file and Mount Gerizim are both fully public,
so any investigator could run this pipeline at any time and temporal priority is
unverifiable. The relevant questions are whether the pattern exists and whether the
anchor was chosen on independent grounds.
Both are answered directly. The global anchor sweep (§5.3) confirms that the
enrichment is a property of the ∼35°E corridor. Every x.18°E anchor produces the
same result, and Gerizim and Jerusalem both place the corridor in the global top
2.0% of 36,000 trial anchors. Historical justifications could be constructed post-hoc
for many high-scoring anchors in the global sweep. What distinguishes Gerizim is
pre-existing documentary evidence, the tabbur ha-aretz cosmographic role (§2.3),
that predates this analysis entirely.
The 3° periodic structure is recoverable by any unanchored 120-phase scan
without reference to Gerizim. The dome enrichment (Test 2) is identical across all
120 x.18°E anchors. The cluster asymmetry (Test 3) requires no keyword filter and
no anchor. Test B is tautological by construction (§4.4) and is noted as such.
The Levantine corridor statistics are reported as the motivated entry point,
not independent confirmation. This ranking does not survive the 36,000-anchor
discovery correction (Test E, p = 0.0564, † ). The primary evidence is Tests 2 and 3,
which require no anchor.

6.2

Geographic and Cultural Selection Bias

The UNESCO list over-represents Europe and the Mediterranean-to-South-Asian
corridor of Cultural/Mixed sites. The regional distribution is as follows: (Europe
& North America: 50%; Arab States: 9%; Asia-Pacific: 23%; Latin Americas:
11%; Africa: 7%). Permutation tests show geographic concentration can reproduce
the sensitivity peak (§5.2), but domed monuments exceed the A+ rate of other
monuments from the same longitude footprint (Null C, p = 0.0394, *), establishing
that the enrichment is carried by a specific building type rather than the geographic
distribution alone. Although UNESCO nomination criteria make no reference to
longitude, it cannot be ruled out that the inscription process carries some of the
same longitudinal structure observed here.
A Monte Carlo unit sweep (10000 permutations, 18 candidate spacing values from
0.5° to 15°) identifies which angular interval produces clustering beyond geographic
concentration without assuming any particular unit. The dominant peak is at 3°
(p < 0.001), with harmonics at 6° and 12° also significant; adjacent candidates 2.5°
and 3.5° do not reach significance. Collapsing harmonics, the dome subset yields 1
independent dominant spacings (P = 0.1951 under the geographic null), supporting
3° as the period to which domed monuments are preferentially aligned.

6.3

Americas Negative Control

The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus (N = 145) shows directional depletion (A+
= 2.1%, p = 0.4102, ns) relative to the 6.42% null; the result is directional but
uninformative at this sample size.

14

6.4

Other Limitations

Geocoding precision. UNESCO XML coordinates are administrative centroids,
introducing unsystematic noise but not directional bias. Dome enrichment is robust
across raw and context-validated populations and all three simulation nulls. The
observed enrichment is not an artifact of site clustering.
Metrological evidence. No physical surveying instrument calibrated in beru
subdivisions has been recovered; documentary sources attest the beru system
(Hunger & Steele, 2019; Robson, 2008; Powell, 1987). This study therefore treats
the coordinate alignment of Mount Gerizim as a novel empirical observation.

7

Discussion

7.1

Alternative Explanations

(1) Independent convergence. Hemispherical structures are a recurring architectural solution and convergent orientations across cultures are documented (Aveni,
2001, 2003), but independent convergence predicts no consistent longitude specificity.
(2) Geographic concentration. This is the strongest alternative and cannot
be excluded on this corpus alone. Null C (p = 0.0394, *) shows other monuments
at the same longitudes are significantly less enriched; the Monte Carlo unit sweep
(§6.2) confirms 3° as the dominant angular period without assuming any unit.
(3) Random coincidence. Three simulation approaches fail to reproduce the
dome enrichment (permutation p = 0.095; bootstrap p = 0.106). Tests C and D
(§4.4) provide positive evidence for anchor specificity and periodicity structure.

7.2

Candidate Transmission Pathways

The statistical evidence does not identify a mechanism, but the geographic structure
of the signal points toward the Buddhist tradition as the most tractable case. Within
the dome-evolution corpus, the stupa sub-stage achieves an A++ rate of 22.2%
(4/18 sites, OR = 6.81×, p = 0.0061 **), the highest of any keyword-defined subpopulation in the analysis and more than five times the geometric null. Three of the
four UNESCO A++ stupa sites fall in the 95°–110°E corridor, including Pyu Ancient
Cities, Silk Roads Chang’an-Tianshan, Borobudur (c. 800 CE) (Miksic, 1990), and
Lumbini (83°E, Nepal). The Indonesia sub-region (95°–141°E) achieves an A+ rate
of 60.0% (3/5 sites, OR = 16.25×, p = 0.0056 **). By contrast, the four C-tier stupa
sites fall exclusively in the India/Pakistan band: Mahabodhi, Elephanta, Taxila,
and Ajanta (OR = 3.75×, p = 0.0819 † ). The Indian heartland’s C-band enrichment
is consistent with the Buddhist A/C bimodal identified in §4.1: India/Pakistan
sites fall on 0.05-beru harmonics, while Indonesia and Southeast Asian sites align
with the 0.10-beru grid. The Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus (§5.6) reproduces the
same pattern independently, with 29.3% of all 229 stupas reaching Tier-A (binomial
p < 0.001 ***; permutation Z = 1.60, pperm = 0.075 ∼), confirming corpus-wide
harmonic enrichment. At the Java node (107°–112°E) the A-tier rate rises to 75.9%
(OR = 18.07×, p < 0.001 ***), and Tier-A+ is also elevated: 20.4% (OR = 4.72×,
p = 0.002 **). The C-band signal localizes to Myanmar and Cambodia (34.3%,
OR = 4.18×, p < 0.001 ***) rather than the Indian subcontinent (Figure 4). The
anti-correlation between the Indian heartland (C-band enriched, A-depleted) and
the Southeast Asian transmission zone (A+ enriched, C-absent) is consistent with

7.3

Conclusion

The UNESCO Cultural and Mixed corpus is quantized to the 0.1-beru (3°) longitude
grid anchored at Gerizim, with the full-corpus enrichment surviving Bonferroni
correction at k = 3. Longitude bands that contain a precisely aligned monument
are 4.45× more densely populated with other heritage sites than bands without
one (Test 3, p = 0.0003 ***), suggesting that the most important sites and the
broader heritage record share the same longitudinal structure. Among architectural
sub-populations, spherically shaped monuments show the strongest alignment of
any class tested, exceeding what geography alone predicts.
Comparison with the Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus suggests a broader pattern:
within the stupa tradition, the most historically significant monuments are those
positioned at x.18°E harmonic longitudes, and UNESCO inscription appears to
select preferentially from this aligned subset.
Geographically, Mount Gerizim lies between the Mediterranean to the west and
the overland Silk Road to the east, placing it at a natural node of the corridors
through which architectural canons circulated in antiquity (Figure 4).

Replication and methodological transferability
The pipeline is transferable to any externally curated corpus; the Wikidata Q180987
result (§5.6) illustrates one such application.

7 morphological keywords from four roots (stupa/stupas, tholos, dome/domed/domes,

Founding-Origin Classifier (Test 5, Post-Hoc)

Results. The classifier finds 34.1% of A+ sites carry a founding keyword versus
29.3% in the full corpus (OR = 1.28, p = 0.179 ns). This carries no weight in the
primary evidential chain as the classifier was iteratively tuned after data inspection.
The founding-origin classifier (lib/founding filter.py) assigns sites to five
categories: Founding Capital (F), Sacred Origin (S), Founding Monument (M),
Founding Axis (X), and Ancient Continuous Landscape (L). Each category has its
own unambiguous keyword set plus context-gated ambiguous terms; positive and
negative gate patterns are defined per category in keywords.json. The classifier
was iteratively tuned after data inspection to reduce false positives; residual interpretive ambiguity at the boundary between founding-related and general heritage
descriptions is accepted. Statistics are reported in §??; the full site-by-site listing is
