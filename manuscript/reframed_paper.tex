\title{Matching the Statistic to the Hypothesis:\\
Geometric-Null Binomial Proximity Testing\\
for Harmonic Longitude Structure in Monument Distributions}

\author{Seth Tenenbaum\thanks{ORCID:
\href{https://orcid.org/0009-0008-5797-2498}{0009-0008-5797-2498}.
Correspondence: seth@fourthtemple.com}\\
Independent Scholar, USA}

\date{May 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Claims that ancient or sacred monuments align with longitude grids
raise two separable questions: whether a spatial pattern is
statistically detectable, and how such a pattern should be interpreted
archaeologically. Following \citet{silva2020statistical}, this paper
treats the first as a problem of statistical description and the second
as a separate historical, cultural, and geographic task. The
methodological focus is statistical matching. The Rayleigh periodogram,
a standard circular-statistical diagnostic in archaeoastronomy,
measures mean phase concentration at period $T$. A thresholded
repeated-line claim, however, concerns proximity to any harmonic
longitude within a specified tolerance. These are distinct quantities.
Rayleigh $R$ remains useful for detecting circular phase concentration,
but it can dissociate from harmonic proximity: a concentrated
off-harmonic cluster can produce large $R$, while many near-harmonic
sites mixed with off-harmonic mass can yield proximity enrichment with
weak Rayleigh concentration.

The matched statistic for ``sites cluster near harmonics of period
$T$'' is a one-sided binomial count of sites within distance $d$ of
any harmonic, benchmarked against the geometric null $p_0 = 2d/T$.
The framework is demonstrated under a fixed operational design: fixed
period, fixed corridor phase, fixed thresholds, and fixed monument corpora,
including a UNESCO morphology-term keyword corpus defined by words
denoting dome-like or hemispherical architecture. In the UNESCO
dome/stupa corpus ($N = \circTwoSampNDome{}$), a period sweep shows
the Rayleigh--proximity dissociation directly: Rayleigh significance
appears at $2.4\degr$, $3.5\degr$, and $3.6\degr$ without
corresponding proximity enrichment, while the enriched $3.0\degr$
period is not Rayleigh-significant. Applied to three operational
hemispherical-monument corpora at the fixed $3\degr$ period and
${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor phase, the geometric-null binomial tests
produce a set of frame-conditional proximity-enrichment measures.
These are summarized as a cross-corpus consistency check by Fisher's
method ($p \geoFisherAPDisp{}$ at Tier A, used descriptively given
non-independent corpora; see Section~\ref{sec:framework}), demonstrating that
Rayleigh concentration and harmonic proximity are complementary but
non-interchangeable descriptors of spatial structure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Do monuments cluster near specific longitude intervals? The question
recurs in the archaeoastronomical and archaeogeographic literature,
usually motivated by observational coincidences involving sacred sites,
trade corridors, or cosmographic traditions. It has also been
criticized on grounds of investigator-assembled site lists,
insufficient correction for multiple comparisons, and lack of
independent replication
\citep{williamson1983leylines,freeman1976,kendall1974}. These
concerns motivate a stricter separation between statistical detection
and archaeological explanation. Alongside them, this paper raises a
complementary issue of statistical matching: the standard
circular-statistical tool used in this literature measures a
different quantity than the one a repeated-line grid hypothesis
predicts.

The tool in question is the Rayleigh statistic $R$. $R$ measures the
degree to which a set of phases concentrates toward a single preferred
direction on the unit circle --- it is a sensitive test for unimodal
directional concentration. The Rayleigh test is a standard circular-statistical tool in
archaeoastronomical analyses of monument orientations and spatial
distributions \citep{silva2020statistical,magli2013,magli2016}; when applied to a
modulo-$T$ phase mapping of site longitudes, a significant $R$ is a
natural but incomplete indicator that sites cluster near harmonics of
$T$.

This is a useful diagnostic but only a partial answer to the
repeated-line question. A grid-placement hypothesis predicts that
sites fall near longitudes $\lambda_0, \lambda_0 + T, \lambda_0 + 2T,
\ldots$. Under modulo-$T$ phase mapping these harmonics collapse to a
common phase, so an ideal grid-aligned corpus would produce strong
Rayleigh concentration. In real geographically clustered corpora,
however, Rayleigh concentration and thresholded harmonic proximity can
dissociate: a dominant off-harmonic cluster can produce significant
Rayleigh concentration, while a corpus with many near-harmonic sites
plus substantial off-harmonic mass can produce binomial enrichment
without significant Rayleigh $R$. A non-significant Rayleigh result
therefore does not rule out harmonic proximity, and a significant
Rayleigh result does not by itself establish proximity to repeated
longitude lines. The two statistics are complementary; they answer
related but distinct questions.

The directly matched test for ``sites cluster near harmonics'' is
straightforward. Under a uniform longitude model, the expected fraction
of longitudes falling within $\pm d\degr$ of any harmonic of period $T$
is $p_0 = 2d/T$. This is the \emph{geometric null}. In clustered
corpora it remains a useful geometric benchmark, but the resulting
$p$-values are interpreted here as corpus-level proximity enrichment
rather than counts of independent placement decisions.

This paper develops that matched test and uses the monument data as a
worked methodological example. Under a fixed period, fixed corridor phase,
fixed proximity thresholds, and fixed operational corpora, the
geometric-null binomial test estimates whether more sites fall near
harmonic longitudes than expected from the geometric coverage of the
threshold bands. Rayleigh statistics are retained as complementary
circular diagnostics, and the period sweep is used to show when phase
concentration and thresholded harmonic proximity agree or dissociate.
The empirical application identifies a reproducible corpus-level
proximity-enrichment pattern at the fixed $3\degr$ period and
${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor phase. Following
\citet{silva2020statistical}, this
is treated as statistical detection and description of spatial
structure; historical, cultural, or geographic explanation is a
separate interpretive task requiring independent evidence.
Section~\ref{sec:rayleigh_dissociation} develops the theoretical
distinction and demonstrates it empirically via a period sweep.
Section~\ref{sec:framework} formalizes the geometric-null binomial
framework. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the corpora.
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the primary results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
discusses methodological implications, empirical interpretation, and
limitations.

\section{Rayleigh Concentration and Harmonic Proximity:\\
Complementary but Distinct Statistics}
\label{sec:rayleigh_dissociation}

\subsection{Why the Two Statistics Can Dissociate}

Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_N$ be site longitudes and $T$ a
candidate grid period with anchor $\lambda_0$. Define phases by
$\theta_i = 2\pi((\lambda_i - \lambda_0) \bmod T)/T$, and the
Rayleigh statistic by

\[
R = \left| \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} e^{i\theta_i} \right|.
\]

A key property of this transformation is that sites near \emph{any}
harmonic of $T$ --- near $\lambda_0$, near $\lambda_0 + T$, near
$\lambda_0 + 2T$, and so on --- all map to the same phase $\theta \approx 0$
under the modulo-$T$ reduction. Under ideal conditions (all sites
falling exactly on a harmonic), $R$ would equal 1. The modulo-$T$
phase map therefore does not, by itself, produce multiple phase
classes: harmonics collapse to one class, not many.

The Rayleigh test and the binomial proximity test can nevertheless
dissociate because they aggregate cross-site information differently.

\emph{What Rayleigh measures.} $R$ is the length of the mean phasor: it
is sensitive to whether a \emph{single direction} on the unit circle
predominates after averaging over the full corpus. In a geographically
clustered corpus, a single dominant geographic cluster can produce
large $R$ at period $T$ simply because that cluster's longitude maps to
a concentrated phase at that particular $T$ --- regardless of whether
the cluster is harmonically proximate. This is a generic property of
clustered data on the unit circle; it can produce significant $R$ at
periods that carry no harmonic-proximity interpretation.

\emph{What the binomial measures.} The binomial proximity test counts
the number of sites whose deviation from the nearest harmonic falls
within a threshold $d$: $\#\{i : \delta_i \leq d\}$. Let
$r_i = ((\lambda_i - \lambda_0) \bmod T)$ and
$\delta_i = \min(r_i, T - r_i)$.
This is independent of the mean phase direction. Sites far from
harmonics (large $\delta_i$) contribute zero to the binomial count
regardless of whether they push $R$ up or down. Conversely, a corpus
can show significant binomial enrichment even when many off-harmonic
sites keep the mean phasor small.

\emph{Complementarity.} The two tests ask complementary questions.
Rayleigh asks: does one phase direction dominate? The binomial asks:
are more sites within a fixed reporting threshold of a harmonic than the
uniform null predicts? In a real corpus these can agree or disagree,
depending on the balance between harmonically proximate sites,
off-harmonic sites, and the geographic clustering structure of the
corpus. A non-significant Rayleigh result does not rule out harmonic
proximity; a significant Rayleigh result does not establish harmonic
proximity unless the phase concentration is verified to coincide with
a harmonic. Relying on Rayleigh alone to evaluate a repeated-line
hypothesis can therefore produce false positives at cluster-dominated
periods and miss genuine proximity enrichment when near-harmonic sites
coexist with substantial off-harmonic mass.

The dissociation is clear. Rayleigh is significant at
$2.4\degr$, $3.5\degr$, and $3.6\degr$, and non-significant at

The pattern is consistent with the mechanism described above. At
$2.4\degr$, $3.5\degr$, and $3.6\degr$, one geographic cluster
dominates the phase distribution, producing concentrated $R$ without
harmonic proximity enrichment. At $3.0\degr$, many sites fall near the
harmonic class, but substantial off-harmonic mass keeps the mean phasor
small; the binomial test correctly registers the thresholded per-site
proximity counts. Rayleigh and the binomial measure genuinely
different aspects of the data; neither is wrong, but only the binomial
directly answers the question ``do sites fall near harmonics?''

primary period for the cross-corpus analysis in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

shuffle) evaluates whether the number of significant proximity peaks
across \mcNcandidates{} candidate spacings spanning 0.5\degr{} to

under geographic concentration alone. The observed count of
\mcDomeNsigObs{} significant spacings is not significant at

completeness. Of the \mcDomeNsigObs{} significant spacings, three
(6\degr{}, 8\degr{}, 12\degr{}) are multiples of 3\degr{}: a
node-overlap analysis confirms that all of their hits are accounted
for by the 3\degr{} harmonic structure, so their off-node components
are non-significant. The $3\degr$ spacing is the only one whose
significance is not explained by overlap with another spacing's nodes.

\section{The Geometric-Null Binomial Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{The Geometric Null}

For a grid of period $T$ anchored at $\lambda_0$, define the
anchor-relative remainder and nearest-harmonic deviation as

\[
r_i = ((\lambda_i - \lambda_0) \bmod T), \qquad
\delta_i = \min(r_i, T-r_i).
\]

A site falls within tier threshold $d$ if $\delta_i \leq d$. Under a
uniform longitude model, the expected fraction of longitudes satisfying
that inequality is $p_0 = 2d/T$. In clustered corpora this remains a
useful geometric benchmark, but the resulting $p$-values are
interpreted here as corpus-level proximity enrichment rather than
counts of independent placement decisions.

Geographic clusters, serial nominations, and shared cultural traditions
are present in all three corpora. Retaining them preserves the
operational corpus definition and avoids imposing an external,
theory-laden decision about the relative importance of major monument
nodes versus smaller dependent sites. If a major stupa center generated
many associated monuments, that multiplicity is part of the observed
historical-geographic pattern being measured. The binomial test
therefore evaluates proximity enrichment in the corpus as documented,
as a corpus-level measure rather than a count of independent
intentional placement decisions.

The two null models reported in Section~\ref{sec:results}
(Null~A: within-dome bootstrap, reported for localization and
transparency; Null~B: same-footprint UNESCO background, the
discriminating comparison) provide complementary assessments of the UNESCO
signal; they contextualize the p-values without requiring a theory of
why sites are geographically distributed as they are.

\subsection{Tier Classification}

We define three proximity thresholds as round fractions of the $T/2$
half-period ($1.5\degr$), calibrated to bracket the empirical natural
proximity scales of the corpora under study: the OWTRAD Silk Road
network (\naturalNullPctOwtrad{}\%), the UNESCO dome subset (\naturalNullPctDome{}\%), the full UNESCO
Cultural/Mixed corpus (\naturalNullPctFull{}\%), and the Wikidata stupa corpus (\naturalNullPctWiki{}\%)
(figures are A$++$ hit rates in those corpora, i.e.\ the fraction of
sites within $\delta \leq \AppThreshDeg\degr$ of any $3\degr$ harmonic).
Each threshold corresponds to a geometric null rate $p_0 = 2d/T$:

Tier labels are descriptive shorthand; the primary test is always the
binomial.

\subsection{Cross-Corpus Consistency via Fisher Combination}

For each corpus $c$ at tier $d$, a one-sided binomial test yields
$p_c$. Fisher's combination statistic $\chi^2 = -2\sum_c \ln p_c$,
referred to $\chi^2(2C)$ for $C$ corpora, summarizes whether the
directional excess is consistent across all corpora simultaneously.
Fisher's method formally assumes independent p-values. The three
corpora were assembled by distinct processes and their geographic
clusters are substantially non-overlapping, but they share historical
and geographic structure (Eurasian corridor, stupa tradition, public
data sources). The combined p-value is therefore used descriptively: it
summarizes whether the directional excesses across corpora are
consistent in sign, not whether the combined value meets a formal
significance threshold. It should not be read as an independent
significance claim beyond what the individual corpus p-values already
establish. Rayleigh statistics are retained as
complementary diagnostics for comparability with prior literature.

\subsection{Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC)}
\label{sec:npc_definition}

The Normalized Phasor Consensus (NPC) statistic is introduced here as
a purpose-built multi-sample phase-agreement test; we are not aware of
a prior formulation combining unit-normalization of mean phasors with a
permutation null in this context. Standard
multi-sample circular tests (e.g., Watson's common-mean test,
likelihood-ratio tests for concentration equality; \citealt{mardia2000directional})
evaluate whether $C$ samples share a mean direction, but they do so in a
way that is sensitive to each sample's internal concentration: a
tightly clustered corpus dominates the combined statistic. NPC removes
this dependence by normalizing each sample's mean phasor to unit length
before combination, so that phase concordance is assessed independently
of how internally concentrated each corpus is. This makes it appropriate
here, where the corpora have very different geographic spread and
effective $N$, and the question is whether they converge on the same
phase \emph{direction} rather than whether they share the same
concentration structure.

For $C$ corpora, let $\bar{z}_c(T)$ denote the mean phasor of
corpus $c$ at period $T$:
\[
\bar{z}_c(T) = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{j=1}^{N_c} e^{i \cdot 2\pi\left[(\lambda_j - \lambda_0) \bmod T\right] / T},
\]
and let $\hat{z}_c(T) = \bar{z}_c(T)/|\bar{z}_c(T)|$ be its unit
normalization. The Normalized Phasor Consensus statistic is
\[
K = \left|\frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C}\hat{z}_c(T)\right| \in [0, 1].
\]
$K = 1$ if all corpora share the same anchor phase exactly; $K = 0$
if the unit phasors are uniformly distributed. Normalization by
$|\bar{z}_c|$ ensures that phase agreement is assessed independently
of each corpus's internal concentration: a geographically spread
corpus and a tightly clustered one contribute equally to $K$.

Statistical inference uses a phase-randomization permutation null:
for each of \simNperms{} draws, each $\hat{z}_c$ is independently
rotated by a uniform random phase on $[0, 2\pi)$, and $K$ is
recomputed. The permutation $p$-value is the fraction of draws with
$K_\mathrm{null} \geq K_\mathrm{obs}$. The anchor longitude
$\hat{\lambda}$ is taken as the nearest $3\degr$ grid line to the
argument of $\sum_c \hat{z}_c$.

The NPC test is used here to evaluate anchor-phase concordance
across independently assembled monument corpora. It is complementary
to the binomial proximity test: the binomial measures per-site
proximity counts against the geometric null, while NPC measures
whether the mean phase directions of multiple corpora converge to the
same harmonic regardless of the corpora's internal cluster structure.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\textbf{UNESCO dome/stupa subset ($N = \circTwoSampNDome{}$).}
The UNESCO World Heritage XML dataset \citep{unescowhc2024xml} contains
\NclusterTotal{} Cultural and Mixed inscribed sites. Morphological
keywords (stupa, stupas, tholos, dome, domed, domes, spherical) were
matched against site names, XML descriptions, and OUV text, yielding
$N = \circTwoSampNDome{}$ sites. The keyword list was assembled
post-hoc by inspection of the UNESCO XML descriptions to identify
morphological terms denoting hemispherical or dome-like architecture;
it was not pre-specified independently of the data. All results conditional on this
keyword definition should be understood as frame-specified rather than
hypothesis-free. All statistical tests use this keyword-matched
population.

\textbf{Wikidata Q180987 stupa corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$).}
All \textit{instance-of: stupa} (Q180987) items in the Wikidata
knowledge base, extracted via SPARQL query on the
\textit{instance-of: stupa} property. The corpus is
geographically dominated by the Java/Sumatra node
(${\sim}105$--$115\degr$E), reflecting the documented historical density
of stupa construction around Borobudur \citep{miksic1990borobudur}.

\textbf{OSM stupa layer ($N = \osmStupaTotal{}$).}
OpenStreetMap features with tags \texttt{building=stupa},
\texttt{historic=stupa}, or \texttt{ruins=stupa}, assembled by
crowd-sourced tagging. Coverage correlates with mapping effort.
Uneven contributor density is a coverage limitation; the OSM corpus is
therefore treated as corroborating evidence.

All three corpora were tested against the same fixed candidate
frame: period $3\degr$ and the ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor
phase. Because open datasets are publicly available, the frame is
treated uniformly as fixed for the present analysis across all three
corpora. The empirical application therefore functions as a
frame-specified descriptive spatial test.

\textbf{Candidate frame.}
The candidate reference phase is the ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor
phase. For reference, this longitude is labeled by Mount Gerizim at
\GerizimLon\degr{}E, a non-corpus topographic point in the central
Levant. Gerizim is not included in the monument corpora. It functions
only as a convenient geographic label for the corridor-level phase near
Jerusalem and the central Levant. The statistical result is
band-invariant: longitudes within the same \xbandLabel\degr{}E band
produce the same proximity enrichment, and the data cannot distinguish
Gerizim from Jerusalem (\JerusalemLon\degr{}E) or from other co-grid
longitudes within the same proximity tier.

\subsection{Negative Controls, Scope, and Exploratory Observations}

The OWTRAD Silk Road route network ($N = \npcNOwtrad{}$ nodes) does
not independently recover the monument phase in the Normalized Phasor
Consensus test ($K = \npcNegUnOwK{}$, perm-$p = \npcNegUnOwPermP{}$,
ns). Using the \NOwtradEdges{} edge midpoints as the proximity sample
--- one value per route segment, non-deduplicated, the most
conservative representation of the network --- the binomial test is
non-significant at both tiers (A$++$: $p = \pOwtradMidApp{}$,
ns; A$+$: $p = \pOwtradMidAp{}$, ns). The route network therefore
shows no enrichment under either the directional or the per-segment
proximity test when each segment is counted once.

A distinct question --- reported here as an exploratory observation, not a primary result --- is whether \emph{high-centrality} route hubs ---
cities where many independent routes converge --- fall preferentially
near harmonics. Degree-weighting each city by the number of edges it
connects ($N = \NOwtradDegW{}$ node-in-edge occurrences) is the
methodologically appropriate representation for a route \emph{network}
rather than a node list: each directed edge is an independent route
segment with its own terminus, so a city connecting six routes
contributes six endpoint counts, reflecting six distinct traversal
events rather than one. Treating each city as a single unweighted
observation would instead distribute route exposure unevenly ---
systematically under-representing high-centrality nodes that are
structurally more salient in the network. Under degree-weighting the
binomial enrichment is significant (A$++$:
$p \pOwtradDegWApp{}$, $z = \zOwtradDegWApp{}$;
A$+$: $p = \pOwtradDegWAp{}$): the most network-central cities fall
near the $3\degr$ harmonic grid more often than the uniform null
predicts.

The UNESCO Americas sub-corpus ($N = \npcNAmericas{}$) shows an A$+$
proximity rate below the \NullRateAp{} geometric null (observed: \AmericasApRate{}\%),
indicating no proximity enrichment at the ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor
phase in the Western Hemisphere.

A type-matched geographic control uses the North American mound corpus
($N = \moundsN{}$): NHL- and NRHP-registered earthen burial and
platform mounds assembled from the NPS National Register database with
coordinates resolved via Wikidata \citep{nps2024nrhp,wikidata2024}.
These are hemispherical structures morphologically comparable to the
Eurasian corpora but located entirely in the Western Hemisphere,
outside any connection to the ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor
phase.
At the $3\degr$ period and ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor phase
the mounds corpus shows
no proximity enrichment at any tier:
A$++$: \moundsNApp{}/\moundsN{} (\moundsRateApp{}\%, null \NullRateApp{},
$p = \moundsPApp{}$, \moundsPAppSig{});
A$+$: \moundsNAp{}/\moundsN{} (\moundsRateAp{}\%, null \NullRateAp{},
$p = \moundsPAp{}$, \moundsPApSig{});
A: \moundsNA{}/\moundsN{} (\moundsRateA{}\%, null \NullRateA{},
$p = \moundsPA{}$, \moundsPASig{}).
All three tiers are at or below the geometric null.
This supports the interpretation that the Eurasian signal is
geographically specific to the Eurasian corridor rather than a generic
consequence of applying the same harmonic-proximity test to
hemispherical monument corpora.

Null~A (within-dome bootstrap, \simNperms{} trials) resamples from
dome/stupa sites' own longitude distribution and recovers the observed
A$++$ count ($p = \geoNullDomeBootP{}$, ns). Null~A is reported for
completeness only. Because it resamples from the observed dome/stupa
longitude distribution, its expected hit rate is defined by the same
geography that carries the observed signal. It is therefore not a
substantive alternative null model; its only role is to confirm that
the enrichment is localized to dome-site geography rather than
introduced by the full-corpus comparison.

Null~B (same-footprint restricted draw, \simNperms{} trials) asks a
narrower subset-specificity question: among all UNESCO cultural sites
whose longitude falls within ${\pm}5\degr$ of any dome site longitude,
does the dome/stupa subset show greater harmonic proximity than the
broader background? The broader same-footprint UNESCO background
expects only ${\sim}\geoNullDomeRestrictedMeanfive{}$ A$++$ hits versus the
observed \NcircTierApp{} ($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPfive{}$ at the
${\pm}5\degr$ window, surviving Bonferroni correction for $k = 3$
windows tested, $\alpha_\mathrm{adj} = \nullBBonfAlpha{}$), supporting
subset-specific enrichment within that geography relative to the full UNESCO
background.
Nulls~A and B are discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Methods Contribution}

This section draws together the methodological implications of
Sections~\ref{sec:rayleigh_dissociation}--\ref{sec:framework} in light of
the empirical results. The period-sweep dissociation (Table~\ref{tab:rayleigh_vs_binom}) is
the operational payoff of the theoretical distinction developed in
Section~\ref{sec:rayleigh_dissociation}: Rayleigh and the binomial disagree
on which periods carry a proximity signal, and only the binomial gives
the correct answer for the repeated-line question. The geometric-null
framework provides a transparent expected hit fraction under uniform
longitude, the tiered thresholds make the proximity claim operational
at multiple scales, and NPC separates phase concordance from within-corpus
concentration. Together they constitute a complete matched toolkit
for spatial periodicity hypotheses of this form.
This framework is applicable regardless of geographic or cultural
context; the monument corpora used here are a worked example, not the
scope of the method.

\subsection{Interpretation of the Empirical Illustration}

The three-corpus demonstration, under the fixed $3\degr$ period and
${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor phase and conditional on the
post-hoc corpus definitions (see Limitations), finds consistent
directional excess over the geometric null
(Fisher $p \geoFisherAPDisp{}$ at Tier~A, used descriptively).
The UNESCO corpus is
the most geographically distributed result, with A-tier sites spanning the full
Eurasian corridor.

Null~A and Null~B address the two tractable aspects of what the UNESCO
signal means. Null~A (within-dome bootstrap) is reported for
completeness only. Because it resamples from the observed dome-site
positions, its expected hit rate is defined by the same geography that
carries the observed signal --- it is not a substantive alternative null
model. Its sole informational content is confirming the enrichment is
localized to dome-site geography, not an artifact of the comparison
procedure ($p = \geoNullDomeBootP{}$, ns).

Null~B provides the discriminating comparison: among all UNESCO cultural sites
occupying the same longitude footprint, is the dome/stupa subset more
harmonically proximate than the broader background? The answer is yes
($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPfive{}$, surviving Bonferroni correction),
supporting subset-specific enrichment within the geography. The cross-corpus
phase concordance (NPC $K \geq \npcOsmStupaK{}$ across all pairings of
three independently assembled monument datasets with different
geographic centers of mass) provides the strongest evidence that the
signal is not an artifact of any single corpus's geographic
distribution: UNESCO dome sites, Wikidata Q180987 stupas, and OSM
stupa tags converge on the same ${\sim}35.25\degr$E phase independently
of one another, a convergence that is not explained by any single
corpus's clustering structure alone.

\textbf{Corpus overlap and curation.}
The three corpora are assembled by distinct processes: the UNESCO
dome/stupa subset derives from keyword matching against the WHC XML
dataset; Wikidata Q180987 was extracted via SPARQL query on the
\textit{instance-of: stupa} property; OSM was assembled by
crowd-sourced tagging of \texttt{building=stupa},
\texttt{historic=stupa}, and \texttt{ruins=stupa} features via
Overpass API. A best-effort empirical check using GPS proximity
(threshold $< 1$~km) and name-string matching finds 4 near-coincident
site pairs between UNESCO and Wikidata, 0 between UNESCO and OSM, and
1 between Wikidata and OSM (see supplementary overlap audit). Because
all three datasets are publicly available, the candidate frame is
handled as fixed across the empirical demonstration. The Fisher
combination is used as a consistency check across differently assembled
datasets. The geographic clusters of the three corpora are
substantially non-overlapping at the region
level: UNESCO dome sites span the full Eurasian corridor from the
Levant to Java; Wikidata is dominated by the Java/Sumatra node; OSM
coverage reflects crowd-sourced mapping density across South and
Southeast Asia. A single shared geographic cluster does not by itself
describe enrichment in all three simultaneously.

The Wikidata result is substantially carried by the Java/Borobudur
node, which is historically the dominant node of the stupa tradition
\citep{miksic1990borobudur}: the cluster's position on a $3\degr$
harmonic is the signal the geometric-null test is designed to detect.
The OSM result, assembled by a process with no relationship to UNESCO
or Wikidata curation, shows the same directional excess
($p = \geoOsmAP{}$ at Tier~A) and the same corridor phase
($K = \npcOsmStupaK{}$ with Wikidata). Figure~\ref{fig:geo_trail}
shows all three corpora mapped against the $3\degr$ harmonic grid.

The interpretation follows \citet{silva2020statistical}'s distinction
between statistical structure and archaeological explanation.
Significance tests evaluate evidence against a null model;
archaeological explanation is developed from evidence independent of
the orientations themselves, including the wider archaeological record
and socio-historical context. The statistical pattern documented here
is compatible with several mechanisms. These include geographic
channeling by trade routes and river systems following the Eurasian
corridor, cultural diffusion patterns concentrating stupa construction
at historically dominant nodes \citep{sen2014silk}, and selection
effects in the UNESCO inscription process.
The hub-centrality result (Section~\ref{sec:results}, exploratory observations) is consistent
with this: if major route hubs and hemispherical monuments are both
concentrated along the same interior Eurasian corridor, the harmonic
proximity of each would be a shared consequence of corridor geography
rather than an independent signal. Disentangling monument proximity
from hub proximity would require a geographic control outside the
corridor, which remains a target for future comparative work.
Historical interpretations involving deliberate placement would
additionally require evidence for pre-modern continental longitude
determination at sub-degree precision
\citep{hunger2019mul, neugebauer1975history}. The contribution of the
present work is to establish what the directly matched statistical
test reports when applied to three differently assembled corpora.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

All $p$-values in Table~\ref{tab:cross_corpus_geo} are conditional on
the fixed $3\degr$ period, ${\sim}35.25\degr$E Levantine corridor
phase, and the post-hoc keyword definition of the UNESCO dome/stupa
subset. The keyword list was assembled by inspection of the data and
was not pre-specified independently; the UNESCO corpus size and
composition are therefore frame-defined. The full keyword list
(\texttt{dome}, \texttt{domed}, \texttt{domes}, \texttt{spherical},
\texttt{stupa}, \texttt{stupas}, \texttt{tholos}) is documented in the
supplementary keyword audit; this was the only list evaluated. Removing
the least-frequent keyword (\texttt{tholos}, $n = 4$ sites) does not
change the primary result ($N$ drops from \circTwoSampNDome{} to 79,
A$++$ hit count and significance unchanged). Because all three datasets
are publicly available open data and the keyword list was assembled
post-hoc, all results are treated uniformly as frame-specified rather
than hypothesis-free. An anchor-sweep correction is applied to the
UNESCO dome/stupa corpus only, as that is the corpus for which the
corridor phase was identified empirically; the Wikidata and OSM corpora
are tested at the same fixed frame as a cross-corpus consistency check
rather than as independent anchor discoveries. The sweep
places the Levantine corridor in the
top \anchorSweepTopPct\% of \anchorSweepNanchors{} trial anchors
globally ($p = \anchorMaxPermP{}$; this value does not meet $\alpha = 0.01$
and should not be read as strong evidence that the phase is globally
optimal --- it provides a boundary condition on phase-free discovery
claims for that corpus, not a positive significance result).

The Bonferroni correction in Null~B ($k = 3$, $\alpha_\mathrm{adj} =
\nullBBonfAlpha{}$) covers the three window widths (${\pm}2\degr$,
${\pm}5\degr$, ${\pm}10\degr$) tested in that null. No additional
proximity tests were run within Null~B beyond these three windows.

Geographic clustering reduces the effective sample size below the
nominal $N$ in all three corpora. The binomial p-values are therefore
anti-conservative by an unknown degree: they should be read as
corpus-level proximity-enrichment measures under the geometric
benchmark, not as counts of independent site-placement decisions. No
clustering correction is applied because the degree of dependence
cannot be modeled without strong assumptions about the historical
process that generated each cluster. The supplementary geographic audit ({\tt stupa\_geo\_audit.txt})
documents the Wikidata corpus ($N = \npcNStupa{}$) across six named
sub-regions (Heartland 70--110\degr{}E, $n=13$; Indonesia
95--141\degr{}E, $n=5$; Java 105--112\degr{}E, $n=2$;
India/Nepal 70--90\degr{}E, $n=9$; SE Asia ex-Indonesia, $n=2$;
non-heartland, $n=5$); the Java sub-region alone drives the
A$++$ signal (OR $= \infty$, $p = 0.0075$), while the heartland
ex-Java is non-significant ($p = 0.281$). At $5\degr \times 5\degr$
resolution the UNESCO dome corpus spans approximately 18 occupied
cells and the OSM corpus 14; effective $N$ is thus $O(10)$ per
corpus regionally, and all p-values should be read accordingly. The three-corpus NPC concordance --- across datasets with
different geographic centers of mass --- provides the principal
structural argument that the frame-conditional measures are not driven
by any single cluster. A reader who weights the clustering caveat
maximally should treat the Fisher combined values as correlation
summaries only, and rely on the Null~B subset-specificity result
($p = \geoNullDomeRestrictedPfive{}$, Bonferroni-corrected) and the
NPC cross-corpus concordance ($K \geq \npcOsmStupaK{}$ across all
three corpus pairings) as the principal structural evidence.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The central claim of this paper is narrow and transferable: the matched
statistic for a repeated-line longitude hypothesis is a one-sided
binomial count against the geometric null $p_0 = 2d/T$, not the
Rayleigh concentration $R$. The period sweep makes this concrete ---
Rayleigh and the binomial dissociate within the same corpus, at the
same phase, using the same data --- and Table~\ref{tab:rayleigh_vs_binom}
documents the dissociation directly. Neither statistic is wrong; they
answer different questions, and the choice between them should be
dictated by the hypothesis under evaluation.

The framework developed here --- geometric null, tiered proximity
thresholds, Fisher cross-corpus summary, and NPC phase concordance ---
is applicable to any spatial periodicity hypothesis of this form,
independent of the specific corpora or cultural context. The three-corpus
worked example demonstrates the complete workflow under realistic
conditions: post-hoc corpus definition, geographic clustering, and
open-data sources. The empirical results are frame-conditional and
reproducible; future work can apply the same framework to
independently specified corpora, or to the same corpora under
pre-specified frames, to establish whether the Eurasian corridor signal
replicates under stricter conditions.
